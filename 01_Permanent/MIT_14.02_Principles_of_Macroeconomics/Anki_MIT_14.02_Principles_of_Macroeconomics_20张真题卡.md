**Card 1**
**Q**: Define the three equivalent methods for calculating **Gross Domestic 
Product (GDP)** in the National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA).
**A**: 
1.  **Final Goods Approach**: Sum of the market value of all final goods and 
services produced ($\sum P_i Q_i$).
2.  **Value Added Approach**: Sum of value added by every firm (Value of 
production minus value of intermediate inputs).
3.  **Income Approach**: Sum of all incomes generated (Wages + Profits + 
Indirect Taxes). 
**Key Insight**: These methods establish the fundamental identity: **Production 
= Income** [1-3].

**Card 2**
**Q**: Explain the **Paradox of Saving** within the short-run Keynesian 
framework.
**A**: While saving is a microeconomic virtue, a collective attempt to increase 
saving (reducing autonomous consumption $c_0$) reduces aggregate demand. Through
the **multiplier effect**, equilibrium output $Y$ falls so sharply that total 
saving—which must equal investment plus the budget surplus in equilibrium ($I + 
T - G$)—may remain unchanged or even decrease. Attempts to save more can result 
in a recession with no net increase in saving [4, 5].

**Card 3**
**Q**: Why is the modern **LM curve** represented as a horizontal line rather 
than the traditional upward-sloping curve?
**A**: Modern central banks (e.g., the Fed) typically utilize **interest rate 
targeting** rather than money supply aggregates ($M$). They supply whatever 
liquidity is necessary to maintain a specific policy rate ($\bar{i}$), meaning 
the interest rate is independent of the level of income $Y$ in the financial 
market equilibrium [6-8].

**Card 4**
**Q**: State the **Fisher Equation** and its implication for the **Zero Lower 
Bound (ZLB)**.
**A**: $r \approx i - \pi^e$. The real interest rate ($r$) is the nominal rate 
($i$) minus expected inflation ($\pi^e$). At the ZLB ($i=0$), $r = -\pi^e$. If 
the economy experiences **deflation** ($\pi^e < 0$), the real interest rate 
rises even when the nominal rate is zero, further depressing demand and 
potentially leading to a "deflationary trap" [9-11].

**Card 5**
**Q**: Define the **Natural Rate of Unemployment ($u_n$)** and list the factors 
that cause it to increase.
**A**: $u_n$ is the rate at which the real wage chosen in wage setting (WS) is 
consistent with the real wage implied by price setting (PS), occurring when $P =
P^e$. 
*   **Equilibrium Condition**: $F(u_n, z) = 1/(1+m)$.
*   **Increases in $u_n$**: Driven by higher **markups** ($m$) (less market 
competition) or increases in **institutional factors** ($z$) (e.g., more 
generous unemployment benefits) [12-14].

**Card 6**
**Q**: Differentiate between **anchored** and **unanchored** inflation 
expectations in the Phillips Curve.
**A**: 
*   **Anchored**: $\pi^e = \bar{\pi}$ (a constant). The Phillips Curve is a 
stable trade-off between inflation and unemployment levels.
*   **Unanchored**: $\pi^e_t = \pi_{t-1}$ (lagged inflation). The 
**"accelerationist" Phillips Curve** relates the *change* in inflation to the 
unemployment gap: $\pi_t - \pi_{t-1} = -\alpha(u_t - u_n)$. Low unemployment 
here causes inflation to rise indefinitely [15-17].

**Card 7**
**Q**: Define the **Output Gap** and its role in the **IS-LM-PC model**.
**A**: The output gap is $(Y - Y_n)$, where $Y_n$ is potential output. 
*   **Formula**: $\pi - \pi^e = (\alpha/L)(Y - Y_n)$.
*   **Significance**: A positive gap (overheating) creates upward pressure on 
inflation; a negative gap (recession) creates downward pressure. Central banks 
raise rates to close a positive gap and move the economy back to $Y_n$ [18-20].

**Card 8**
**Q**: What is the **"Natural Rate of Interest" ($r_n$ or $r^*$)**?
**A**: The specific real interest rate that yields an equilibrium output level 
exactly equal to potential output ($Y = Y_n$). In the medium run, the central 
bank must adjust the policy rate to $r_n$ to maintain stable inflation [17, 21].

**Card 9**
**Q**: State the **Fundamental Equation of Capital Accumulation** in the Solow 
Growth Model.
**A**: $\Delta k = sf(k) - (\delta + g_n + g_A)k$.
*   $sf(k)$ = Actual investment per effective worker.
*   $(\delta + g_n + g_A)k$ = **"Break-even" investment** required to maintain 
the capital-labor ratio against depreciation ($\delta$), population growth 
($g_n$), and technological progress ($g_A$) [22-24].

**Card 10**
**Q**: In the Solow steady state with technological progress ($g_A$), what are 
the growth rates of: 1) Total Output $Y$, 2) Output per worker $Y/N$, and 3) 
Capital per effective worker $K/AN$?
**A**: 
1.  **Total Output ($Y$)**: $g_A + g_N$.
2.  **Output per worker ($Y/N$)**: $g_A$.
3.  **Capital per effective worker ($K/AN$)**: $0$ (it is constant in the steady
state) [23, 25, 26].

**Card 11**
**Q**: Explain the **Solow Residual** and its usage in growth accounting.
**A**: It is the portion of output growth that cannot be explained by the 
accumulation of capital or labor. It measures the rate of **technological 
progress** ($g_A$): $g_A = g_Y - [\alpha g_N + (1-\alpha)g_K]$, where $\alpha$ 
is the labor share of income. It is often referred to as a "measure of our 
ignorance" [27, 28].

**Card 12**
**Q**: What is **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)** and why is it critical for 
international growth analysis?
**A**: PPP uses a common set of international prices to value the consumption 
baskets of different nations. It corrects for the fact that non-traded goods 
(services) are typically much cheaper in low-income countries, providing a more 
accurate comparison of real standards of living than market exchange rates [29, 
30].

**Card 13**
**Q**: Define the **Real Exchange Rate ($\epsilon$)** and its relationship to 
the trade balance.
**A**: $\epsilon = EP/P^*$, where $E$ is the nominal exchange rate (foreign 
currency/domestic), $P$ is domestic price, and $P^*$ is foreign price. 
*   **Appreciation** ($\epsilon$ up): Domestic goods become more expensive; net 
exports ($NX$) fall.
*   **Depreciation** ($\epsilon$ down): Domestic goods become cheaper; $NX$ 
rises (assuming the **Marshall-Lerner condition** holds) [31-33].

**Card 14**
**Q**: State the **Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)** condition and the effect of
domestic interest rate changes on the current exchange rate.
**A**: $E_t = \frac{1+i_t}{1+i^*_t} E^e_{t+1}$.
Assuming the expected future exchange rate ($E^e$) is fixed, an increase in the 
domestic interest rate ($i$) causes an **immediate appreciation** of the 
domestic currency ($E$ rises). This creates an expected future depreciation to 
equalize the returns on domestic and foreign bonds [34-36].

**Card 15**
**Q**: Why is **Monetary Policy** more effective in the **Mundell-Fleming 
model** (flexible exchange rates) than in a closed economy?
**A**: Monetary policy operates through **two transmission channels**:
1.  **The Investment Channel**: $i$ up $\rightarrow$ Investment down 
$\rightarrow$ $Y$ down.
2.  **The Exchange Rate Channel**: $i$ up $\rightarrow$ Currency appreciation 
($E$ up) $\rightarrow$ Net Exports down $\rightarrow$ $Y$ down. 
The exchange rate movements reinforce the central bank's policy impact on 
aggregate demand [36, 37].

**Card 16**
**Q**: What is the **"Policy Impossible Trinity"** (Trilemma) for an open 
economy?
**A**: A country cannot simultaneously maintain:
1.  A **Fixed Exchange Rate**.
2.  **Free Capital Mobility**.
3.  An **Independent Monetary Policy**.
If a country fixes its rate and allows capital flows, it must set $i = i^*$, 
losing control over domestic output stabilization [38-40].

**Card 17**
**Q**: Define **Expected Present Discounted Value (EPDV)** and its role in 
valuation.
**A**: EPDV is the current value of a sequence of future payments ($Z$), 
adjusted for the time value of money (interest rates $i$) and uncertainty. 
**Formula**: $V_t = Z_t + \frac{E_t[Z_{t+1}]}{1+i_t} + 
\frac{E_t[Z_{t+2}]}{(1+i_t)(1+E_t)} + \dots$ 
It is the mathematical foundation for pricing bonds, stocks, and making 
investment decisions [41, 42].

**Card 18**
**Q**: What is the **Expectations Hypothesis** of the yield curve?
**A**: It posits that long-term interest rates ($i_{nt}$) are determined by the 
average of current and expected future short-term interest rates: $i_{nt} 
\approx \frac{i_{1t} + E_t + \dots + E_t}{n}$. An **inverted yield curve** 
(short rates > long rates) typically indicates that the market expects future 
short-term rates to fall, often due to an impending recession [43, 44].

**Card 19**
**Q**: How does the inclusion of **Expectations** affect the slope of the **IS 
curve**?
**A**: Expectations make the IS curve **steeper**. If consumers and firms 
believe a change in current interest rates is only temporary, it will have a 
negligible effect on their total wealth (EPDV of future income/profits), leading
to a smaller change in current consumption and investment [45, 46].

**Card 20**
**Q**: Describe the conditions for an **Expansionary Fiscal Contraction**.
**A**: A reduction in government spending ($G$) that leads to an *increase* in 
output. This occurs if the consolidation is highly credible and improves 
expectations of the future so significantly (lowering expected future taxes and 
interest rates, and raising expected future income $Y'^e$) that the resulting 
rightward shift of the IS curve (via expectations) outweighs the direct 
contractionary effect of the spending cut [46, 47].
