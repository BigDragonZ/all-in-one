# MIT 14.02 Principles of Macroeconomics: Course Syllabus

This course provides an introduction to macroeconomics, focusing on the "big" 
questions: aggregate output, inflation, unemployment, exchange rates, and 
long-term economic growth [1]. It emphasizes the use of simple models to 
understand the interactions within a system and how they reach equilibrium 
[1-3].

---

## Module 1: Basic Macroeconomic Concepts
**Focus:** Establishing the definitions and measurement tools for tracking the 
health of an economy.

### Key Topics
*   **Macroeconomics vs. Microeconomics:** Understanding aggregate variables 
(inflation, exchange rates) versus individual household or firm decisions [1].
*   **Measuring Aggregate Output (GDP):**
    *   The three equivalent methods: Final Goods approach, Value Added 
approach, and Income approach [4-7].
    *   Distinction between **Nominal GDP** (current prices) and **Real GDP** 
(constant prices) [8, 9].
*   **The Labor Market Indicators:** Definitions of employment, unemployment, 
the labor force, and the participation rate [10, 11].
*   **Inflation:** Measuring price levels through the **GDP Deflator** and the 
**Consumer Price Index (CPI)** [12, 13].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Define the major macroeconomic indicators and explain why "shortcuts" and 
models are necessary in macroeconomics [1, 2].
2.  Calculate GDP using three different methods and explain why they yield the 
same result [6, 7].
3.  Differentiate between nominal and real variables to account for inflation 
[8, 9].

---

## Module 2: The Short Run — The Goods and Financial Markets
**Focus:** How demand determines output in the very short term (roughly one 
year) [14, 15].

### Key Topics
*   **The Goods Market:**
    *   The consumption function: Autonomous consumption ($c_0$) and the 
marginal propensity to consume ($c_1$) [13, 16].
    *   The **Multiplier Effect**: How changes in autonomous spending are 
amplified in the economy [17, 18].
    *   Equilibrium condition: $Y = Z$ (Production = Demand) [19].
*   **The Financial Market:**
    *   Money demand as a function of nominal income and the interest rate [20, 
21].
    *   How central banks target the interest rate through open market 
operations [22, 23].
*   **The IS-LM Model:**
    *   Deriving the **IS curve** (Goods market equilibrium with endogenous 
investment) [24, 25].
    *   The **LM curve** (Modern interpretation: interest rate targeting) [26, 
27].
    *   Analyzing fiscal policy (shifts in IS) and monetary policy (shifts in 
LM) [28-30].
*   **Extended IS-LM:** Incorporating the **Fisher Equation** (nominal vs. real 
interest rates) and the **Risk Premium** ($x$) [31-34].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Explain how the goods market reaches equilibrium and why "saving" can 
sometimes lead to recession (the Paradox of Saving) [18, 35, 36].
2.  Describe the mechanism of monetary policy and how it influences the interest
rate [22, 37].
3.  Use the IS-LM framework to predict the impact of policy changes on output 
and interest rates [28, 38].

---

## Module 3: The Medium Run — The IS-LM-PC Model
**Focus:** How the economy adjusts as prices begin to change and the supply side
becomes a constraint [14, 39].

### Key Topics
*   **Labor Market Dynamics:** Wage setting (WS) based on expected prices and 
unemployment; price setting (PS) based on markups [40-42].
*   **The Natural Rate of Unemployment ($u_n$):** The rate where actual price 
levels match expected price levels [41, 43, 44].
*   **The Phillips Curve (PC):** The relationship between the inflation gap and 
the unemployment gap (or output gap) [45-47].
*   **The IS-LM-PC Model:** Integrating the short-run IS-LM model with the 
Phillips Curve to study dynamic adjustments [48, 49].
*   **Policy and Shocks:**
    *   Anchored vs. Unanchored inflation expectations [50-53].
    *   **Stagflation**: Analyzing supply shocks (e.g., oil price increases) 
[54, 55].
    *   The **Zero Lower Bound (ZLB)** and the Deflationary Trap [55-57].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Derive the Phillips Curve from labor market behaviors and explain the role 
of expectations [45, 58, 59].
2.  Analyze how the economy moves from the short-run equilibrium to the 
medium-run steady state [60, 61].
3.  Evaluate the challenges central banks face when dealing with "unanchored" 
expectations [52, 62].

## Module 4: The Long Run — Economic Growth
**Focus:** The structural factors that determine the standard of living over 
decades [15, 63].

### Key Topics
*   **Facts of Growth:** Measuring wealth across countries using **Purchasing 
Power Parity (PPP)** and the concept of **Convergence** [64-66].
*   **The Solow Growth Model:**
    *   Capital accumulation, depreciation, and the steady state [67-69].
    *   The role of the saving rate and the **Golden Rule** level of capital 
[70, 71].
*   **Technological Progress:** Redefining the model in terms of **effective 
labor** to explain sustained growth in output per person [72-74].
*   **Growth Accounting:** Using the **Solow Residual** to measure technical 
progress [75, 76].
*   **Cross-country Variation:** The role of Human Capital (education) and 
institutional differences [77-79].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Explain why capital accumulation alone cannot sustain growth in the long run
[80, 81].
2.  Use the Solow model to determine the steady-state levels of output and 
capital [69, 82].
3.  Calculate the Solow Residual to identify the sources of economic growth in a
specific country [75, 76].

## Module 5: The Open Economy
**Focus:** Integrating international trade and capital flows into macroeconomic 
analysis [83, 84].

### Key Topics
*   **Openness Concepts:** Openness in goods markets (trade) and financial 
markets (capital flows) [85, 86].
*   **Exchange Rates:** Nominal vs. Real Exchange Rates and the 
**Marshall-Lerner Condition** [87, 88].
*   **Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP):** The relationship between 
domestic/foreign interest rates and expected exchange rate changes [89-91].
*   **The Mundell-Fleming Model:** Extending the IS-LM model for an open economy
with floating exchange rates [92-94].
*   **Exchange Rate Regimes:** Comparing Flexible vs. Fixed exchange rates and 
understanding Speculative Attacks [95-97].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Analyze how domestic policy impacts the trade balance and the exchange rate 
[88, 98, 99].
2.  Explain why a country loses independent monetary policy under a fixed 
exchange rate regime [95, 97].
3.  Use the Mundell-Fleming model to evaluate the effectiveness of fiscal and 
monetary policies in an open economy [93, 97].

---

## Module 6: Expectations and Asset Pricing
**Focus:** How the anticipation of future events influences current economic 
decisions [100, 101].

### Key Topics
*   **Valuation Basics:** **Expected Present Discounted Value (PDV)** and the 
mechanics of discounting [102-104].
*   **Financial Assets:**
    *   **Bond Pricing**: The yield curve, arbitrage, and the expectations 
hypothesis [105-107].
    *   **Stock Pricing**: Fundamental value based on expected dividends [107, 
108].
*   **Expectations and the Macroeconomy:**
    *   The **Permanent Income Theory** of consumption [109, 110].
    *   Investment decisions based on future profitability and the stock market 
[111, 112].
*   **Policy with Expectations:** How forward-looking behavior makes the IS 
curve steeper and changes the impact of fiscal and monetary policy [110, 113, 
114].

### Learning Objectives
1.  Calculate the present value of future cash flows and apply it to bond and 
stock pricing [102, 104, 108].
2.  Explain why "good news" for the economy can sometimes be "bad news" for the 
stock market [115].
3.  Describe how expectations can potentially lead to "expansionary fiscal 
contractions" [110, 114].
