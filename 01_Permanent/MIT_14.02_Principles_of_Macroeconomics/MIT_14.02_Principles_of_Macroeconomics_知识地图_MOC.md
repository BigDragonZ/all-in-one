# MIT 14.02 Principles of Macroeconomics: Map of Content (MOC)

## 1) Core Concept Hierarchy

### I. The Short Run: Demand-Driven Economy
*   **The Goods Market:** Focuses on equilibrium where production ($Y$) equals 
demand ($Z$) [1].
    *   **Components:** Consumption ($C$), Investment ($I$), Government Spending
($G$) [2].
    *   **The Multiplier:** The process by which an initial change in autonomous
spending leads to a larger change in total output [3, 4].
*   **Financial Markets:** Focuses on interest rate ($i$) determination through 
money demand and supply [5].
    *   **Central Banking:** Modern banks target the policy rate rather than 
money aggregates [6, 7].
*   **The IS-LM Model:** Combines goods and financial markets to show how policy
(Fiscal and Monetary) affects output and interest rates [8, 9].

### II. The Medium Run: Supply-Side Constraints
*   **The Labor Market:** Analyzes wage setting ($WS$) and price setting ($PS$) 
behavior [10, 11].
    *   **The Natural Rate:** The unemployment rate ($u_n$) consistent with 
expectations matching reality ($P=P^e$) [11, 12].
*   **The Phillips Curve:** The relationship between the inflation gap and the 
unemployment (or output) gap [13, 14].
*   **The IS-LM-PC Model:** Integrates short-run demand with medium-run supply 
to show how the economy returns to potential output ($Y_n$) via central bank 
intervention [15, 16].

### III. The Long Run: Economic Growth
*   **The Solow Growth Model:** The foundational model for capital accumulation 
and output [17, 18].
    *   **The Steady State:** The point where investment equals depreciation 
plus population and technology growth [19, 20].
*   **Technological Progress:** The only source of sustained per-capita growth 
in the long run [20, 21].
*   **Growth Accounting:** Using the **Solow Residual** to measure unobservable 
technical progress [22].

### IV. The Open Economy
*   **Openness Dimensions:** Goods markets (trade) and financial markets 
(capital flows) [23, 24].
*   **Exchange Rates:** The role of nominal ($E$) and real ($\epsilon$) exchange
rates in trade balance [25, 26].
*   **The Mundell-Fleming Model:** Extending IS-LM with the **Uncovered Interest
Parity (UIP)** condition [27, 28].

### V. Expectations and Asset Pricing
*   **Valuation:** The **Expected Present Discounted Value (EPDV)** as the basis
for all asset pricing [29, 30].
*   **Financial Assets:** Determination of bond prices (yield curve) and stock 
prices (fundamental value) [31, 32].
*   **Expectations-Augmented IS-LM:** How anticipation of future output, taxes, 
and rates shifts current demand [33, 34].

## 2) Formula Reference Sheet

### Aggregate Output and Demand
*   **National Income Identity:** $Y \equiv C + I + G$ (Closed) [35]; $Y \equiv 
C + I + G + X - IM/\epsilon$ (Open) [26]
*   **Consumption Function:** $C = c_0 + c_1(Y - T)$ [36]
*   **The Multiplier:** $\frac{1}{1 - c_1}$ [3]

### Interest Rates and Prices
*   **Fisher Equation:** $r_t \approx i_t - \pi^e_{t+1}$ [37]
*   **Bond Price (One-year):** $P_B = \frac{\$100}{1 + i}$ [38]
*   **Real Exchange Rate:** $\epsilon = \frac{EP}{P^*}$ [25]

### The Medium Run (Labor & Inflation)
*   **Price Setting Equation:** $\frac{W}{P} = \frac{1}{1 + m}$ [11]
*   **Phillips Curve (Modern):** $\pi_t - \pi^e_t = -\alpha(u_t - u_n)$ [13]
*   **Output Gap Version:** $\pi_t - \pi^e_t = \left(\frac{\alpha}{L}\right)(Y_t
- Y_n)$ [14]

### The Solow Growth Model
*   **Capital Accumulation:** $\Delta k = s f(k) - (\delta + g_N + g_A)k$ [20, 
39]
*   **Steady State Condition:** $s f(k^*) = (\delta + g_N + g_A)k^*$ [20, 40]
*   **Solow Residual ($g_A$):** $g_A = g_Y - [\alpha g_N + (1 - \alpha)g_K]$ 
[22]

### Expectations and Open Economy
*   **Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP):** $E_t = \frac{1 + i_t}{1 + i^*_t} 
E^e_{t+1}$ [41]
*   **EPDV of a sequence $Z$:** $V_t = Z_t + \frac{E_t[Z_{t+1}]}{1+i_t} + 
\frac{E_t[Z_{t+2}]}{(1+i_t)(1+E_t)} + \dots$ [30]

## 3) Cross-Module Concept Map

*   **Output ($Y$):** In the **Short Run**, $Y$ is determined by demand ($Z$) 
[42]. In the **Medium Run**, $Y$ returns to its natural level ($Y_n$) dictated 
by the labor market [16]. In the **Long Run**, $Y_n$ itself grows based on 
capital, labor, and technology defined in the Solow model [21].
*   **Interest Rates ($i, r$):** Determined by liquidity preference in the SR 
[43]. In the MR, the central bank moves the real rate toward the **natural rate 
of interest ($r_n$)** to stabilize inflation [16, 44]. In the **Open Economy**, 
rates are linked across borders via the UIP condition [41].
*   **Expectations:** First introduced as "anchored" or "unanchored" inflation 
in the Phillips Curve [45, 46]. They later become the primary driver of **Asset 
Pricing** [47] and shift the **IS curve** in the short run [33, 34].
*   **Policy Mix:** The interaction between fiscal policy (moving IS) and 
monetary policy (moving LM) is explored in closed economies [48], then extended 
to handle **Zero Lower Bound** traps [49], and finally analyzed in **Open 
Economies** where the exchange rate adds a second transmission channel for 
monetary policy [28].

## 4) Key Takeaways Summary

1.  **Macroeconomics as an Art:** Because building a system from millions of 
individuals is too complex, macroeconomics uses "shortcuts" (models) to capture 
the essence of aggregate interactions [50, 51].
2.  **The Multiplier Effect:** Any change in autonomous spending is amplified 
because production creates income, which in turn fuels more consumption [3, 42].
3.  **The Paradox of Saving:** While saving is a microeconomic virtue, if 
everyone tries to save more simultaneously in the short run, it can lead to a 
recession that reduces total income and saving [52, 53].
4.  **Expectations are Decisive:** The effectiveness of policy depends on its 
ability to manage the "heads" of the public. **Forward guidance** and 
credibility allow a central bank to move the economy with minimal actual rate 
changes [34, 54].
5.  **The Zero Lower Bound (ZLB):** When nominal interest rates reach 0%, 
traditional monetary policy fails, and the economy can fall into a 
**deflationary trap** where real rates rise despite the central bank's efforts 
[48, 49].
6.  **Growth is Structural:** In the long run, neither fiscal nor monetary 
policy can create sustained per-capita growth; only **technological progress** 
can improve the standard of living over decades [20, 21, 55].
7.  **The Policy Impossible Trinity:** In an open economy, a country cannot 
simultaneously have a fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility, and an 
independent monetary policy [56, 57].
