# Module 2: The Short Run — Goods and Financial Markets

This module explores the determination of equilibrium output and interest rates 
in the short run (approximately one year), where prices are assumed to be fixed 
and output is entirely determined by aggregate demand [1-4].

---

## 1) Definition and Core Concepts

### The Goods Market (IS Relation)
In the short run, the **equilibrium condition** is that production ($Y$) equals 
the demand for goods ($Z$) [5, 6]. Demand is composed of Consumption ($C$), 
Investment ($I$), and Government Spending ($G$) in a closed economy [7, 8].
*   **The Multiplier Effect:** Any change in autonomous spending (spending 
independent of output) triggers a positive feedback loop where increased 
production creates higher income, leading to further consumption and production 
[9, 10].
*   **Investment/Saving (IS) Identity:** Equilibrium can equivalently be 
expressed as Investment equals Total Saving (Private + Public), denoted as $I = 
S + (T - G)$ [10-12].

### The Financial Market (LM Relation)
Interest rates are determined by the interaction between **money demand** and 
**money supply** [13, 14].
*   **Money Demand ($M^d$):** A function of the level of transactions (proxied 
by nominal income $\$Y$) and the opportunity cost of holding money (the interest
rate $i$) [15, 16].
*   **Modern Central Banking:** Unlike traditional models where the central bank
targets a fixed quantity of money ($M$), modern central banks typically target a
**policy interest rate** ($\bar{i}$) and supply whatever liquidity is necessary 
to maintain that rate [16-19].

### The IS-LM Framework
The **IS-LM model** represents the simultaneous equilibrium of the goods and 
financial markets [12, 16]. The intersection of the downward-sloping IS curve 
and the (modern) horizontal LM curve determines the unique combination of output
($Y$) and interest rate ($i$) for the economy in the short run [20, 21].

## 2) Mathematical Derivations and Formulas

### Equilibrium in the Goods Market
Defining consumption as a linear function of disposable income $Y_D = Y - T$:
$$C = c_0 + c_1(Y - T)$$
Where $c_0$ is autonomous consumption and $c_1$ is the marginal propensity to 
consume ($0 < c_1 < 1$) [3, 22].
Substituting into the equilibrium condition $Y = Z$:
$$Y =  + I + G$$
Solving for $Y$ yields the equilibrium output:
$$Y = \frac{1}{1 - c_1} $$
Here, $\frac{1}{1 - c_1}$ is the **multiplier** and $$ is **autonomous 
spending** [6, 9, 10].

### The Extended IS Curve
Investment is endogenous, depending on output ($Y$) and the real borrowing cost 
($r + x$):
$$Y = C(Y - T) + I(Y, i - \pi^e + x) + G$$
*   **Fisher Equation:** The real interest rate $r$ is approximated by the 
nominal rate $i$ minus expected inflation $\pi^e$: $r \approx i - \pi^e$ [23, 
24].
*   **Risk Premium ($x$):** Represents the credit spread over the risk-free 
rate, determined by default probability and investor risk aversion [24, 25].

### Bond Pricing and Interest Rates
For a one-year zero-coupon bond with face value $\$100$:
$$P_B = \frac{\$100}{1 + i} \implies i = \frac{\$100 - P_B}{P_B}$$
This establishes the **inverse relationship** between bond prices and interest 
rates used in open market operations [16, 26].

## 3) Real-world Case Studies

### The COVID-19 "Policy Mix" (2020–2021)
The pandemic recession saw a combined application of massive **expansionary 
fiscal policy** (IS shifts right via $G$ and $T$ transfers) and **aggressive 
monetary policy** (LM shifts down to the Zero Lower Bound) [12, 27, 28]. This 
"All In" approach was designed to prevent a deep depression but eventually 
contributed to the economy "overheating" [29-31].

### The Zero Lower Bound (ZLB) and Liquidity Trap
When the nominal interest rate reaches 0%, monetary policy loses its 
conventional power because people become indifferent between holding money and 
bonds [32, 33]. This occurred in the **US** during the Great Recession and 
COVID-19, and has been a persistent feature of the **Japanese** economy for 
decades [27, 32, 34].

### The Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) Shock (2023)
The SVB collapse is modeled as a sudden increase in the **risk premium ($x$)**. 
This shock increased the borrowing costs for firms despite the risk-free rate 
staying constant, effectively shifting the IS curve to the left and creating 
contractionary pressure on output [35-37].

## 4) Critical Analysis and Limitations

### The Paradox of Saving
In the short run, if consumers attempt to save more (lowering $c_0$), aggregate 
demand falls. Through the multiplier, output decreases so much that total saving
may actually remain unchanged or even decrease. This highlights that individual 
"virtue" can lead to collective recession [10, 38].

### Policy Lags
While the model suggests instantaneous adjustment, real-world monetary policy 
operates with **"long and variable lags"**—estimated at roughly six quarters to 
reach full impact [30, 34].

### Fixed Price Assumption
The primary limitation of this module is the assumption that prices ($P$) do not
respond to demand [2, 39]. This makes the model unsuitable for analyzing 
inflation or supply-side constraints, which are the focus of subsequent modules 
[2, 4, 40].
