### Chapter 7: Specialized Multiples and Asset-Based Valuation — Deep-Dive Notes

#### 1. Definition and Core Concepts
This chapter focuses on valuation techniques that move beyond earnings to look 
at **accounting book values**, **top-line revenues**, and the **sum of 
individual assets**.

*   **Book Value Multiples:** These compare the market's assessment of a firm to
the accountant's estimate of its worth. The **Price-to-Book (P/B)** ratio is an 
equity-based multiple, while **EV-to-Invested Capital** is a firm-wide measure 
[1, 2].
*   **Revenue Multiples:** Often used for young firms or those with volatile 
earnings. While **Price-to-Sales (P/S)** is common, it is technically 
inconsistent because the numerator is equity-only while the denominator (sales) 
belongs to all capital providers. **Enterprise Value-to-Sales (EV/Sales)** is 
the more robust, consistent alternative [3, 4].
*   **Asset-Based Valuation:** This approach values a firm by summing its 
constituent parts rather than looking at its aggregate cash flows. It is 
primarily used in three scenarios:
    *   **Liquidation Valuation:** Assessing what assets would fetch in a sale 
today [5, 6].
    *   **Fair Value Accounting:** Valuing assets for financial reporting 
purposes [7, 8].
    *   **Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP):** Valuing a conglomerate by independently 
evaluating each business segment [5, 9].

#### 2. Mathematical Derivations and Formulas
Every specialized multiple is a mathematical "shorthand" for an intrinsic 
valuation model, with specific **companion variables** that drive its value.

**The Fundamental P/B Ratio Derivation:**
Derived from a stable growth dividend discount model, the P/B ratio is a 
function of the spread between the return on equity and the cost of equity:
$$\frac{P_0}{BV_0} = \frac{ROE \times \text{Payout Ratio} \times (1 + g_n)}{k_e 
- g_n}$$
A more intuitive version isolates the "excess return":
$$\frac{P_0}{BV_0} = 1 + \frac{ROE - k_e}{k_e - g_n}$$
This demonstrates that a firm should trade at its book value ($P/B = 1$) only if
its **Return on Equity (ROE)** equals its **Cost of Equity ($k_e$)** [10].

**The Fundamental EV/Sales Ratio Derivation:**
Revenue multiples are driven primarily by profit margins. For a stable firm:
$$\frac{EV}{\text{Sales}} = \text{After-tax Operating Margin} \times \frac{(1 - 
\text{Reinvestment Rate}) \times (1 + g_n)}{WACC - g_n}$$
This proves that a high revenue multiple is only justified if the firm can 
maintain high margins [11, 12].

**Brand Name Valuation:**
The value of a brand can be isolated by comparing a branded firm’s value to its 
value if it had the margins of a generic competitor:
$$\text{Value}_{\text{Brand}} = \text{Value}_{\text{Branded Margin}} - 
\text{Value}_{\text{Generic Margin}}$$ [13].

#### 3. Real-world Case Studies
*   **Japanese P/B Ratios (2013):** In 2013, the median P/B for Japanese firms 
was only **0.67**, meaning they traded at a 33% discount to book value. This was
not necessarily a "bargain"; it reflected the fact that typical Japanese firms 
had an **ROE lower than their cost of equity**, thereby destroying value [2].
*   **The Coca-Cola Brand:** A brand valuation of Coca-Cola replaced its 18.56% 
operating margin with the 5.23% margin of **Cott** (a generic soda producer). 
This adjustment revealed that **$64 billion** of Coca-Cola’s $80 billion value 
was derived specifically from its brand-driven pricing power [12, 14].
*   **United Technologies SOTP:** To value this diversified conglomerate, the 
firm was broken into six segments (e.g., Otis, Pratt & Whitney). Each was valued
using industry-specific **EV/EBITDA multiples** and intrinsic DCF models. The 
final valuation subtracted **$42 billion** in unallocated corporate expenses to 
reach a firm value of $75 billion—significantly higher than its market price at 
the time [9, 15].

#### 4. Critical Analysis and Limitations
*   **The Margin Trap:** High revenue multiples are dangerous because they are 
highly sensitive to margin contractions. If a firm’s margin drops, its EV/Sales 
multiple will collapse **disproportionately**, leading to a double-whammy on 
price [11, 13].
*   **Accounting Dependency:** Book value is an accounting construct. If a firm 
has high **intangible assets** (like R&D) that were expensed rather than 
capitalized, the book value will be understated and the P/B ratio will be 
artificially inflated [1, 16].
*   **Asset Separability:** SOTP valuation only works if assets are 
**separable** and have independent cash flows. If different business units have 
high **synergies** (e.g., Disney’s movies and theme parks), valuing them in 
pieces will miss the value created by their interaction [7].
*   **Liquidation vs. Going Concern:** Liquidation value is almost always lower 
than going concern value because it ignores growth potential and often requires 
a **liquidation discount** for a fast sale [6].

#### 5. Cross-chapter Connections
*   **Connection to Cost of Capital (Chapter 2):** The P/B and EV/Sales formulas
both contain $k_e$ or $WACC$ in the denominator, meaning every multiple is 
implicitly "at the mercy" of the interest rate environment [10, 12].
*   **Connection to Cash Flows (Chapter 3):** To accurately use P/B or SOTP, one
must often correct accounting errors, such as **capitalizing R&D or operating 
leases**, which changes the "Invested Capital" and "Book Value" denominators [1,
17, 18].
*   **Connection to Loose Ends (Chapter 8):** When performing an SOTP valuation,
one must remember to subtract **unallocated corporate expenses** and add 
**cash** to the sum of the operating segments to move from "sum of assets" to 
"equity value" [15, 19].
*   **Connection to Real Options (Chapter 9):** For asset-based firms like oil 
companies, the "assets" are often **delay options** on undeveloped reserves. In 
these cases, a simple SOTP might understate value by ignoring the 
volatility-driven value of those options [20, 21].
