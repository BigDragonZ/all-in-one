# **Course Syllabus: Valuation – Theory and Practice**

## **Chapter 1: Philosophy and Foundations of Valuation**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The "Bermuda Triangle" of valuation: Bias, Uncertainty, and Complexity 
[1, 2].
    *   Intrinsic vs. Relative vs. Real Option valuation approaches [3, 4].
    *   The Financial Asset Balance Sheet: Assets-in-place vs. Growth assets 
[5].
    *   The difference between valuing equity and valuing the whole firm [6, 7].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Understand how personal and institutional bias impacts value [8].
    *   Identify the core pillars of value: cash flow, growth, risk, and 
terminal value [9].
    *   Choose between equity and firm valuation models based on internal 
consistency [7, 10].

## **Chapter 2: Estimating the Discount Rate (Cost of Capital)**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   Defining and estimating the Risk-Free Rate across different currencies 
[11, 12].
    *   Equity Risk Premiums: Historical data vs. Implied forward-looking 
estimates [13, 14].
    *   Relative Risk: The limitations of regression Betas and the power of 
Bottom-up Betas [15-17].
    *   Cost of Debt and the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) [18, 19].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Calculate a synthetic risk-free rate for emerging market currencies 
[20].
    *   Mitigate "noise" in risk measures by using industry-average unlevered 
betas [21].
    *   Estimate a company’s default spread using interest coverage ratios [22].

## **Chapter 3: Estimating Cash Flows and Growth**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The path from accounting earnings to free cash flows [23, 24].
    *   Correcting accounting "misclassifications": Capitalizing R&D and 
Operating Leases [25, 26].
    *   Fundamental growth: Reinvestment Rate and Return on Invested Capital 
(ROIC) [27, 28].
    *   Forecasting growth for young, money-losing firms using top-down revenue 
models [29, 30].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Normalize earnings for cyclical and commodity firms [31].
    *   Reconstruct financial statements to treat R&D as a capital investment 
rather than an expense [32].
    *   Distinguish between "good" growth (above cost of capital) and 
value-destroying growth [33].

## **Chapter 4: Terminal Value and model Closure**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The three approaches to closure: Liquidation, Growing Perpetuity, and 
Multiples [34-36].
    *   The "Cap" on terminal growth: Why it cannot exceed the growth of the 
economy [37, 38].
    *   Stable growth characteristics: Adjusting beta, reinvestment, and risk 
[39, 40].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Recognize why using a multiple to estimate terminal value is actually 
relative valuation in "drag" [41, 42].
    *   Ensure the model’s terminal phase reflects a mature, sustainable 
business [43].

## **Chapter 5: Value Enhancement and Acquisition Dynamics**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The four levers of value enhancement [44, 45].
    *   Valuing Synergy: Operating vs. Financial synergies [46, 47].
    *   The Value of Control: Status Quo vs. Optimal management [48, 49].
    *   The Complexity Discount in valuation [50, 51].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Quantify how changes in debt ratios and reinvestment rates impact firm 
value [45, 52].
    *   Assess the probability of management change to value "Control" [48].
    *   Differentiate between price enhancement (market optics) and true value 
enhancement [53].

## **Chapter 6: Relative Valuation: First Principles and Earnings Multiples**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The 4-Step Process: Define, Describe, Analyze, and Apply [54, 55].
    *   Consistency in multiples: Numerator vs. Denominator matching [56, 57].
    *   Deep dive into PE Ratios and EV/EBITDA [58, 59].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Identify "mismatches" where a stock is cheap because of fundamentals 
rather than market error [60].
    *   Use multiple regressions to control for differences in growth and risk 
among peers [61, 62].

## **Chapter 7: Specialized Multiples and Asset-Based Valuation**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   Book Value Multiples and the critical link to ROE [63, 64].
    *   Revenue Multiples and the impact of profit margins [65, 66].
    *   Asset-Based Valuation: Liquidation, Fair Value accounting, and 
Sum-of-the-parts [67-69].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Isolate the value of a brand name by comparing branded vs. generic 
profit margins [70, 71].
    *   Value a conglomerate by performing separate valuations for its distinct 
business segments [72, 73].

## **Chapter 8: Loose Ends: Distress, Dilution, and Illiquidity**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   Bridging Firm Value to Equity Value: Cash, Cross-holdings, and Other 
assets [74, 75].
    *   Accounting for truncation risk (Financial Distress) [76, 77].
    *   Dealing with Illiquidity and the cost of employee stock options 
(Dilution) [78-80].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Adjust valuation for non-diversified owners in private company 
transactions [81, 82].
    *   Incorporate the probability of bankruptcy into a DCF model [77, 80].

## **Chapter 9: Real Options in Valuation**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   The intuition of Real Options: Learning and Adaptive Behavior [83, 84].
    *   The Option to Delay: Valuing patents and undeveloped natural resources 
[85-87].
    *   The Options to Expand and Abandon [88-90].
    *   Equity in distressed firms as a call option on assets [91, 92].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Understand why higher volatility increases value in an option framework 
[93, 94].
    *   Determine the "cost of delay" when choosing when to exercise a business 
option [95, 96].

## **Chapter 10: Closing Thoughts and Narrative**
*   **Key Topics:**
    *   Integrating Story with Numbers [97].
    *   Selecting the right model for the right asset [98, 99].
    *   The role of luck and the "Rescue of the Lemmings" [100].
*   **Learning Objectives:** 
    *   Defend a valuation by articulating the underlying narrative behind the 
assumptions [1, 97].
    *   Maintain the discipline of valuation to slow down emotional investment 
decisions [8].
