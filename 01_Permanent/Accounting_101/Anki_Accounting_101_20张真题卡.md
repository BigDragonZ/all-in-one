### Card 1
**Q:** Define the three-fold role of accounting from an external perspective and
the fundamental identity that governs the balance sheet.
**A:** Accounting serves to **verify** transactions occurred, **record** them 
consistently, and **report** them in a comprehensible format [1]. The 
fundamental identity is:
$$Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity$$
In this framework, the accountant is viewed as a **historian**, not a forecaster
[1, 2].

### Card 2
**Q:** What three core economic questions must financial statements answer for 
any business?
**A:** 
1. **Asset Stewardship:** What do you own, when was it acquired, and what is its
accounting value? [2, 3]
2. **Obligation Profile:** What do you owe to creditors? [2, 4]
3. **Operating Performance:** How much did you earn (profit/cash flow) over the 
last period? [2, 5]

### Card 3
**Q:** Distinguish between **Accrual** and **Cash** accounting and explain the 
impact on revenue recognition.
**A:** Cash accounting records transactions when currency changes hands 
("checkbook accounting") [6, 7]. Accrual accounting records transactions when 
they happen, regardless of payment [7]. This requires the **matching 
principle**, where expenses associated with revenue are recorded in the same 
period [7].

### Card 4
**Q:** Outline the hierarchical structure of the **Income Statement** from 
top-line revenue to bottom-line residual earnings.
**A:** 
1. **Gross Profit** = Revenues - COGS [5, 8]
2. **Operating Income (EBIT)** = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D) -
Depreciation [5, 8]
3. **Taxable Income** = EBIT - Interest Expenses + Non-operating Income [5, 8]
4. **Net Income** = Taxable Income - Taxes [5, 8]

### Card 5
**Q:** Define the three distinct types of **Depreciation** and which one appears
in financial statements.
**A:** 
1. **Economic:** The true loss in usefulness as an asset ages (not in 
financials) [9].
2. **Accounting:** Rule-driven (e.g., straight-line) write-offs found in 
financial statements [9].
3. **Tax:** Accelerated write-offs designed specifically to minimize tax 
payments [9].

### Card 6
**Q:** How do **Minority** and **Majority** holdings in other companies differ 
in their accounting treatment?
**A:** 
* **Minority (<50%):** Uses the **Equity Method**; a percentage of the other 
firm's net income is recorded below the operating line [10, 11].
* **Majority (>50%):** Requires **Consolidation**; 100% of the subsidiary's 
revenues/EBIT are included, and the non-owned portion is recorded as 
"non-controlling interest" [10, 12].

### Card 7
**Q:** Describe the **"Mixed-Attribute"** model of the Balance Sheet and its 
primary limitation.
**A:** The balance sheet is "neither fish nor fowl" because it records some 
items at **historical cost** (e.g., land, equipment) and others at **fair 
value** (e.g., marketable securities, leases) [13, 14]. This hodgepodge makes it
difficult to measure true invested capital accurately [14].

### Card 8
**Q:** Define **Goodwill** and explain why it is considered a "fiction" or 
"plug" variable.
**A:** Goodwill is the difference between the price paid for an acquisition and 
its accounting book value [15]. It is not a tangible asset but an accounting 
adjustment to balance the sheet; it only appears after an acquisition [15]. 
Since the 1990s, it is no longer amortized but subject to **impairment tests** 
[15].

### Card 9
**Q:** Explain the phenomenon of **Negative Shareholders' Equity** and why it 
does not always imply insolvency.
**A:** Negative equity can result from accumulated losses in young firms (e.g., 
Peloton) or aggressive **stock buybacks** in mature firms [16, 17]. Because 
buybacks are recorded at market price, they can deplete the much smaller book 
value of equity [16, 18].

### Card 10
**Q:** Why is the **Statement of Cash Flows** considered the most "honest" 
financial statement?
**A:** Because accounting rules cannot easily manipulate the actual inflow and 
outflow of currency [19, 20]. It reconciles the change in cash balance across 
three activities: **Operating**, **Investing**, and **Financing** [19, 21].

### Card 11
**Q:** Provide the formula for **Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)** using the 
indirect method and define the role of working capital.
**A:** 
$$CFO = Net\ Income + Non\text{-}cash\ Charges (Depreciation) - \Delta 
Non\text{-}cash\ Working\ Capital$$
Working capital is the "residue" of accrual accounting; an increase in current 
assets (like inventory) is a cash drain, while an increase in current 
liabilities (like accounts payable) is a cash inflow [22, 23].

### Card 12
**Q:** Define **Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)** and what it represents for a 
firm.
**A:** FCFE is the "potential dividend" a company can afford after debt and 
reinvestment [24]. 
$$FCFE = CFO - (CapEx - Divestitures + Cash\ Acquisitions) + (New\ Debt - Debt\ 
Repayment)$$

### Card 13
**Q:** Distinguish between **Marginal**, **Effective**, and **Cash** tax rates.
**A:** 
* **Marginal:** Statutory rate defined by tax law [25].
* **Effective:** Accrual-based taxes on the Income Statement divided by taxable 
income [25, 26].
* **Cash:** The actual amount of checks written to the government, found by 
adjusting the accrual tax for deferred tax movements [25, 27].

### Card 14
**Q:** Why is **Stock-Based Compensation (SBC)** a controversial "add-back" in 
cash flow statements?
**A:** While not a cash expense, SBC is a **"grant in kind"** that dilutes 
existing shareholders [28, 29]. Adding it back (like depreciation) creates an 
illusion of cash health while ignoring the real transfer of value/ownership to 
employees [29, 30].

### Card 15
**Q:** What is the 2019 accounting shift regarding **Operating Leases**, and how
are they capitalized?
**A:** All lease commitments are now treated as **debt** [28, 31]. The debt 
value is the **Present Value (PV)** of future lease commitments discounted at 
the firm's pre-tax cost of borrowing [31, 32].

### Card 16
**Q:** Why is treating **R&D** as an operating expense considered an accounting 
failure, and how is it corrected?
**A:** R&D creates benefits over multiple years and is thus a **capital 
expenditure** [31, 33]. To correct it, an analyst must: 
1. Determine an amortizable life. 
2. Collect historical R&D data. 
3. Capitalize the unamortized portion as an asset and replace the current R&D 
expense with the calculated amortization [34, 35].

### Card 17
**Q:** Define the **Consistency Principle** in ratio analysis and provide the 
formula for **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)**.
**A:** The numerator (return) must match the perspective of the denominator 
(capital source) [36]. 
$$ROIC = \frac{Operating\ Income \times (1 - t)}{Total\ Debt + Equity - Cash}$$
*Note: Cash is subtracted because it is a non-operating asset* [36, 37].

### Card 18
**Q:** What is the **"Dark Side of Growth,"** and which ratio measures it?
**A:** Growth is only value-creative if its benefits exceed its cost. The 
**Sales to Invested Capital** ratio measures this by showing how much revenue is
generated per dollar of reinvested capital [38, 39].

### Card 19
**Q:** Explain the difference between **Market** and **Book** leverage ratios 
using the Netflix example.
**A:** Book leverage uses historical accounting values ($7.6B for Netflix 
equity), while market leverage uses current stock prices ($216B for Netflix 
equity) [39]. Growth firms often have high book leverage (>100%) but low market 
leverage (7%) because the market recognizes value the accountant has not yet 
recorded [39].

### Card 20
**Q:** Define **Interest Coverage Ratio** and what a "high" ratio signifies.
**A:** 
$$Interest\ Coverage = \frac{Operating\ Income}{Interest\ Expense}$$
A higher ratio (e.g., 5 or 10) indicates a larger "buffer," meaning the firm is 
at lower risk of failing to meet its contractual debt obligations [40, 41].
