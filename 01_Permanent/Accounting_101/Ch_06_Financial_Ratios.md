# Chapter 6: Financial Ratios — Deep-Dive Notes

## 1. Definition and Core Concepts
Financial ratios serve as the primary tool for **standardizing** accounting 
data, enabling the comparison of firms across different scales, industries, and 
time periods [1]. Without standardization, absolute values lead to 
"apples-to-oranges" comparisons, as a large firm's absolute profit will 
naturally dwarf that of a smaller competitor [1]. 

The course adopts a **pragmatic, "less is more" philosophy** toward ratio 
analysis [2]. Rather than drowning in hundreds of metrics, the analyst should 
focus on a select group of ratios that build a forward-looking story about a 
company's strategic position, efficiency, and risk [2]. These ratios are broadly
categorized into five clusters: **Profitability (Margins)**, **Accounting 
Returns**, **Efficiency (Turnover)**, **Financial Leverage**, and 
**Liquidity/Credit Risk** [3, 4].

## 2. Mathematical Derivations and Formulas
The integrity of ratio analysis depends on the **consistency principle**: the 
numerator (the return or result) must match the perspective of the denominator 
(the capital source or scale) [5].

### Profitability Ratios (Denominator: Revenues)
These measure the percentage of each dollar of sales that survives various 
levels of expense [3].
*   **Gross Margin:** 
    $$Gross\ Margin = \frac{Revenues - COGS}{Revenues}$$
*   **Operating Margin:** 
    $$Operating\ Margin = \frac{Operating\ Income\ (EBIT)}{Revenues}$$
*   **Net Margin (Equity Perspective):** 
    $$Net\ Margin = \frac{Net\ Income}{Revenues}$$
*   **EBITDA Margin (Cash Proxy):** 
    $$EBITDA\ Margin = \frac{EBITDA}{Revenues}$$

### Accounting Returns (Numerator-Denominator Matching)
*   **Return on Equity (ROE):** Matches equity earnings to equity capital [5].
    $$ROE = \frac{Net\ Income}{Shareholders'\ Equity}$$
*   **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC):** Matches total operating earnings to 
total capital deployed [5].
    $$ROIC = \frac{Operating\ Income \times (1 - t)}{Invested\ Capital}$$
    *Where $Invested\ Capital = Total\ Debt + Equity - Cash$ [5, 6].*

### Efficiency and The "Dark Side of Growth"
*   **Sales to Invested Capital:** Measures how much revenue is generated for 
every dollar of capital reinvested [7].
    $$Sales\ to\ Invested\ Capital = \frac{Revenues}{Invested\ Capital}$$

### Leverage and Coverage
*   **Debt to Capital Ratio (Market or Book):**
    $$Debt\ to\ Capital = \frac{Debt}{Debt + Equity}$$
*   **Interest Coverage Ratio:** Measures the "buffer" a firm has to meet 
interest obligations [4].
    $$Interest\ Coverage = \frac{Operating\ Income}{Interest\ Expense}$$

## 3. Real-world Case Studies
Ratio profiles evolve predictably across the **Corporate Life Cycle** [8].

*   **Peloton (The Young Firm):** Exhibits a positive gross margin but deeply 
negative operating and net margins due to the burden of fixed costs on a small 
revenue base [6, 9]. Its ROE is often extreme (e.g., -79%) because small equity 
bases amplify losses [6].
*   **Netflix (The Growth Firm):** Demonstrates high efficiency with a Sales to 
Invested Capital ratio of 1.71, meaning it requires less reinvestment to grow 
than peers [10]. Its **Market Debt-to-Equity (7%)** is drastically lower than 
its **Book Debt-to-Equity (>100%)**, as the market prices in massive growth 
potential not captured on the balance sheet [11].
*   **Coca-Cola (The Mature Firm):** Features high margins (driven by low COGS) 
and stable accounting returns, defending its ROIC through brand-driven 
competitive advantages [6, 9].
*   **Toyota (The Mature Manufacturing Firm):** Operates in a capital-intensive 
industry with lower margins and unit-digit returns compared to technology or 
brand-heavy firms [6]. It maintains the highest leverage among the sample firms,
reflected in its Debt to EBITDA and coverage ratios [11, 12].

## 4. Critical Analysis and Limitations
*   **The "Dark Side" of Growth:** High growth is not inherently value-creative;
it must be weighed against the **reinvestment cost** measured by efficiency 
ratios [11]. If a firm must reinvest more than it earns in returns, growth 
destroys value [11].
*   **Accounting Bias in Returns:** Ratios like ROE and ROIC are "compositions 
of economic reality and accounting choice" [10]. For example, **Dr. Reddy's** 
high ROIC is partially an illusion caused by expensing R&D, which keeps its 
largest asset off the denominator (Invested Capital), artificially inflating the
return [10].
*   **Market vs. Book Divergence:** Book-value-based leverage ratios are often 
misleading for growth firms. For Netflix, the market value of equity is roughly 
\$216 billion compared to a book value of \$7.6 billion; the analyst should 
generally trust the market's assessment of equity value over the accountant's 
historical record [11].
*   **Normalization Requirements:** For firms with volatile earnings, a single 
year's ratio (e.g., 2019) may be "dangerous" [2]. Analysts should **normalize** 
by using average revenues or margins over a cycle to find a representative 
baseline [2].

## 5. Cross-chapter Connections
*   **To Chapter 2 & 3 (Financial Statements):** Ratios provide the connective 
tissue between the temporal performance of the Income Statement and the 
structural snapshot of the Balance Sheet [5].
*   **To Chapter 5 (Accounting Inconsistencies):** To calculate an accurate 
ROIC, the analyst must first perform the **capitalization of R&D and Leases** 
discussed in Chapter 5 [5]. Without these adjustments, the Invested Capital 
(denominator) and Operating Income (numerator) are fundamentally misstated [5, 
10].
*   **To Valuation:** Ratios are not the "end" of analysis but the "raw 
material" for building a future-oriented story [2]. Specifically, the **Sales to
Invested Capital** ratio is a primary input for forecasting the reinvestment 
needed to sustain future growth in a valuation model [7].
