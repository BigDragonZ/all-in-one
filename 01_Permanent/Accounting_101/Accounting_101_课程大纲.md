# Course Syllabus: Accounting 101

## Course Overview
This course provides a pragmatic overview of accounting, designed specifically 
for those interested in corporate finance and valuation [1]. Rather than 
focusing on complex rules or debits and credits, the course centers on how to 
read, interpret, and adjust financial statements to reflect economic reality [1,
2].

---

## Chapter 1: Introduction and the Financial Statement Overview
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Understand the role of accounting from an external perspective [3].
*   Identify the three core questions every financial statement should answer 
[4].
*   Distinguish between the primary financial statements and understand their 
interconnections [4, 5].

**Key Topics:**
*   **The Role of Accounting:** Verifying, recording, and reporting transactions
consistently [3].
*   **Three Core Questions:** 
    1.  What do you own? (Assets) [4].
    2.  What do you owe? (Liabilities) [4].
    3.  How much did you make last year? (Income/Cash Flow) [4].
*   **The Three Pillars:** Overview of the Balance Sheet, Income Statement, and 
Statement of Cash Flows [4].
*   **Accounting Standards:** Introduction to GAAP and IFRS and their 
convergence [5].

---

## Chapter 2: The Income Statement – Measuring Profitability
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Differentiate between accrual and cash accounting [6, 7].
*   Categorize expenses into operating, financing, and capital buckets [8].
*   Analyze how the income statement evolves through a company’s life cycle [9, 
10].

**Key Topics:**
*   **Accrual vs. Cash Accounting:** Recognizing transactions when they happen 
versus when cash changes hands [7].
*   **Income Statement Structure:** From Revenues and COGS to Gross Profit, 
Operating Profit, and Net Income [11].
*   **Expense Classification:** Defining operating (current benefits), financing
(cost of debt), and capital (multi-year benefits) expenses [8, 12].
*   **Depreciation:** Differentiating between economic, accounting 
(straight-line), and tax depreciation [13].
*   **Life Cycle Dynamics:** Analyzing earnings patterns in young (Peloton), 
growth (Netflix), mature (Coca-Cola), and declining (Toyota) companies [10, 
14-16].

---

## Chapter 3: The Balance Sheet – Assessing Value and Obligations
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Compare historical cost versus fair value accounting [17, 18].
*   Identify the components of assets, liabilities, and equity [18].
*   Understand the "fiction" of goodwill and the impact of stock buybacks [19, 
20].

**Key Topics:**
*   **Asset Categories:** Fixed (tangible), current (short-term), financial 
(investments), and intangible assets [18, 21].
*   **Intangible Assets:** The critique of Goodwill as a "plug" variable and the
limitations of accounting for brands and patents [19, 22, 23].
*   **Liabilities and Debt:** Distinguishing between interest-bearing debt and 
non-interest-bearing liabilities [24].
*   **Shareholders’ Equity:** The impact of retained earnings and how stock 
buybacks can lead to negative book equity [20, 25, 26].
*   **Sector Variations:** Unique balance sheet items in banking (financial 
assets) and commodities (inventory volatility) [23].

---

## Chapter 4: The Statement of Cash Flows – Following the Cash
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Convert accrual-based net income into cash flow from operations [27, 28].
*   Analyze the reinvestment needs and financing choices of a firm [29, 30].
*   Calculate Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) [31].

**Key Topics:**
*   **Three Sections of Cash Flow:** 
    1.  **Operating:** Starting with net income and adjusting for non-cash 
charges and working capital [27, 32].
    2.  **Investing:** Capital expenditures (CapEx), acquisitions, and 
divestitures [29, 33].
    3.  **Financing:** Debt issuances/repayments and equity returns (dividends 
and buybacks) [30, 34].
*   **Non-Cash Adjustments:** The treatment of depreciation and Stock-Based 
Compensation (SBC) [27, 35].
*   **Life Cycle Cash Patterns:** Why young firms have negative 
operating/investing cash flows but positive financing cash flows [36-38].

---

## Chapter 5: Accounting Inconsistencies and Adjustments
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Navigate the differences between marginal, effective, and cash tax rates 
[39].
*   Convert operating lease commitments into debt [40, 41].
*   Capitalize R&D and other "capital-like" operating expenses [42-44].

**Key Topics:**
*   **Taxes:** Understanding Deferred Tax Assets (DTAs) and Liabilities (DTLs) 
and Net Operating Losses (NOLs) [45-47].
*   **Lease Accounting:** The 2019 shift to treating all leases as debt and its 
impact on operating income and assets [40, 41, 48].
*   **R&D Capitalization:** The 3-step process to treat R&D as a capital 
expenditure: defining life, collecting history, and amortizing [43, 44, 49].
*   **Stock-Based Compensation:** Debunking the myth that SBC is a non-cash 
expense like depreciation; treating it as a real dilution of value [40, 50, 51].

---

## Chapter 6: Financial Ratios – Standardization and Analysis
**Learning Objectives:**
*   Standardize company performance across different scales and time periods 
[52].
*   Calculate and interpret key ratios for profitability, efficiency, and 
leverage [53-55].
*   Use ratios to build a "story" about a company's future [56].

**Key Topics:**
*   **Profit Margins:** Gross, Operating, EBITDA, and Net margins [53, 57].
*   **Accounting Returns:** Return on Equity (ROE) and Return on Invested 
Capital (ROIC) [54, 58].
*   **Efficiency Ratios:** Asset turnover and Sales-to-Invested Capital (the 
"dark side" of growth) [55, 59, 60].
*   **Leverage and Risk:** Debt-to-Equity and Debt-to-Capital (Book vs. Market 
value), and Interest Coverage ratios [55, 60-62].
*   **Analysis Pitfalls:** Avoiding "ratio noise" and focusing on 
future-oriented insights [52, 56].
