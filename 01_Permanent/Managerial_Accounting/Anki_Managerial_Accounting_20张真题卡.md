# Managerial Accounting Anki Flashcards

**Card 1**
**Q:** On the Statement of Cash Flows (Ch 13), how is the **Indirect Method** 
adjustment for a **Gain on Sale of Equipment** handled, and what is the 
underlying accounting logic?
**A:** A gain is **subtracted** from Net Income in the Operating Activities 
section [1-3]. The logic is that the total cash proceeds from the sale are 
recognized in the **Investing Activities** section; because the gain is a 
non-cash component of Net Income that does not represent an *additional* cash 
inflow from operations, it must be removed to avoid double-counting and to 
isolate purely operational cash flows [2-4].

**Card 2**
**Q:** Define the three components of the **DuPont Equation** (Ch 14) and 
explain how they interact to determine **Return on Equity (ROE)**.
**A:** The DuPont Equation deconstructs ROE into: 
1. **Profitability:** $\frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}}$ (Net Profit 
Margin)
2. **Efficiency:** $\frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Assets}}$ (Total Asset Turnover)
3. **Leverage:** $\frac{\text{Assets}}{\text{Equity}}$ (Equity Multiplier) [5, 
6]. 
Mathematically, $\text{ROE} = \text{Profitability} \times \text{Efficiency} 
\times \text{Leverage}$. This framework allows managers to identify whether a 
superior return is driven by high margins, efficient asset utilization, or the 
use of financial debt [7, 8].

**Card 3**
**Q:** What is the primary difference between **Product Costs** and **Period 
Costs** (Ch 15) regarding their impact on the Balance Sheet and Income 
Statement?
**A:** **Product Costs** (DM, DL, and MOH) are capitalized as assets (Inventory)
on the Balance Sheet and only become an expense (**COGS**) on the Income 
Statement when the product is sold [9-11]. **Period Costs** (SG&A) are not 
related to production and are expensed on the Income Statement in the period 
they are incurred [3, 12, 13].

**Card 4**
**Q:** In a **Job Order Costing** system (Ch 16), how is **Underapplied 
Overhead** calculated and what does it indicate about the period's financial 
reporting?
**A:** Underapplied Overhead occurs when **Actual MOH > Applied MOH** [14, 15]. 
It indicates that the actual factory costs incurred were higher than the 
estimates used to cost products during the period; consequently, the cost of 
goods sold was originally understated, and a debit adjustment to COGS is 
typically required at year-end to reflect the higher actual costs [15, 16].

**Card 5**
**Q:** Explain the concept of **Equivalent Units of Production (EUP)** in 
Process Costing (Ch 17) and why it is essential for the Production Summary.
**A:** EUP measures the work done on partially completed units in terms of fully
completed units [17, 18]. It is calculated as: $\text{EUP} = \text{Units 
Completed} + (\text{Ending WIP Units} \times \% \text{Completion})$ [19, 20]. It
is essential for determining the **Unit Cost**, as total manufacturing costs 
must be divided by the total work performed (EUP), not just the number of 
finished physical units [21, 22].

**Card 6**
**Q:** How does **Activity-Based Costing (ABC)** (Ch 18) address the phenomenon 
of **"Product Cross-Subsidization"** found in traditional costing?
**A:** Traditional costing often uses a single plantwide rate (like direct labor
hours), causing high-volume, simple products to over-absorb overhead and 
effectively subsidize low-volume, complex products [23, 24]. ABC assigns 
overhead to products based on their actual consumption of specific activities 
(e.g., setups, inspections), leading to more accurate costing where complex 
products bear the costs of the complexity they create [25, 26].

**Card 7**
**Q:** In cost estimation (Ch 19), what is the **Coefficient of Determination 
($R^2$)** and how does it measure the strength of a cost model?
**A:** $R^2$ represents the percentage of variability in the total cost (Y) that
can be explained by the change in activity level (X) [27]. For example, an $R^2$
of 0.723 suggests that 72.3% of cost fluctuations are explained by the chosen 
cost driver, while the remaining 27.7% is due to other factors [27]. A higher 
$R^2$ indicates a more reliable cost-prediction model.

**Card 8**
**Q:** What is the formula for **Breakeven in Units** and **Breakeven in 
Dollars** (Ch 20)?
**A:** 
* **Units:** $\frac{\text{Total Fixed Costs}}{\text{Contribution Margin per 
Unit}}$ [28, 29].
* **Dollars:** $\frac{\text{Total Fixed Costs}}{\text{Contribution Margin Ratio 
(\%)}}$ [29, 30].
The breakeven point is where total contribution margin exactly equals total 
fixed costs, resulting in zero net income [28, 29].

**Card 9**
**Q:** Define **Margin of Safety** (Ch 20) and explain its significance as a 
risk metric.
**A:** The Margin of Safety is the difference between current (or budgeted) 
sales and breakeven sales [31]. 
$\text{MoS \%} = \frac{\text{Actual Sales} - \text{Breakeven 
Sales}}{\text{Actual Sales}}$ [31, 32].
It represents the "cushion" a company has before it starts incurring a loss; a 
higher percentage indicates lower risk if demand suddenly drops [32].

**Card 10**
**Q:** Why does **Absorption Costing Net Income** differ from **Variable Costing
Net Income** (Ch 21) when production exceeds sales?
**A:** Under **Absorption Costing**, a portion of Fixed MOH is "trapped" in 
ending inventory as a capitalized asset [33, 34]. Under **Variable Costing**, 
100% of Fixed MOH is expensed as a period cost [34, 35]. When **Production > 
Sales**, inventory increases, causing Absorption Net Income to be higher because
less fixed overhead was expensed during the period [33, 34].

**Card 11**
**Q:** What is the specific mathematical relationship used to reconcile the 
difference between Absorption and Variable Net Income?
**A:** $\text{Difference in Net Income} = \Delta \text{Inventory Units} \times 
\text{Fixed MOH per Unit}$ [33].

**Card 12**
**Q:** In **Master Budgeting** (Ch 22), what is the formula to determine the 
**Units to Produce**?
**A:** $\text{Units to Produce} = \text{Budgeted Sales Units} + \text{Target 
Ending Inventory} - \text{Beginning Inventory}$ [36, 37]. The target ending 
inventory is typically set as a percentage of the *following* month's sales to 
create a buffer [38, 39].

**Card 13**
**Q:** How is **Standard Quantity (SQ)** determined in Variance Analysis (Ch 
23)?
**A:** $SQ = \text{Actual Units Produced} \times \text{Standard Quantity of 
Input per Unit}$ [40, 41]. This "flexes" the budget to show the inputs that 
*should* have been used for the actual output achieved, rather than the original
planned output [42].

**Card 14**
**Q:** Define the **Direct Labor Efficiency Variance** (Ch 23) and provide the 
formula.
**A:** It measures the financial impact of using more or fewer labor hours than 
the standard allowed for the actual production.
Formula: $(AQ \text{ Hours} - SQ \text{ Hours}) \times SP \text{ Rate}$ [43, 
44].

**Card 15**
**Q:** What are the decision rules for **Net Present Value (NPV)** and 
**Internal Rate of Return (IRR)** (Ch 24)?
**A:** 
* **NPV:** Accept if $NPV \geq 0$. This indicates the project meets or exceeds 
the required rate of return [45, 46].
* **IRR:** Accept if $IRR \geq \text{Required Rate of Return (Hurdle Rate)}$. 
This indicates the actual percentage return exceeds the cost of capital [47, 
48].

**Card 16**
**Q:** Define the **Profitability Index (PI)** (Ch 24) and explain when it is 
most useful.
**A:** $\text{PI} = \frac{\text{Present Value of Future Cash 
Inflows}}{\text{Initial Investment}}$ [49]. It is most useful for comparing and 
ranking projects of different sizes, as it measures the "bang for the buck" or 
return per dollar invested [50].

**Card 17**
**Q:** In **Relevant Costing** (Ch 25), what is the definition of a **Sunk 
Cost** and how should it be treated in decision-making?
**A:** A Sunk Cost is a cost that has already been incurred and cannot be 
changed by any future decision (e.g., the historical cost of a machine) [51, 
52]. Sunk costs are **irrelevant** and should be ignored; only future costs that
differ between alternatives are relevant [53, 54].

**Card 18**
**Q:** In a **Make or Buy (Outsourcing)** decision (Ch 25), what is the formula 
for the "Cost to Make"?
**A:** $\text{Cost to Make} = \text{Total Variable Costs} + \text{Avoidable 
Fixed Costs}$ [55, 56]. Unavoidable fixed costs are irrelevant because they will
persist regardless of the decision [55, 56].

**Card 19**
**Q:** When considering a **Special Order** (Ch 25), what is the minimum 
acceptable price if there is **excess capacity**?
**A:** The minimum price is anything greater than the **variable cost per unit**
[52, 57]. Since fixed costs are already covered by regular sales and will not 
increase due to the order, any price above the variable cost contributes to 
total net income [58, 59].

**Card 20**
**Q:** Why might dropping a "losing" product line (Ch 25) actually decrease 
overall company profit?
**A:** If the product line has a **positive Contribution Margin**, it is helping
to cover the company's unavoidable fixed costs [60, 61]. If the line is dropped,
the positive contribution is lost, but the unavoidable fixed costs remain and 
must be absorbed by the other, remaining product lines [60, 62].
