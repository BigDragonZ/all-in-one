# Chapter 14: Financial Statement Analysis — Deep-Dive Notes

## 1. Definition and Core Concepts
Financial Statement Analysis is the systematic process of **normalizing** 
financial data to facilitate comparisons over time and between entities of 
disparate sizes [1, 2]. It transforms raw accounting figures into actionable 
intelligence for internal and external stakeholders.

### Primary Analytical Frameworks
*   **Horizontal (Trend) Analysis:** Focuses on the trajectory of performance 
metrics over multiple periods (e.g., Year 1 to Year 3) to quantify growth or 
contraction in revenue, costs, and profits [3, 4].
*   **Vertical (Common-Size) Analysis:** Standardizes statements by expressing 
each line item as a percentage of a base figure. On the **Income Statement**, 
the base is **Net Sales** (100%); on the **Balance Sheet**, the base is **Total 
Assets** (100%) [2, 5-7].
*   **The PALMS Framework:** A graduate-level organizing principle for ratios: 
**P**rofitability, **A**ctivity, **L**iquidity, **M**arket, and **S**olvency 
[8].

## 2. Mathematical Derivations and Formulas

### Normalization Formulas
*   **Trend Percentage:**
    $$ \text{Trend \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Analysis Period Amount}}{\text{Base 
Period Amount}} \right) \times 100\% $$ [3]
*   **Common-Size (Vertical) Normalization:**
    $$ \text{Income Statement Line \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Line 
Item}}{\text{Net Sales}} \right) \times 100\% $$ [5, 6]
    $$ \text{Balance Sheet Line \%} = \left( \frac{\text{Line Item}}{\text{Total
Assets}} \right) \times 100\% $$ [2, 7]

### The Ratio Hierarchy
#### Liquidity (Short-term Debt-Paying Ability) [9]
*   **Current Ratio:** $ \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current 
Liabilities}} $ [10, 11]
*   **Quick (Acid-Test) Ratio:** $ \frac{\text{Cash + Marketable Securities + 
Receivables}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} $ [11, 12]

#### Activity (Asset Efficiency Ratios) [13]
*   **Inventory Turnover:** $ \frac{\text{Cost of Goods Sold}}{\text{Inventory}}
$ [14, 15]
*   **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO):** $ \frac{365}{\text{Receivables Turnover}} 
$ [16, 17]
*   **Total Asset Turnover (TAT):** $ \frac{\text{Net Sales}}{\text{Total 
Assets}} $ [18, 19]

#### Solvency (Long-term Financial Viability) [20]
*   **Debt-to-Equity:** $ \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Total Equity}} $
[21, 22]
*   **Equity Multiplier:** $ \frac{\text{Total Assets}}{\text{Total Equity}} = 1
+ \text{Debt-to-Equity} $ [21, 23, 24]
*   **Times Interest Earned (TIE):** $ \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Interest 
Expense}} $ [23, 24]

#### Profitability and the DuPont Identity
*   **Return on Assets (ROA):** $ \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Total Assets}} 
$ [25, 26]
*   **The DuPont Equation (ROE Deconstruction):**
    $$ \text{ROE} = \underbrace{\left( \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}} 
\right)}_{\text{Profitability}} \times \underbrace{\left( 
\frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Assets}} \right)}_{\text{Efficiency}} \times 
\underbrace{\left( \frac{\text{Assets}}{\text{Equity}} 
\right)}_{\text{Leverage}} $$ [27, 28]

## 3. Real-world Case Studies

### Case A: The Profitability Paradox (Common-Size Utility)
An analysis of a firm experiencing sales growth from $\$350,000$ to $\$420,000$ 
revealed a **decrease in net income**. Common-size analysis pinpointed the 
culprit: **Operating Expenses** jumped from $9.7\%$ to $16.0\%$ of sales, 
overriding the benefits of a stable gross profit margin and growing revenue [1, 
29].

### Case B: Leverage as a Performance Magnifier
A comparison between a "High-Debt" and "Low-Debt" company showed identical 
**ROA** ($9.14\%$) and operating efficiency. However, the High-Debt firm’s 
**ROE** was $32\%$, while the Low-Debt firm’s was only $10.67\%$. The 
$3.5\times$ **Equity Multiplier** of the High-Debt firm magnified the shared 
operating return into a superior return for shareholders [30-32].

## 4. Critical Analysis and Limitations

*   **Arbitrary Allocations:** A major limitation in divisional analysis is that
fixed cost allocations (e.g., $1/3$ split per division) are often arbitrary 
[33]. Managers must be careful not to drop a "losing" division that still 
provides a positive **Contribution Margin**, as its removal would simply shift 
the unavoidable fixed costs to other divisions [34, 35].
*   **The End-of-Period Bias:** While some favor averaging inventory or 
receivables, using **end-of-period balances** is often more useful for 
identifying emerging trends that averages would otherwise "blend" and obscure 
[18].
*   **Contextual Necessity:** Ratios are "meaningless in a vacuum." They require
comparison against **past performance** or **industry benchmarks** to determine 
if a value (like a $28.3$ P/E ratio) indicates a growth stock or an overvalued 
asset [19, 36-38].

*   **Chapter 13 (Statement of Cash Flows):** The liquidity ratios derived in 
Chapter 14 rely on the cash balances reconciled and explained through the 
operating, investing, and financing activities of Chapter 13 [39, 40].
*   **Chapter 15 (Manufacturing Cost Flows):** The **Inventory Turnover** and 
**TAT** ratios are directly influenced by how a firm manages its three inventory
stages (Raw Materials, WIP, Finished Goods) and classifies its product costs 
[41-43].
*   **Chapter 25 (Relevant Costing):** Profitability analysis in Chapter 14 
often serves as the "red flag" for management. If a product line shows a low 
common-size net income, managers then apply the **Relevant Costing** principles 
of Chapter 25 to decide whether to drop the line based on avoidable vs. 
unavoidable costs [44, 45].
