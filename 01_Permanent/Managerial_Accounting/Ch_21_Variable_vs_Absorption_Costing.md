# Chapter 21: Variable Costing vs. Absorption Costing — Deep-Dive Notes

## 1. Definition and Core Concepts
The fundamental distinction between **Variable Costing** (also known as direct 
or marginal costing) and **Absorption Costing** (traditional or full costing) 
lies in the temporal treatment of **Fixed Manufacturing Overhead (MOH)** [1, 2].

*   **Absorption Costing:** Under this framework, all manufacturing 
costs—regardless of behavior—are "absorbed" into the product cost [3, 4]. This 
includes Direct Materials (DM), Direct Labor (DL), Variable MOH, and a pro-rata 
share of Fixed MOH [4, 5]. It is the standard required for external reporting 
under **US GAAP** and **IFRS** [6-8].
*   **Variable Costing:** This method restricts product costs exclusively to 
variable manufacturing inputs (DM, DL, and Variable MOH) [2, 3, 9]. Fixed MOH is
viewed as a **period cost** and is expensed in its entirety during the period it
is incurred, regardless of production or sales volume [1, 2, 10].

### The Reporting Dichotomy
Absorption costing utilizes the **Traditional Income Statement** format, 
emphasizing **Gross Profit**, while variable costing employs the **Contribution 
Margin** format, emphasizing cost behavior [5, 7].

## 2. Mathematical Derivations and Formulas

### Unit Product Costing
The unit cost ($C$) is the primary driver of inventory valuation and COGS.

*   **Variable Unit Cost ($C_v$):**
    $$ C_v = DM_{unit} + DL_{unit} + Variable\ MOH_{unit} $$ [3, 9]
*   **Absorption Unit Cost ($C_a$):**
    $$ C_a = DM_{unit} + DL_{unit} + Variable\ MOH_{unit} + \frac{Total\ Fixed\ 
MOH}{Units\ Produced} $$ [3-5]

### Reconciliation of Net Income
The divergence in net income ($NI$) between the two methods is mathematically 
linked to the change in inventory levels and the deferral of fixed costs [11].

$$ NI_{Absorption} - NI_{Variable} = \Delta \text{Inventory Units} \times 
\text{Fixed MOH per Unit} $$ [11]

**Decision Rules for Income Comparison:**
1.  If **Units Produced > Units Sold** $\implies$ Inventory Increases $\implies$
$NI_{Absorption} > NI_{Variable}$ (Fixed costs are deferred in inventory) [11, 
12].
2.  If **Units Produced < Units Sold** $\implies$ Inventory Decreases $\implies$
$NI_{Variable} > NI_{Absorption}$ (Prior-period fixed costs are released from 
inventory) [11, 12].
3.  If **Units Produced = Units Sold** $\implies$ $NI_{Absorption} = 
NI_{Variable}$ [12].

## 3. Real-world Case Studies

### Blue Company: Volume Disparity Analysis
In a controlled study of Blue Company, the firm produced 5,000 units but sold 
only 4,200, leaving 800 units in ending inventory [12, 13].
*   **Variable Method:** Net Income was **$145,000**. The ending inventory was 
valued at **$20,800** ($800 \times \$26$ variable cost) [9-11].
*   **Absorption Method:** Net Income was **$158,120**. The unit cost rose to 
**$42.40** because it included $16.40 of fixed overhead ($82,000 / 5,000 units) 
[5, 11, 12].
*   **Reconciliation:** The $13,120 difference in profit is exactly the 800 
units of inventory multiplied by the $16.40 fixed MOH per unit [11].

### Ace Company: Unit Cost Comparison
The Ace Company case illustrates the magnitude of cost shifting. By producing 
2,000 units with a total fixed MOH of $15,000, the absorption unit cost was 
$29.50, approximately **34% higher** than the variable unit cost of $22.00 [3]. 
This demonstrates how absorption costing can significantly inflate the reported 
value of current assets on the balance sheet [3, 12].

## 4. Critical Analysis and Limitations

*   **Profit Manipulation (The Production Incentive):** A significant limitation
of absorption costing is that managers can artificially increase reported net 
income by increasing production [11]. By overproducing, more fixed MOH is 
"trapped" in ending inventory on the balance sheet rather than being expensed on
the income statement, potentially masking poor sales performance [11].
*   **Decision Obscurity:** Absorption costing can make a product appear 
unprofitable if it has a low gross profit margin after high fixed cost 
allocations, even if it has a high contribution margin that helps cover those 
fixed costs [7, 10].
*   **Internal Utility:** Variable costing is generally considered superior for 
internal management because it provides a clearer picture of how profits will 
react to changes in sales volume, unclouded by changes in inventory levels [7, 
10].
