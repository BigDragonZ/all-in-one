Let's start by thinking about what economics is, and that discussion begins with **scarcity**. The idea behind scarcity is that society has **unlimited wants and limited resources**. It's impossible for us to produce all of the goods and services that people want to consume. Scarcity leads to **trade-offs**, which we'll get into in just a second, but scarcity is also what leads to the field of **economics**.

Let's talk for a second about what economists do and what the field of economics is. One of the exercises that I go through with my face-to-face classes is something I started when I first began teaching. On the first day of class, before we talked about anything, we would meet, discuss the syllabus, and I would explain what I expected from the students and what they could expect from me. We wouldn't have time to get into the material, so I would send them home with a little survey. The survey was designed to get them to think about some of the topics we would discuss in class and to gather some information about them.

One of the questions I asked on that first-day survey was designed to help them understand what economics was. I would give them a list of topics, and the question was: "Which of the following subjects do you think economics can help us understand?" I wanted them to put a checkmark if they thought the topic was something we would study in an economics class. I told them it wasn't a trick question; economics doesn't help us understand everything.

Let me read some of these topics to you, and you can think about whether you believe we would talk about them in an economics class. The first one is the **business cycle**. What I do now on the first day of class is I just read these off and have the class raise their hands. When I say "the business cycle," everybody recognizes that as an economics topic and raises their hand. When it was a survey, everybody would check that. The second one: what causes **unemployment**? Again, people typically raise their hand.

Let me just read some of the others: the freezing point of milk; what causes **inflation**; how the human heart works; how fast people drive on the highway; how to maximize profit; how much people eat at a buffet; how much students study for a test; the cause of earthquakes; how long a homeowner waits to mow their lawn; how long students pay attention during an economics lecture; how the banking system works; why stock prices change; how long parents wait before checking on a crying baby; why criminals sometimes voluntarily confess to the police; why gamblers tend to lose money; when a baserunner decides to steal a base; why fish swim in schools; why politicians engage in negative campaigns.

There are several more on the list. Of course, what happens, whether it's students checking a box on a survey or raising their hand in a classroom, is that hands go up when I say something like "how to maximize profit," "how the banking system works," or "why stock prices change." Everybody's hand goes up. But when I mention topics like "how long a homeowner waits to mow their lawn," "how fast people drive on the highway," or "when a base runner decides to steal a base," hardly any hands ever go up. And of course, if I were to say something like "why fish swim in schools," no hands go up.

The reason I do this is to illustrate that if your hand went up for topics like the banking system or the stock market, but it didn't go up when I said "how long students pay attention during an economics lecture" or "how fast people drive on the highway," then you don't really understand what economics is. People typically raise their hand when the topic has something to do with dollars: stock prices, the banking system, inflation, and stuff like that. But they skip the topics like how much people eat at a buffet or how long a homeowner waits to mow their lawn. That's economics. All of those things you do raise your hand for, like the business cycle, are economics too. But a lot of that other stuff I read, which most people don't raise their hand for, is also economics.

Let's talk about the definition of economics. At its most basic level, it's the **study of human behavior**. That is what economists do; they study human behavior. Once you understand that, you should raise your hand for everything that involves somebody making a decision. How long a homeowner waits to mow their lawn is a human behavior. How long parents wait before checking on a crying baby, or when a baserunner decides to steal a base—there's a lot of economics literature devoted to things like that.

The real reason I do this exercise in class or with a survey is to get students to realize that economics is not just about the financial fate of people. We can and will talk about that, but economics is so much more. Economics is about human behavior. If I were to ask a thousand people on the street where on a college campus you should go to study human behavior, a vast majority of them would say you should go to the sociology or psychology department. And those are places where they study human behavior. But if you want to study human behavior at its most basic level, the Economics Department is where that's going on. We typically talk a lot about business-type situations because there are large numbers of dollars on the line. If you understand human behavior, it's natural to talk about it in the context of large amounts of dollars being transacted. But at its most basic level, it's the study of human behavior.

Turns out, it's actually more general than that. There's some economics literature where they study the behavior of animals. For example, consider a rat in a cage. At the end of the cage, there's a device where the rat presses a lever. If they press the lever once, a piece of food falls out, and the rat eats it. If they press it again, another piece of food falls out. If we were to observe the amount of food the rat consumes in a day and then change the "price" of food for the rat, what would happen? If I were to ask most people on the street, they would say it won't matter; the rat won't respond because it's just a rat with nothing to do all day except press the lever and eat. They assume it will eat the same amount of food.

Turns out, that's not at all what happens. Rats respond to prices the same way we do. When the price of something goes up, all other things equal, we want to buy less of it. If you raise the price of food for the rat, it will consume less food. "Raising the price" in this context means making it press the bar more than one time for a piece of food. If you raise the price, it will consume less. So, some economic literature is devoted to the study of behavior, whether it's human or another animal. Obviously, in this class, we're not going to be thinking about the eating habits of rats. We're going to be thinking about the study of human behavior. But at its most basic level, that's what we study in an economics class: we study how people make decisions.

In this video, we're going to talk about ten basic principles of economics. These are principles that you tend to see in any economics class you might take. The field of economics is very broad; it's an old discipline with lots and lots of sub-disciplines. The big distinction is between **microeconomics** and **macroeconomics**. In macro, you're looking at the big picture: how an economy functions. In micro, you're looking at the small picture: how an individual or an individual business might make its decisions.

Within those two main categories, we've got lots of subdivisions. There are all kinds of different subfields within economics: there's labor economics, international economics, public choice theory, game theory, econometrics, and a subdivision called **Cliometrics**, which is the overlap between history and economics, and many others. Regardless of which sub-discipline you're studying, there are some basic principles that tend to pop up in all of them. So, what we're going to do here is talk about those basic principles. The numbers are not important, but let's run through them.

The first one is that **people face trade-offs**. You might think that goes without saying, but it doesn't. The idea here is that to get something you want, you're always going to have to give up something else you want. We could be thinking about how you choose to spend your household income. You have some income from working, and you have to decide whether to spend it on pizzas, textbooks, a movie, or all the other things you could spend that money on. If you spend money on one thing, that's money that can't be spent on something else, so there's a trade-off. We could also think about your time. How do you spend your time? There's a trade-off there. If you spend one hour studying for a test, that's an hour you can't spend sleeping, going out with friends, or watching TV. You face trade-offs in terms of income, time, and many other opportunities.

It's also the case that society faces trade-offs. A classic trade-off that society faces is between the production of consumer goods and services and national defense. Sometimes we refer to that as the **"guns versus butter" trade-off**. We like to have both national defense and consumer goods and services, and it would be great if we could just have more of everything, but we can't. Any resources that we devote to national defense are resources that can't be devoted to building new roads or providing scholarships for school.

There's also a trade-off that society faces between **efficiency** and **equity**. **Efficiency** has to do with the **size of the economic pie**, while **equity** has to do with **how fairly the pie is divided**. Unfortunately, there's a trade-off there. It would be great if there wasn't, but the reality is that if we put more emphasis on equity—if we try to make sure that everybody has an equal slice of the pie—then the economic pie gets smaller. The reason for that is that ensuring everyone has the same size slice means we're taking resources from people who earned them and providing them to people who didn't. Every economy does that, and this is not an argument that we should do none of that. But we have to recognize the reality that the more of that we do, the less of everything there is to go around. Society faces trade-offs just like we individually face trade-offs.

Let's think about the second principle: **the cost of something is what you give up to get it**. You'll learn quickly in this class that I'm going to use the word "cost" a little more generally than you've used it in the past. Typically, when we use the word cost, we refer to the number of dollars you've given up to get something. If you have a cup of coffee and I ask, "How much did that cost you?" you might say, "That cost me two dollars." You would recognize that I'm using "cost" to refer to the number of dollars. I'm going to use the word more generally to refer to whatever you give up to get it. It could be that you had to give up two dollars plus the effort to walk over to the place to get it.

Economists typically use the phrase **opportunity cost** to remind ourselves that it's more than just the dollars you have to give up. The dollars are certainly part of the cost of something, but there are other things you're giving up, and we have to include whatever it is that you've given up. Let's think about the opportunity cost of going to class. There are no dollars transacted when you walk into a classroom; the professor doesn't require any money at the door. The tuition has already been paid. We're talking about just going to that particular class, walking in, and sitting down to listen. There are no dollars transacted there. The opportunity cost of going to class is the time it takes you to go, sit there, and get back to your apartment or dorm room.

It's tempting at first to think that the opportunity cost is giving up your time, but we have to be careful. It's not the number of minutes that is the cost; it's whatever you would have done in those minutes. It's whatever your **next best alternative use of your time** would be. It could be that if you weren't going to class, you would spend that time sleeping. So by going to class, what you're giving up is sleeping. Or it could be that your next best use of time would be playing video games. Or maybe your parents are in town, and they call you in the morning and say, "Hey, we'd like to take you out to lunch." And you say, "I've got class at 11 o'clock, I can't go." Clearly, that would be a bigger thing to give up than just sleeping. So it's not the number of minutes; it's whatever your next best alternative is.

Let's think about buying something, like a pizza. Suppose you call the pizza place, and they say it's ten dollars. You pay with your credit card, and they deliver it. Let's think about the opportunity cost. In this case, there is a dollar component because you paid ten dollars. It's tempting to say that part of the opportunity cost is ten dollars, but we have to remind ourselves that it's whatever you would have spent the ten dollars on. What you really give up is not the ten dollars itself; it's the other goods and services that you could have bought with the ten dollars. Plus, there's a little bit of effort—you had to make a phone call and get up off the couch to get the pizza. That's part of the cost. The big thing here is that you gave up other goods and services.

When we talk about the cost of something, we're going to include the dollars you have to pay, but there are lots of behaviors, like going to class, where there's no dollar transaction, but there's still a cost. It's whatever your next best alternative is. One common mistake students make is to say, "If I didn't go to class, I could be watching TV, playing video games, hanging out with my friends, or going for a walk." You can list an infinite number of things you could be doing. Sometimes students say what they're giving up is infinite. We're always going to think about just your next best alternative. The cost of something is what you give up, and that leads to the basic idea in economics that you've probably heard before: **there's no such thing as a free lunch**. There is nothing that is truly free; everything has a cost. If you define cost as just dollars, then sometimes you might say something is free. But we're defining it as whatever you give up to get it, and in that case, there is no such thing as a free lunch. There is always a cost associated with everything.

Let's talk about principle number three: **rational people respond to incentives**. You might think this is another one that goes without saying, but it's actually really easy to forget. The reason we think about people responding to incentives is because we're interested in understanding human behavior. Incentives are at the root of all behavior. It's not always easy to see the incentives that change, so you may not be able to understand why somebody engaged in a particular behavior, but you can rest assured that all behavior is a response to incentives.

Let's talk about different types of incentives. We can think about what we call **economic incentives**. An economic incentive is typically what we mean when we're talking about dollars or points in a class. If I give a test, the reason I put points on it is to give you an incentive to earn those points and get a good grade. If you are studying to do well on the test, it's easy to understand why you're responding to that incentive. Economic incentives are often really easy to see.

But there are other types of incentives that are not as easy to see. We can talk about **social incentives**. Social incentives are created by society, such as the desire for acceptance or the avoidance of ridicule. A lot of the behavior we all engage in is driven by our desire to be accepted by people we respect and to avoid being ridiculed. Whether that's good or bad is another discussion, but if we want to understand behavior, we must recognize these social incentives. More of our behavior is driven by social incentives than by economic incentives.

Then we could also think about **moral incentives**. A lot of your behavior is driven by your sense of what's right and what's wrong. These two types of incentives, social and moral, are much harder to see than economic incentives. So, while we can be assured that behavior is driven by incentives, that doesn't make it easy to understand all behavior because some incentives are very challenging for us to observe. It's also important that not everyone responds to the same incentive in the same way. I might give all my students the exact same economic incentive (points on a test), but I will observe them all reacting somewhat differently. Some students will study really hard for every point, while other students, for whatever reason, will not.

Let's talk about how easy it is to forget that people respond to incentives by discussing our consumption of oil. Let me give you some numbers. The amount of crude oil in reserve in the world is about 531,000,000,000 (531 billion) barrels. This is a real number. "In reserve" means we know where it is and we can get it out. World annual usage of crude oil is about 16,500,000,000 (16.5 billion) barrels per year. When politicians and activists use numbers like these, they often divide the reserve amount by the annual usage to get the number of years before we run out of oil. Using these numbers, that would be something in the 30s.

What if I were to tell you that that's a completely wrong way to use those numbers? What if I told you that the right answer to the question "When will we run out of oil?" is **never**? We will never, ever run out of oil. I can say that with 100% confidence. Anyone who tells you otherwise either has never taken an economics class, doesn't understand that people respond to incentives, or is lying to you. How do I know for sure? Because I know people respond to incentives.

Let me give you a different example. Suppose for your birthday I give you a giant room full of peanuts—531 billion peanuts, to be precise. The only rule is you can't take anything out of the room; shells must stay inside. You and your friends love peanuts, so you start eating them. On day one, the cost of eating a peanut is practically zero. You can reach anywhere and grab one. But as you eat more and more, the shells start to pile up and cover the good peanuts. Now, the cost of eating a peanut is no longer close to free. You have to dig through a layer of shells to get to the good ones.

You don't need an economics class to know what happens when something gets more expensive: we consume less of it. As the cost of consuming peanuts from the room goes up, you and your friends will consume fewer of them. Eventually, the cost will rise so much that it's no longer worth it. You'll realize it's easier to just drive to Walmart and buy a bag of peanuts. So, when will you run out of peanuts in the peanut room? Never. You'll voluntarily choose to consume an alternative when the cost gets too high.

That's what will happen with oil. When will we consume the last barrel of oil? Never. Eventually, oil will get expensive enough that we will voluntarily switch to some other alternative source of energy. When we switch, it won't be because of education or environmental awareness; it will be because the alternative has become cheaper. People respond to incentives. It is not appropriate to simply divide the total reserve by current consumption to project how long it will last.

Let's talk about principle number four: **rational people think at the margin**. A **marginal change** is an **incremental change to a plan of action**. You can think of the margin as the edge of decision-making. It's very rare to have a plan of action and then dogmatically stick to it regardless of what happens. What happens is you have a plan, you start to execute it, and you pay attention to how incentives change, and your plan may switch.

Studying is a great example. Do you ever decide, "I'm going to study for exactly 82 minutes and 17 seconds"? You don't. Instead, you have a plan to study, and you make decisions at the margin. A decision-maker takes an action if and only if the **marginal benefit of the action is greater than the marginal cost**. I'll abbreviate this as $MB > MC$. This is a really, really important concept.

The **marginal benefit** ($MB$) is the additional benefit you get from continuing an action. The **marginal cost** ($MC$) is the additional cost you incur. When you're studying, the marginal benefit is the additional knowledge you gain. The marginal cost is what you're giving up (your next best alternative). If the marginal cost is low (your friends aren't doing anything) and the marginal benefit is high (you're learning a lot), you continue to study because $MB > MC$.

Now, suppose a friend knocks on the door and invites you to go out. The incentives have changed. The marginal benefit of studying hasn't changed, but the marginal cost has gone up. Does that mean you quit studying? It depends. If the marginal cost goes up but is still less than the marginal benefit, you continue to study. But if the marginal cost is now bigger than the marginal benefit, you might close your book and go out. What matters is the relative size of $MB$ and $MC$. They could both be very high or very low, but the comparison between them is what drives the decision. That's what it means to think at the margin.

Next is principle number five: **trade can make everyone better off**. This doesn't say trade *always* makes everyone better off, but that it *can*. We can think about this in terms of trade between people or between countries. It's not uncommon for people to be skeptical of international trade because they think the world is a **zero-sum game**—a game where if I win, you have to lose. Poker is a zero-sum game.

Trade is not that way. Voluntary trade is a **positive-sum game**, meaning we can all walk away having gained from it. An easy way to see this is to think about what would happen if you didn't trade. If you decide to "Buy American," you close off options from other countries. If "Buy American" is good, what about "Buy Missouri"? Or "Buy Warrensburg"? You quickly realize that the less you trade, the more stuff you have to produce yourself, and the worse off you become. The absence of trade makes people worse off.

Principle number six is that **markets are usually a good way to organize economic activity**. I'll insert the word "free" here: **free markets** are the best way. By "free market," we don't mean a situation with a complete absence of regulation where sellers can do whatever they want. What we mean is that sellers are free to sell what they want within the bounds of the law, and consumers are free to buy what they want within the bounds of the law. The alternative to a free market is a **planned economy**, like socialism or communism, where the government owns the means of production. A basic principle of economics is that capitalism (free markets) is a better way to organize economic activity than communism or socialism.

That doesn't mean capitalism is perfect. This leads to principle number seven: **governments can sometimes improve market outcomes**. This happens when there is what we call a **market failure**. Free markets are great, but sometimes they don't work quite as well. For example, we can have an **externality**, which is when one person's behavior imposes a cost on somebody else. In cases of market failure, the government can sometimes improve the free-market outcome.

Principle number eight: **a country's standard of living depends on its ability to produce things other people want to buy**. This is also true for an individual. Your standard of living in the future will depend on your ability to produce goods or services that other people want to buy from you. That's why it's important to have skills that other people are willing to pay for.

Principle number nine is that **prices rise when the government prints too much money**. When the government prints too much money, it drives prices up, causing **inflation**. This is a basic principle of macroeconomics.

The tenth principle is that **society faces a short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment**. In the short run, if the government wants to decrease inflation, it may cause unemployment to rise, and vice versa. There's a trade-off. We have to decide which one we dislike more because policies to decrease one will tend to increase the other.

These are the ten principles. In our next videos, we'll think about some basic things that economists do and how they do it. Then we'll move on to talk more about the principle that trade can make everyone better off.
