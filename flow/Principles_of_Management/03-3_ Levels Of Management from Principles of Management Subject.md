In the first two classes on Principles of Management, I gave an introduction to what management is, its objectives, and its significance. Today, we will discuss one of the most important topics: Levels of Management. This is a mandatory topic for every student and is useful not just for examinations, but also in practice.

You know that management has to manage the entire organization. Strictly speaking, there are two types of levels: organizational levels and administrative levels. If we only follow these two levels to handle the organization, it becomes very difficult. They can't manage everything. This might work for a very small, tiny organization, but when an organization is large, it's difficult. That is why we need different levels of management. Management is not just the top management; there are different levels. From these different levels, we can handle, organize, and control the organization. This is why there are three levels of management.

Let me discuss the levels of management. There are a total of three levels. The first is the **Top-Level Management** (Level 1). The second is the **Middle-Level Management** (Level 2), and the third is the **Lower or Operating Level Management** (Level 3).

This structure is what we call an organizational hierarchy. You have to follow the hierarchy. You might hear phrases like, "Don't come to me directly," when you join an organization. Top-level management doesn't handle problems related to the lower levels or employees directly. They want to hear only from the middle management; their connection is with them. Middle-level management, in turn, talks only with the lower-level management, not directly to the employees or workers. Employees cannot reach the top management or even the middle management directly; they must go through the lower-level management. This hierarchy is maintained for communication flowing both upwards and downwards.

### Top-Level Management

Whom do we call top-level management? Mainly, it includes the **Board of Directors (BOD)**, **Managing Director (MD)**, **President**, **Vice President (VP)**, **Chief Executive Officer (CEO)**, and **Chief Financial Officer (CFO)**. These people are the top-level management. They make the most significant decisions and have control over the organization.

What are their functions?
- **Determining Objectives**: They decide the main organizational objectives.
- **Framing Plans and Policies**: They are responsible for framing the plans and policies.
- **Assigning Jobs to Middle-Level Management**: They assign tasks and responsibilities to the middle-level management.
- **Controlling Departments**: They control the various departments, such as production, HR, marketing, and finance, which are managed by the middle level.
- **Arranging Resources**: They are responsible for arranging all necessary resources, including men, material, money, and machinery. They decide how many employees are needed, how much capital is required, and what machinery is necessary.
- **Providing Overall Leadership**: They provide overall leadership and have the final say in decisions. They need to have a strong command and a comprehensive understanding of the organization's activities, which they gain with the help of middle management. There must be proper coordination between top and middle management.

### Middle-Level Management

Who are the middle-level managers? They are the heads of various departments, such as the **Production Manager**, **Operations Manager**, **Marketing Manager**, **Regional Manager**, **Finance Manager**, and **Human Resource Manager (HRM)**.

What are their functions?
- **Interpretation of Policies**: They interpret the policies framed by the top management and explain them to the lower-level management and employees.
- **Organizing Departmental Activities**: They are responsible for managing and organizing the activities within their specific department. For example, the production manager handles all production activities, and the sales manager focuses on optimizing sales.
- **Selecting Employees**: They are involved in selecting employees for their particular department, often with the help of the HR department.
- **Motivating Employees**: They are supposed to motivate the lower-level management and, through them, the employees. This includes providing incentives, awards, rewards, bonuses, and other fringe benefits.
- **Instructing and Controlling Employees**: They provide instructions on what to do and how to do it, and they control the employees within their department.
- **Cooperating with Other Departments**: They must maintain good cooperation and interaction with other departments to ensure the organization runs smoothly and achieves its goals.
- **Implementing Policies**: It is their duty to implement the policies that have been framed by the top management.
- **Laying Down Departmental Rules**: They can lay down rules and regulations specific to their own department to ensure its smooth functioning.

### Lower or Operational Level Management

This level includes **Supervisors**, **Superintendents**, **Sectional Officers**, **Inspectors**, and **Foremen**. They are at the root level and directly handle the employees and laborers. They are the first to identify any major problems in the organization. They are expected to solve problems at their level, and if they cannot, they bring the issue to the middle-level management.

What are their functions?
- **Addressing Employee Problems and Grievances**: They must address all the problems and grievances of the employees under them.
- **Maintaining Good Working Conditions**: They ensure that both machinery and employees are working in good condition, without any obstructions, to maintain a smooth workflow.
- **Recruitment and Selection of Employees**: They are involved in the recruitment and selection of workers for the tasks at the operational level.
- **Taking Feedback from Employees**: They take feedback from employees. If the employees are happy, the organization will be happy.
- **Maintaining Quality of Products**: They must be very careful to maintain the quality of the products. A drop in quality can damage the company's reputation and reduce sales.
- **Minimizing Wastage**: By minimizing wastage, they help reduce costs, which is crucial for the organization.
- **Motivating Workers**: They are directly responsible for motivating the workers under their control by ensuring they receive proper salaries, bonuses, awards, and other incentives without delay.
