# F.W. Taylor's Scientific Management

## Introduction to F.W. Taylor

Today, we are discussing a very important topic in the Principles of Management: **F.W. Taylor's Scientific Management**. Many students get confused between Taylor and Fayol. We discussed Fayol's 14 principles in the previous class; now, we will focus on Taylor.

F.W. Taylor is known as the **Father of Scientific Management**. He started his career as an apprentice in a steel factory and later rose through the ranks to become a chief executive. He implemented new methods to increase production and decrease costs, which is the optimal goal for any business. He scientifically tested and proved his methods, which are now known as the principles of Scientific Management.

## Definition of Scientific Management

F.W. Taylor provided a simple definition for Scientific Management:

> "Scientific management means knowing exactly what you want men to do and seeing that they do it in the best and cheapest way."

This means clearly defining the work expected from employees and ensuring they perform it in the most efficient and cost-effective manner.

## Techniques of Scientific Management

Taylor proposed eight techniques to implement his management theory. These techniques provide practical ideas for modernization and implementing new methodologies.

### 1. Functional Foremanship

Taylor suggested that the planning and execution functions should be separated. He proposed a system with eight specialist foremen to supervise workers, which increases specialization and efficiency. This structure is divided into two departments: the Planning Department and the Production Department, each with four foremen.

#### Planning Department
This department focuses on planning the work. The four foremen under this department are:
- **Route Clerk**: Designs the exact sequence and path that work will follow through the production process.
- **Instruction Card Clerk**: Prepares detailed instructions for workers on how to perform their jobs, including tools and procedures.
- **Time and Cost Clerk**: Determines the time allowed for a job and prepares the cost sheets for the work.
- **Shop Disciplinarian**: Ensures that workers follow all rules and regulations and that discipline is maintained in the factory.

#### Production Department
This department is responsible for the actual execution of the work. The four foremen are:
- **Gang Boss**: Responsible for arranging the machines, tools, and materials required for the job so that work can begin without delay.
- **Speed Boss**: Ensures that work is completed at the specified speed and on time. He monitors the pace of work and addresses any causes of delay.
- **Repair Boss**: Ensures that all machines and tools are kept in perfect working condition, handling all maintenance and repairs to prevent production stoppages.
- **Inspector**: Checks the quality of the work done by the workers to ensure it meets the set standards.

### 2. Standardization and Simplification of Work

Taylor emphasized establishing standards for every business activity, from processes and raw materials to time and machinery. **Standardization** helps maintain quality and consistency. **Simplification** aims to eliminate unnecessary varieties, sizes, and dimensions of products, which saves costs of labor, machines, and tools. The goal is to achieve economy in using machines, staff, and labor, thereby reducing overall costs.

### 3. Fatigue Study

Recognizing that workers are human and get tired, Taylor advocated for a **Fatigue Study**. This study aims to determine the amount and frequency of rest intervals required for a worker to complete a job. A well-designed schedule of breaks helps workers regain their stamina and work at their optimal capacity, which ultimately increases productivity. The study involves observing a standard worker to determine when they start feeling tired and how much rest they need to recover.

### 4. Method Study

The objective of **Method Study** is to find the "one best way" of doing a job. This involves analyzing all the different methods of performing a task to identify the one that minimizes cost and maximizes quality and customer satisfaction. This includes studying and modernizing production techniques, upgrading methodologies, and adopting the most efficient practices available.

### 5. Time Study

**Time Study** is a technique used to determine the standard time required to perform a well-defined job. Taylor suggested observing a worker's performance over a period to set a standard time for a task. This standard time is then used as a target for all workers. It helps in determining the number of workers required, framing incentive schemes, and controlling labor costs. By setting a fair standard time, management can reduce laziness and increase overall production.

### 6. Motion Study

**Motion Study** involves the close observation of the movements of a worker's body and limbs while performing a job. The purpose is to eliminate wasteful and unproductive movements. By cutting down on unnecessary motions (e.g., walking a long distance to get a tool), the time required to complete a job is reduced, and efficiency is increased. The focus is on differentiating between productive and unproductive movements and redesigning the work process to eliminate the latter.

### 7. Differential Piece-Wage System

This is a wage payment system that links wages directly to performance. Taylor advocated for paying different wage rates to efficient and inefficient workers. A standard output is determined, and two wage rates are set: a higher rate for those who achieve or exceed the standard and a lower rate for those who fall below it. This system strongly motivates workers to improve their efficiency and earn higher wages, which in turn boosts the company's overall productivity. For example, if the standard is 100 units, workers producing 100 or more get a higher rate per piece, while those producing less get a lower rate.

### 8. Mental Revolution

**Mental Revolution** calls for a complete change in the attitude and mindset of both workers and management. Typically, management wants to extract maximum work for minimum pay, while workers want maximum pay for minimum work, leading to conflict. Taylor argued that both sides should realize that their prosperity is intertwined. Management should share the company's gains with the workers, and workers should contribute their best efforts to increase profits. This creates a cooperative and harmonious relationship, which is beneficial for the organization's long-term success.
