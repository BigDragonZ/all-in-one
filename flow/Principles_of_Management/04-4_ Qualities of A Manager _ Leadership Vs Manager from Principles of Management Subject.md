Hello dear students, welcome to Devi's Commerce and Management Academy. In our Principles of Management course, the introduction chapters are over. Today, we'll discuss the qualities of a manager and the difference between leadership and a manager. This is an important topic.

Simply giving someone the post of manager isn't enough. They must focus on management aspects, possess knowledge, be responsible, and have the power to make decisions. The entire organization's success or failure depends on the manager—how they handle the organization, their understanding, and the command they have. That is why we expect certain qualities from a manager.

### Qualities of a Manager

Here are the nine key qualities we expect from a manager:

1.  **Education**: Previously, anyone capable could become a manager, but today, a proper management qualification is expected. While technical knowledge is valuable, a management-related education provides the theoretical foundation. This subject knowledge, combined with practical experience, enables a manager to make better decisions and have a sound understanding of the organization.

2.  **Intelligence**: A manager must be intelligent. Leading the organization towards profit and making sound decisions depends on the manager's ability to handle situations intelligently. We expect a manager to have the skills to manage the organization effectively.

3.  **Leadership Qualities**: A manager is different from a leader. A manager has formal authority from top management. Leadership, however, is about having command over people who willingly accept you as their leader. We expect a manager to also possess these leadership qualities. Every manager must be a leader, but a leader is not necessarily a manager.

4.  **Training**: A manager needs continuous training. This includes technical training related to the company's production and processes, as well as human skills training for handling people and the organization. A manager needs a complete understanding of all departments, management levels (top, middle, lower), products, and profitability. Initial training is crucial, and with experience, their command grows.

5.  **Technical Knowledge**: A manager should have technical knowledge about the organization's operations, processes, and products. If a technical problem arises, this knowledge allows them to make quick, informed decisions to solve it.

6.  **Maturity**: A manager must possess maturity. This means having the patience to understand problems, handle people effectively, and know when to wait before making a decision. A mature manager avoids impulsive actions or hurtful comments, ensuring a stable and respectful work environment.

7.  **Positive Attitude**: A manager needs a positive attitude. This involves handling complaints and situations calmly and constructively. Instead of reacting defensively, they should listen, understand the issue, and handle things without tension.

8.  **Self-confidence**: As the primary decision-maker, a manager holds the organization's fate in their hands. Success is impossible without self-confidence. A manager who lacks confidence cannot be trusted to lead effectively. Self-confidence is built through knowledge, practice, and experience.

9.  **Foresight**: A manager must have foresight—the ability to see into the future. This isn't just about managing the present but anticipating future trends, competition, and challenges, whether one week, one month, or several years ahead. Foresight is essential for strategic planning and making proactive decisions.

### Leadership vs. Manager

Now, let's look at the difference between a manager and a leader.

A **manager** is someone who has formal authority from the top management and uses this power to handle subordinates.

A **leader**, on the other hand, may not have any formal authority. A leader emerges when a group of people trusts an individual to guide them. For example, in a group of 20 people, one person might naturally be looked to for guidance because the others trust their judgment. This person becomes the leader informally, without any official declaration. A good example is a union leader, who is elected by the members themselves.

Every manager should be a leader, but a leader is not necessarily a manager. Here are the key differences:

-   **Basis of Influence**:
    -   A **manager** influences the behavior of their subordinates, who work under their formal authority.
    -   A **leader** leads and influences informal groups, who follow them based on trust.

-   **Authority**:
    -   A **manager** is given formal authority by the organization. It is an official, declared position.
    -   A **leader** has informal authority, granted by the group members themselves. It is not officially assigned.

-   **Objective**:
    -   The main objective of a **manager** is to achieve the goals of the organization.
    -   The main objective of a **leader** is to satisfy the needs and goals of their group members.
