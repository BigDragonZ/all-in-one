Hello dear students, welcome to Devi's Commerce and Management Academy. In Principles of Management, we have learned about planning, and now we will move on to organizing. The planning chapter is complete. Organizing means arranging all things in a proper way so that the organization can achieve its objectives.

In simple terms, you organize your business, your home, or even your room. This means everything is arranged in a systematic way to make things comfortable. In an organization, we organize many things, including material and non-material resources. Non-material resources include human beings or manpower, while material things include money, machinery, etc. We have to organize all these things together to achieve our organization's ambitions. This sometimes involves delegating authority, giving power to others, and ensuring everyone works together systematically.

Coordination and communication are necessary, and there should be no lag. In official language, a simple definition to learn is: **Organizing is the establishment of an authority relationship among persons to achieve organizational objectives.** Remember, it's about the authority relationship among people to achieve the organization's objectives.

Now, what are these organizational aspects and how do we manage them? We must learn a few things, starting with the characteristics or nature of organization. We will also look at the principles of organization and the types of organization. First, let's discuss the characteristics.

The first characteristic is the **division of work**. In any organization, all the work cannot be given to a single person, no matter how expert, knowledgeable, or experienced they are. The work must be divided, which is decentralization. You do your work and assign the rest to others. Before dividing the work, you must be careful to identify the right person with the right skills who you can rely on.

The second characteristic is **coordination**. There must be coordination among all employees for work to go smoothly. Good coordination between employees of the same level and with authority is essential to run the organization smoothly, without any hurdles or hassles.

Third, every organization has **common objectives**. These objectives should be known and followed by the entire organization. Everyone must work towards achieving these common objectives.

Fourth is a **cooperative relationship**. The relationship between employees, and between authorities and subordinates, must be smooth. This relationship can be vertical or horizontal. A vertical relationship is when authority flows from top to bottom, for example, from boss A to employee B, and then to employee C. A horizontal relationship exists when a boss, A, appoints different authorities for different departments, like B, E, and F for the finance, HR, and production departments, respectively. All these heads (B, E, F) are at the same level, working under A. This is a horizontal structure. Whether the relationship is vertical or horizontal, it must be cooperative to achieve our goals.

The final characteristic is a **well-defined authority and responsibility relationship**. When authority is given, it must be accompanied by responsibility. If both are present, we can expect good results and hold people accountable if things go wrong. This allows us to reach a conclusion about performance.

These are the five main characteristics. Now, let's discuss the principles of organization. There are a total of 10 principles we must be aware of.

The first is the **principle of objective**. Every organization has an objective—an ambition, an achievement, a target. Everyone in the organization, from top to bottom, must be aware of this objective and work towards it. It's not just the responsibility of top-level management. When everyone works collectively, you can expect good results.

Second is the **principle of specialization**. This means work should be assigned to a person who is specialized and expert in that area. If a person is specialized, we can make them responsible, and they can give the best of their services and knowledge. If anything goes wrong, we can hold them accountable. Therefore, you should appoint employees who are experts in their particular area.

The next principles are the **principle of coordination** and the **principle of authority relationship**. As we've discussed, proper coordination—good understanding and communication—is a must. Similarly, the authority relationship principle states that you give authority and also make the person responsible for the outcome.

Next is the **span of control**. This refers to the number of employees a supervisor or authority figure is responsible for. For example, if supervisor X is in charge of 15 employees, that is his span of control. The principle suggests we must ensure that the supervisor is capable of handling that number of people effectively. If there are any disturbances or improper handling of things, changes must be made. The span of control helps identify who is controlling whom.

The **principle of balance** states that everything in the organization should go smoothly. Any difficulty that arises must be handled properly, at the right time, by the right people, with the right decision. This prevents problems from escalating and harming the organization.

The **principle of continuity** is based on the expectation that an organization is not temporary; it is meant to run for the long term. To maintain this continuity, you must be prepared to handle any problems that arise. Identify whether a problem is short-term, medium-term, or long-term, and be ready with a solution to handle it effectively.

The **principle of uniformity** says that every organization has certain rules and regulations. These rules must be followed uniformly by the entire organization, with no changes or differences for individuals. The established rules should be distributed to all employees, and they must follow them.

Next is the **principle of unity of command**. This principle states that an employee should have only one boss to whom they are directly responsible. For example, if employees P, Q, and R report to boss X, another boss, Y, from a different department should not give them direct instructions. If Y needs work done by P, Q, or R, Y must approach their boss, X. The work should then be assigned through X. This prevents confusion and conflicting orders.

The **principle of exception** states that higher-level management, like the Board of Directors, should not interfere in the routine, day-to-day activities of lower-level employees. They should only intervene in exceptional cases. For instance, the Board of Directors should communicate with the executive manager, not directly with a production manager or their subordinates on a regular basis. They do not have the right to interfere in regular activities; their involvement should be the exception, not the rule.

So, the ten principles of organization are: objective, specialization, coordination, authority and relationship, span of control, balance, continuity, uniformity, unity of command, and exception.

Finally, let's discuss the types of organization. There are two main types: formal organization and informal organization. A **formal organization** is one where everything is systematic. The organization has prepared rules and regulations, and work is assigned formally. Everyone knows who is responsible for what and by when. Everything is official. An **informal organization**, on the other hand, refers to the unofficial interactions and communications that happen, like rumors or behind-the-scenes talk. This information may or may not be reliable, but it is not officially sanctioned. Both types exist in any organization and cannot be avoided.
