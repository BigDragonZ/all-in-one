What is planning? You can look at planning from many different aspects. A lot goes into planning and producing products, and all of these different aspects have to come together almost flawlessly just to get that product on the shelf. Think about everything that goes into planning for a sandwich or a burger: the bread, lettuce, onions, tomatoes, and meat. You have to get these from different suppliers in most cases and make sure that they are there on time so that you can serve your customer at your restaurant. You don't want to ever be out of anything. This is tough to do, but if you plan well, it becomes manageable.

Essentially, planning is broken into what we call long-term **aggregate planning** and near-term **master production scheduling (MPS)**. First, let's discuss demand types. It all starts with consumer demand. We have **independent demand**, which refers to finished products—the final items that are listed on your master production schedule to be produced. Then you have **dependent demand**, which refers to items that are not finished goods, but rather raw materials or components that will be used in some aspect of production to create a finished good. For dependent demand items, you use **Material Requirements Planning (MRP)**. For independent demand items—the finished goods—you use your **master production schedule**.

The **aggregate plan** combines items into groups or families for planning purposes. This aggregate plan then feeds into the **master production schedule (MPS)**. The MPS, in turn, disaggregates the aggregate plan, breaking it down into specific, detailed production plans.

Let's go over a few key terms for the master production schedule and planning:
- **Planning Horizon**: This is the length of time covered by the plan, from forecasting to scheduling to production. A medium-range planning horizon is typically 6 to 18 months, which is an industry standard. Long-term planning can be five to ten years. The key is that the planning horizon must be long enough to cover the cumulative lead time to produce the product.
- **Time Fence**: A time fence is a boundary within the planning horizon. If your planning horizon is 18 months and you plan in weekly or quarterly buckets, each of those period boundaries is a type of time fence.
- **Time Bucket**: This is the unit of time used in the plan. It's usually one week, but it can be as short as one hour, depending on the company and what they are producing. The time bucket defines the periods in which you group production.
- **Lead Time**: This is the total time it takes to plan, produce, and deliver a product to the customer. For example, if you order a custom-made computer, the company might tell you the lead time is 21 days, which includes building, manufacturing, and shipping it to you.

The master production schedule works with inputs to generate outputs. Think of ordering at a restaurant: you tell the cashier what you want (the input), and this information is sent to the kitchen, which then starts producing your order (the output). The MPS works the same way. The **inputs** include demand forecasts, actual customer orders, and current inventory levels. Once you feed this information into the MPS, it generates **outputs**, such as how much to produce, what your projected inventory levels will be in future weeks, and the capacity (e.g., number of people) required to meet the production plan.

Another important output is the **Available-to-Promise (ATP)** inventory. Let's say you have 100 units available, but 60 of those are already allocated to customer orders that are due in the future. The remaining 40 units are your ATP inventory—this is the inventory that is not yet committed to a customer order and can be promised to new orders. The MPS also calculates **projected inventory balances**, showing the amount of inventory you are expected to have at the end of each period after production and shipments.

As mentioned, the aggregate plan feeds into the **master production schedule (MPS)**, which disaggregates the plan into a more detailed level. The detailed MPS shows inventory, ATP, and projected inventory levels on a weekly basis. In this example, you can see a problem in March where we have negative numbers for projected inventory. This means we have customer orders coming in that we will not be able to fill. The MPS allows us to see this problem in advance so we can plan around it. The simplified MPS shows what is being produced during those months and weeks, which rolls up to the numbers in the aggregate plan.

Key takeaways: Planning is very important. We have short-term planning (the MPS) and long-term planning (the aggregate plan). The aggregate plan feeds into the MPS, which disaggregates it. Remember the key terms:
- The **planning horizon** is the total time frame of the plan (e.g., 6-18 months).
- **Time buckets** are the periods within the plan (e.g., weekly).
- **Time fences** are the boundaries between these buckets.
- **Lead time** is the total time to get a product to the customer. Lead times can vary dramatically; for example, in the airline industry, the lead time for a complex part could be about a year.
