# Externalities

## Introduction: Externalities as a Market Failure

So far in the class, we've been building on a big picture. We started with the **First Fundamental Theorem of Welfare Economics**, which states that a competitive market will maximize total social welfare. Then we said that this theorem will not hold true under conditions of **market failure**. Remember, market failure doesn't mean market collapse; it means that there are barriers preventing the market from achieving this first-best, welfare-maximizing outcome.

One barrier is imperfect competition. Another was imperfect information. A third barrier to welfare maximization, or a third source of market failure, is **externalities**. That's what we're going to talk about today.

## Defining Externalities

What is an externality? Let's be very clear about the definition. An externality occurs whenever one party's actions make another party better or worse off, but the first party does not bear the consequences of that action. When my actions make you better or worse off, but I don't bear the consequences, then there's an externality.

### Negative Production Externality: The Classic Example

Let's start with the classic example: a **negative production externality**. Imagine you've got a river, and on that river is a steel plant. The steel plant produces steel, but as a byproduct of that production, it dumps sludge into the river. You might remember this from Dr. Seuss's *The Lorax*.

This sludge floats down the river and kills the fish. The death of the fish means that fishermen downstream cannot make as much money. To make the model simple, let's assume that the amount of sludge is directly proportional to the production of steel; for every unit of steel produced, there's one unit of sludge emitted from the factory. This sludge hurts the livelihood of the fishermen.

This is a classic example of an externality because the steel plant's behavior is imposing a cost on the fishermen, but the steel plant doesn't bear any consequence of that cost. They just dump the sludge and forget about it.
*   It's a **negative** externality because the steel plant's actions are hurting the fishermen.
*   It's a **production** externality because it comes out of the production process for steel.

So, a negative production externality means that when one party's production adversely affects another party, the party doing the production doesn't bear any consequences of that effect.

### Graphical Analysis of a Negative Production Externality

What effect does this have? Let's go to Figure 22-1, which shows the market for steel, with the quantity of steel on the x-axis and the price on the y-axis. The market is initially in equilibrium at point A, where the demand curve (the downward-sloping blue line) equals the supply curve (the upward-sloping blue line).

In a perfectly competitive market, the demand curve represents the **marginal benefit** (or marginal willingness to pay) that consumers get from the good. The supply curve represents the firm's **marginal cost**. In a market without externalities, the equilibrium where marginal benefit equals marginal cost yields the welfare-maximizing outcome. The market succeeds.

The difference now is that we're going to drive a wedge between private costs and social costs.
*   The **private marginal cost** (PMC) is the cost to the steel producers, represented by the supply curve.
*   The **social marginal cost** (SMC) includes not only the private cost but also the **marginal damage** (MD) done to the fishermen. Society encompasses all actors: the steel plant *and* the fishermen.

Therefore, the formula is:
**Social Marginal Cost (SMC) = Private Marginal Cost (PMC) + Marginal Damage (MD)**

This social marginal cost is represented by the red line in the figure, which is above the private supply curve. From a welfare perspective, what we care about is the intersection of social marginal benefits and social marginal costs. The social optimum, the welfare-maximizing point, is actually at point C, where the social marginal cost equals the social marginal benefit.

### The Inefficiency of Overproduction

What's happening is that the steel company, by not considering the damage it's doing, overproduces. The company produces at point A, where its private marginal cost equals the private (and in this case, social) marginal benefit. However, the socially optimal point is C, which corresponds to a lower quantity of production (Q2 instead of Q1). Producing less would reduce the damage being done to the fishermen.

This market failure creates a **deadweight loss**, represented by the triangle ABC. This area represents the units traded (between Q2 and Q1) that are socially inefficient. For these units, the social marginal cost is higher than the social marginal benefit. They are privately efficient (benefit is higher than private cost), but once you account for the damage done to the fish, their total cost to society is greater than their benefit. Private actors, by not accounting for the social implications of their actions, create this inefficiency. This is a classic refutation of Adam Smith's "invisible hand" in the presence of market failures.

## Other Types of Externalities

### Negative Consumption Externality

Externalities don't have to be on the production side; we can also have **negative consumption externalities**. This is a case where my consuming a good makes you worse off.

A quick clarification: if I consume a good in a perfectly competitive market, I might raise the demand and thus the price for everyone. Is that an externality? No, because an externality only occurs when you don't bear the cost of your actions. When I decide to buy a car and the price goes up, I also have to pay that higher price.

A better example is **smoking**. When I smoke, it can affect you in several ways.
1.  **Secondhand Smoke:** If I smoke in this classroom, you inhale secondhand smoke and may get ill.
2.  **Healthcare Costs:** If I get sick from smoking, my healthcare costs go up. If we share a health insurance plan (like at MIT), my fellow employees bear some of those costs. When I retire, society bears those costs through Medicare, which is financed by taxation.
3.  **Fires:** Worldwide, an estimated 100,000 people die each year in fires caused by smokers. One person falling asleep with a lit cigarette can cause immense damage.

What about the fact that smokers are less productive at work? Is that an externality? Not necessarily. If they are paid less because they are less productive, then they are bearing the consequences of their actions. It only becomes an externality if their wage doesn't fully adjust and their lower productivity affects the firm's profits or other workers. The key is whether the person causing the effect is compensated for it.

Crucially, the fact that I might kill myself by smoking is **not** an externality. If I sit by myself on a rock in the middle of nowhere and smoke until I die, there is no externality. My privately optimal decision has no effect on anyone else, so it is also socially optimal. Externalities only arise from the effects on others.

### Positive Consumption Externality

There can also be **positive externalities**, where my actions make you better off. Let's say my neighbor has large, unsightly piles of dirt in his yard that I have to look at from my kitchen. Removing them would cost him $1,000. The benefit to him is only $800, so he doesn't do it. However, the benefit to me of him removing them would be $300.

The total social benefit of removing the dirt piles is $800 (his benefit) + $300 (my benefit) = $1,100. This is higher than the social cost of $1,000. From a social perspective, he should do it, but he doesn't because it's not privately optimal. This is a **positive consumption externality**.

### Positive Production Externality

Finally, we have **positive production externalities**. The classic example is **research and development (R&D)** by private firms. When a firm does R&D, it doesn't just create learning for itself; it creates knowledge that might benefit other firms as well (a "spillover").

The best economic estimates suggest that the social returns to a dollar of R&D are two and a half times the private returns. This means that for every dollar of R&D a firm undertakes, society benefits by $2.50 for every $1.00 the firm benefits. As a result, firms under-invest in R&D from a social perspective, leading to too little innovation and economic growth.

A powerful example comes from the race to invent **statins** (cholesterol-lowering drugs) in the 1980s between Merck (US) and Sankyo (Japan). When Sankyo's animal trials ran into a problem (some dogs got sick), they stopped their research. Merck, concerned about the same issue, asked Sankyo for their data, but Sankyo refused, citing competition. Merck stopped its research as well. Five years later, academics discovered the issue with the dogs was unrelated to the drug. Statins were eventually invented and now save hundreds of thousands of lives a year, but they arrived five years later than they could have. Literally, a million people may have died because the two firms could not benefit from the spillovers of each other's R&D knowledge.

## Government Solutions to Externalities

If the private market fails to deliver the optimal outcome, can the government make things better? Private solutions, like me offering to pay my neighbor to remove his dirt piles, often fail. Why?
1.  **Information Asymmetry:** I don't know his true costs, and he doesn't know my true benefits, making negotiation difficult.
2.  **Transaction Costs & Social Norms:** It's often socially awkward or impractical to negotiate these things. Imagine trying to negotiate with every driver on the road to reduce their driving to combat global warming.

Given that private solutions are often unworkable, we turn to government solutions.

### 1. Regulation (Quantity Control)

The government could simply regulate the quantity. For the steel plant, the government could mandate that it produce no more than Q2, the socially optimal quantity. The problem is that this requires the government to know a great deal—it needs to know the full supply and demand curves to calculate the exact optimal quantity.

### 2. Corrective Taxation and Subsidies (Price Control)

A more elegant solution is a **corrective tax** (also known as a **Pigouvian tax**). If the government knows the marginal damage of pollution (e.g., $100 worth of fish killed per unit of steel), it can impose a tax of exactly that amount on the steel plant for each unit produced.

This tax forces the firm to **internalize the externality**. The firm's private marginal cost curve effectively shifts up to become the social marginal cost curve. The firm, now facing the true social cost of its actions, will choose to produce at the socially optimal quantity, Q2. This is our first example of a "good" tax—one that corrects a market failure rather than just distorting the economy.

Similarly, for a positive externality like R&D, the government can offer a **corrective subsidy**. By subsidizing R&D, the government lowers the private cost for the firm, encouraging it to invest more, moving production closer to the socially optimal level.

## Applications in the Real World

### Environmental Externalities: Global Warming

Global warming is a classic, massive negative externality. Every time you drive your car, you contribute to carbon emissions that have global consequences. The natural economic solution is a **carbon tax**—a corrective tax on the use of carbon. In Europe, gasoline taxes are already high, but in the U.S., even small gas tax proposals are politically toxic.

Because price-based solutions are politically difficult, the world has often turned to quantity regulation, such as the **Kyoto Protocol** and the **Paris Agreement**, which attempt to set global caps on carbon emissions. However, getting international agreement and enforcement is incredibly challenging.

### Health Externalities

Many personal behaviors create health externalities.
*   **Drunk Driving:** Causes approximately 13,000 deaths and 400,000 injuries in the U.S. each year.
*   **Obesity:** Leads to costly illnesses like diabetes. Projections suggest one-third of Americans born in 2000 will develop diabetes.

What can the government do?
1.  **Information:** Public information campaigns about the dangers of smoking have been very effective, though more so among the well-educated.
2.  **Taxation:** Cigarette taxes are effective at reducing smoking, especially among youth who are more price-sensitive. Taxing alcohol or unhealthy food is trickier, as most consumption is not harmful, and it raises equity concerns.
3.  **Penalties:** We can impose steep penalties for harmful behavior, like drunk driving. However, enforcement can be imperfect (e.g., inaccurate breathalyzers).
4.  **Illegality:** Making a substance illegal is an extreme form of regulation. While it doesn't eliminate consumption, it significantly reduces it. The debate over legalizing marijuana centers on this trade-off.

These tools—information, taxation, penalties, and regulation—all play a role in addressing the market failures caused by externalities. Understanding which tool to use, and how to design it correctly, is one of the great challenges of public policy.
