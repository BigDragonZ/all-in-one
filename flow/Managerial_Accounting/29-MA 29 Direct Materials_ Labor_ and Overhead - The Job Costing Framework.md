Let's get started. Here we have the November Company, and they use job costing. First, they buy materials on account for $8,000. Then, they use materials for Job 501, 502, and 503. Materials used for the factory are indirect materials. The same thing applies to labor. We have labor used for the first three jobs, and then factory labor, which would be indirect labor. We're going to estimate overhead as 90% of our direct labor cost. We also have some actual overhead coming in. We finished all three jobs and sold Job 501 and 503 for a specific amount. The problem we need to solve is to complete a job cost card for each job.

For **Job 501**, we would have materials, labor, and overhead, which gives us a total cost for that job. The job could be finished, still in work in process, or sold. We do the same math for all jobs. Let's start with Job 501. The material cost for Job 501 is $1,800. Our direct labor is $3,500. Remember, we're estimating overhead as 90% of our direct labor cost. So, we'll take $3,500 times 90% (0.9), which gives us $3,150. The total cost for Job 501 is the sum of these three, which is $8,450.

Now for **Job 502**. We keep up with everything by job cost number. The direct materials are $1,700. The direct labor for Job 502 is $2,800. We then take $2,800 times 90% to get our overhead, which is $2,520. The total for these three is $7,020.

Next, we need the materials and labor for **Job 503** to finish the job cost card. Job 503 has $1,500 of materials and the labor is $2,900. Then, $2,900 times 90% is $2,610 for overhead. The total cost is $7,010. Now we have the cost for all three jobs: Job 501, Job 502, and Job 503. We know the cost of each job.

We finished all three jobs, but we only sold two: **Job 501** and **Job 503**. Now, let's calculate our gross profit. Gross profit is sales minus cost of goods sold. Our total sales are $11,200 plus $9,500, which equals $20,700. The cost of goods sold is the cost of the jobs we sold, which are Job 501 and Job 503. We need the cost of Job 501, which is $8,450, plus the cost of Job 503, which is $7,010. The total cost of goods sold is $15,460. So, $20,700 in sales minus $15,460 in cost of goods sold gives us a gross profit of $5,240.

This is how you do job costing. We keep up with the cost by different jobs, and each job has a different cost. Once we finish the jobs, we know the cost of goods manufactured. Once we sell them, we know the cost of goods sold and our gross profit. This is how you handle job costing and profit in this chapter. Thanks for watching. Please like, comment, and subscribe.
