Let me give you a little bit of teaching, and then we'll work a problem. On the statement of cash flows, we have three main types of activities.

First, **operating activities**, which include everything involved in net income. This means anything on the income statement, any changes in current assets or current liabilities, and any gains or losses.

Second, **investing activities**. This is anytime we buy or sell non-current assets, regardless of whether it's a purchase or a sale. Non-current assets include land, buildings, equipment, machines, intangible assets, or investments. All transactions involving these are investing activities.

Third, **financing activities**. This is how we finance our operations. We either borrow or pay back long-term debt (long-term liabilities), or we deal with equity, such as issuing stock or paying dividends.

Some transactions might seem to involve two categories. For example, what if we buy assets by issuing stock, or buy assets by issuing debt? In these cases, no cash changes hands. This would be a **non-cash investing and financing activity**. It's "investing" because you're buying a long-term asset like a building, and "financing" because you're issuing stock or borrowing money. These transactions don't appear in the main body of the statement but are disclosed in a footnote.

Now, let's get to our problem and classify some cash flows. I'm in Chapter 13, which covers the statement of cash flows in managerial accounting.

- **Depreciation expense**: This goes on the income statement, so it is an **operating activity**.
- **Cash dividends**: This is related to equity, so it is a **financing activity**.
- **Pay off long-term bonds payable**: This is paying back long-term debt, so it is a **financing activity**.
- **Prepaid rent**: This is a current asset, so any change is an **operating activity**.
- **Accounts receivable**: This is a current asset, so any change is an **operating activity**.
- **Purchase land by issuing common stock**: The purchase of land is investing, and issuing stock is financing. Since no cash is involved, this is a **non-cash investing and financing activity**, typically disclosed in a footnote.
- **Increase in inventory**: Inventory is a current asset, making this an **operating activity**.
- **Issuing stock**: This is a **financing activity**.
- **Sale of equipment**: Equipment is a non-current (long-term) asset, so selling it is an **investing activity**.
- **Gain on sale of equipment**: While the sale itself is an investing activity, the resulting gain or loss is reported under **operating activities**.
- **Issuing bonds**: This is borrowing money long-term, so it's a **financing activity**.
- **Increase in accounts payable**: Accounts payable is a current liability, so this is an **operating activity**.
