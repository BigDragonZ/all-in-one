We have two separate problems with missing information. We know that direct materials, direct labor, and overhead (which can be called manufacturing overhead or factory overhead) add up to equal our total product cost. Then, we add the beginning work in process inventory, which includes items we've started but haven't finished. This gives us the total cost of work in process. Finally, if we subtract the ending work in process inventory, we get the cost of goods manufactured. This entire process is detailed in the schedule of cost of goods manufactured.

Another way to think about it is to use a T-account for the Work in Process inventory. The beginning balance, direct materials, direct labor, and overhead are all debits. These four items represent the total work in process available. We subtract what we didn't finish (the ending work in process, a credit) to find the cost of goods manufactured, which is transferred out.

Let's work through the problems using the schedule format, as the subtotals are built-in, making the relationships clear.

## Problem 1: Juliet

First, we need to find the direct materials. We know direct labor is $87,400 and manufacturing overhead (or factory overhead, FOH) is $113,000. The problem states the total product cost is $302,500. It's important to note that "total product cost" is the same as "total manufacturing cost"—they are used interchangeably and consist of materials, labor, and overhead. To find the direct materials used, we take the total product cost and subtract the other two costs: $302,500 - $87,400 - $113,000 = $102,100. So, direct materials used must be $102,100.

Next, we're given that the total work in process is $344,700 and the cost of goods manufactured is $287,300. The total manufacturing cost ($302,500) plus the beginning work in process equals the total work in process ($344,700). To find the beginning work in process, we work backward: $344,700 - $302,500 = $42,200.

Finally, the total work in process ($344,700) minus the ending work in process equals the cost of goods manufactured ($287,300). To find the ending work in process, we simply subtract: $344,700 - $287,300 = $57,400.

## Problem 2: Kilo

For the Kilo problem, let's plug in the numbers we know. Direct materials used are $12,600, and overhead is $6,700. The total product cost (or total manufacturing cost) is $26,400. The beginning work in process is $7,200, and the ending work in process is $4,400. To solve these types of problems, you must know the format. You can't simply look at the numbers; you have to plug them into the schedule and solve for what you don't know.

First, let's find the direct labor used. The total manufacturing cost ($26,400) is the sum of direct materials, direct labor, and overhead. So, we calculate: $26,400 - $12,600 (materials) - $6,700 (overhead) = $7,100. The direct labor used is $7,100.

Next, we find the total work in process. This is the total manufacturing cost plus the beginning work in process: $26,400 + $7,200 = $33,600.

Finally, to get the cost of goods manufactured, we take the total work in process and subtract the ending work in process: $33,600 - $4,400 = $29,200.
