# MA 52 Variable Overhead Rate vs Efficiency Variance

Hello and welcome. Today we're working in managerial accounting on the standard costing chapter, specifically on a problem involving variable overhead variances. My name is Jeff from Finally Learn. I teach accounting, finance, and Excel. Please like, comment, and subscribe. Our previous problems covered direct material and direct labor variances. Now we're moving to variable overhead variances. Remember, overhead has a fixed component and a variable component; this problem focuses on the variable part.

Let's look at the problem. The estimated standard cost is five hours to make each product, and each hour is expected to cost $18. We produced 3,000 units. This is where the concept of flexible budgeting comes in. We have a standard for one unit (our standard cost card), and we flex or stretch that budget for the actual production level of 3,000 units. Our actual results were: we used 15,400 hours (of direct labor or machine hours), and we spent $19 per hour, compared to the standard of $18.

We need to figure out the rate variance and the efficiency variance, and then the total variable manufacturing overhead (MO) variance. The format we'll use involves three calculations. There are two different ways to do this type of problem, but I use a template so I don't have to remember the formulas.

1.  **Actual Quantity (AQ) × Actual Price (AP)**
2.  **Actual Quantity (AQ) × Standard Price (SP)**
3.  **Standard Quantity (SQ) × Standard Price (SP)**

The difference between the first and second columns gives us the **Variable Rate Variance**. The difference between the second and third columns gives us the quantity variance, which we call the **Variable Efficiency Variance**. I remember the middle column with the mnemonic "ASP" (Actual quantity, Standard Price).

Let's start from left to right. The actual quantity of hours is 15,400, and the actual price is $19. Multiplying 15,400 by $19 gives us a total actual cost of **$292,600**. For the middle column, we use the actual quantity of 15,400 hours but multiply it by the standard price of $18 per hour. This gives us **$277,200**. The difference between $292,600 and $277,200 is **$15,400**. Since we spent $19 per hour when our standard was $18, this is an **unfavorable** rate variance.

Now for the efficiency variance. We first need to calculate the standard quantity of hours allowed for the actual output. We produced 3,000 units, and the standard is 5 hours per unit, so the standard quantity is **15,000 hours** (3,000 units × 5 hours/unit). We multiply this standard quantity by the standard price of $18 per hour, which equals **$270,000**. The difference between the middle column ($277,200) and this column ($270,000) is **$7,200**. We thought we could do the work in 15,000 hours, but it actually took 15,400 hours. We were less efficient. Therefore, this is also an **unfavorable** efficiency variance.

To find the total variable MO variance, we can either sum the individual variances or take the difference between the far-left and far-right totals. Since both the rate variance ($15,400) and the efficiency variance ($7,200) are unfavorable, we add them together to get a total unfavorable variance of **$22,600**. This total variance explains the difference between our total actual cost ($292,600) and our total standard cost ($270,000). We spent $22,600 more than planned because our rate per hour was higher and we were less efficient with our time.

That's how you calculate the variable MO rate and efficiency variances. Thanks for watching. We'll see you on the next video.
