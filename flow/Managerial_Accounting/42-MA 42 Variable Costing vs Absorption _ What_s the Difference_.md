Let's say we have the Ace Company, and it produced 2,000 units. We have direct materials and direct labor, which are always variable costs. Then, overhead is broken down into variable cost and fixed cost. We also have our general and administrative expenses, which we could call **SG&A** (selling, general, and administrative expenses). These are operating expenses. Remember, product costs are made up of direct materials, direct labor, and **MOH** (Manufacturing Overhead). General and administrative expenses are **period costs**, not **product costs**.

So, how do we calculate the product cost per unit under **variable costing**? It's very simple. We only use the variable product costs. We take the direct materials of $5, the direct labor of $7, and the variable overhead of $10. We exclude general and administrative expenses because they are not product costs. We also exclude the fixed overhead. In this method, the allocation for fixed MOH is zero. The total product cost under variable costing is **$22**.

Now, for **absorption costing**, we start with the same three variable costs and add the fixed overhead per unit. So, we still have the $5 for direct materials, $7 for direct labor, and $10 for variable overhead. For the fixed overhead, we take the total fixed manufacturing overhead of $15,000 and divide it by the 2,000 units produced, which gives us $7.50 per unit. Therefore, our total product cost under absorption costing is **$29.50**.

To summarize, the product cost per unit under variable costing is $22, and under absorption costing, it's $29.50. The difference is in how we account for fixed overhead. Absorption costing includes the fixed overhead in the product cost, while variable costing excludes it, treating it as a period expense rather than rolling it into the product cost.
