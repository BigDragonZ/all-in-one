Hello and welcome. Today we're in the managerial accounting cost-volume-profit chapter, and we're going to use the **high-low method** to figure out how much of our costs are variable and how much are fixed. If you're new, my name is Jeff from Finally Learn, and I teach a lot of accounting.

---

Let's start with a small problem to ensure we all understand what **variable costs** and **fixed costs** are. Here we have a company with different levels of volume or units. Cost A goes from zero to 700, to 1,400, to 2,100. You can see it varies directly with the number of units we produce, so this must be a **variable cost**. This is the definition of a variable cost: a total cost that increases as volume increases and decreases as volume decreases.

What about cost B? Cost B is the same at each volume level, so that's the definition of a **fixed cost**.

What about cost C, where you start with some number and then it goes up every time? This looks like it has a fixed component and a variable component, so this is the definition of a **mixed cost**.

---

Now, let's work on a cost estimation problem. Let's say we have a company with the following costs for several months, from April through December. We have the number of units and the corresponding cost. Using the **high-low method**, we're going to answer several questions:
1. What is the estimated variable cost per unit?
2. What is the estimated fixed cost?
3. What is the cost formula?
4. What is the estimated cost if we produce 180 units?
5. If our total costs are $8,000, what is our estimated number of units?

---

Here's how we do the **high-low method**. Step one is to find the high and the low number of units, not the cost. Looking at the data, our lowest number of units is 100, with a corresponding cost of $5,600. You might be tempted to pick the lowest cost, which is $5,100, but we must base our selection on the activity level (units). The highest number of units is 180. So, we will take the high and low unit values and their related costs.

- **High Point**: 180 units, $7,200 cost
- **Low Point**: 100 units, $5,600 cost

If you pick two different data points, you'll get a completely different answer.

---

Next, we'll estimate our variable cost. We take the difference in cost and divide it by the difference in units.

The difference in cost is $7,200 - $5,600, which equals $1,600.
The difference in units is 180 - 100, which equals 80 units.

An 80-unit increase in production caused our costs to go up by $1,600. To find the variable cost per unit, we'll take $1,600 divided by 80, which gives us $20 per unit. So, our estimated variable cost per unit is $20.

---

Now, what's our estimated fixed cost? We know that total cost is made up of a fixed component and a variable component. We can calculate the fixed cost using either the high or the low point.

**Using the High Point:**
- Total Cost at 180 units = $7,200
- Variable Cost = 180 units * $20/unit = $3,600
- Fixed Cost = Total Cost - Variable Cost = $7,200 - $3,600 = $3,600

**Using the Low Point:**
- Total Cost at 100 units = $5,600
- Variable Cost = 100 units * $20/unit = $2,000
- Fixed Cost = Total Cost - Variable Cost = $5,600 - $2,000 = $3,600

In both cases, it gives us the same total fixed cost of $3,600.

---

So, what's our cost formula? Let's think of it in terms of the equation of a line. The cost formula is `Y = A + BX`, which is equivalent to `Y = MX + B`.

- `Y` is the total cost.
- `A` (or `B` in the second formula) is our total fixed cost, the Y-intercept.
- `B` (or `M` in the second formula) is our variable cost per unit, the slope of the line.
- `X` is the number of units (the activity level).

In statistics, X is called the independent variable, and Y is the dependent variable. Plugging in our numbers, the cost formula is:
`Y = 3,600 + 20X`

This means our cost starts at a fixed base of $3,600 and increases by $20 for every unit we produce.

---

Now we can use this formula to make predictions. What if we produce 180 units?
`Y = 3,600 + 20 * 180`
`Y = 3,600 + 3,600`
`Y = 7,200`
The total cost is $7,200, which matches our high point data. What if we produced 200 units? The cost would be `3,600 + 20 * 200`, which equals $7,600.

What if our total costs are $8,000? How many units is that? We need to work backwards with algebra, solving for X.
`8,000 = 3,600 + 20X`
First, subtract the fixed cost:
`8,000 - 3,600 = 20X`
`4,400 = 20X`
Now, divide by the variable cost per unit:
`X = 4,400 / 20`
`X = 220`
So, for a total cost of $8,000, we estimate that we produced 220 units.

---

Let's review the questions we've answered:
- **Estimated variable cost per unit:** $20
- **Estimated total fixed cost:** $3,600
- **Cost formula:** `Y = 3,600 + 20X`
- **Cost for 180 units:** $7,200
- **Units for a total cost of $8,000:** 220 units

This is how you use the **high-low method**. Remember, you find the high and low units, not the high and low costs. Then, you estimate the variable cost and use that to find the fixed cost. Thanks for watching. We'll see you in the next video.
