We're going to use the format that we introduced in direct materials. It's the same format, except it asks us for the **labor rate** and **efficiency variances**, not the price and quantity variances. Here's a little secret: when we talk about materials, we talk about price and quantity. When we talk about labor and overhead, instead of calling it a price variance, we call it a **rate variance**. It's the same thing. The quantity variance, which is a quantity of hours, is called the **efficiency variance**. This measures how efficient we are. If you think something should take three hours and you did it in two hours, you're more efficient. So, price and quantity now magically become rate and efficiency.

Let's set up the problem. We'll start with the actual quantity times the actual price to get the actual cost. Remember, it's price times quantity equals cost. Our actual quantity of hours is 12,600, and our actual price (or rate) is $24.60 per hour. Let's multiply this out: 12,600 hours times $24.60 gives us $309,960.

Next, we calculate the actual quantity times the standard price. The actual quantity of hours is still 12,600, but we use the standard price, which is given as $24. This column shows what we would have paid for the actual hours at the standard rate. So, 12,600 hours times $24 equals $302,400.

The last column is the standard quantity times the standard price. We need to figure out the standard quantity allowed for the actual production. The standard is 3 hours of direct labor per unit. Since we produced 4,000 units, the standard quantity is 4,000 units times 3 hours per unit, which equals 12,000 hours. The standard price per hour is $24. So, 12,000 hours times $24 equals $288,000.

Our standard cost for 4,000 units was $288,000, but we actually spent $309,960. This difference is due to the rate variance and the efficiency variance.

The difference between the first two columns is the **rate variance**. The only difference between these columns is the price (the rate). We thought we would pay $24 per hour, but we actually paid $24.60 per hour. This is **unfavorable**. The variance is the difference between $309,960 and $302,400, which is $7,560 **unfavorable**.

The difference between the middle and last columns is the **efficiency variance**. This is about the hours we used. We thought we could do the job in 12,000 hours, but it took 12,600 hours. We were less efficient than planned, so this is also **unfavorable**. The variance is the difference between $302,400 and $288,000, which is $14,400 **unfavorable**.

The total labor variance is the sum of these two variances. $7,560 unfavorable plus $14,400 unfavorable gives a total variance of $21,960 **unfavorable**. We can also calculate this as the difference between the total actual cost ($309,960) and the total standard cost ($288,000). We spent $21,960 more than we budgeted, which is unfavorable.
