In contrast, managerial accounting is private information used by managers and owners. It includes both current and future information, such as budgets and reports for internal decisions. For example, we want to know how much our product costs individually, but we don't want our competitors to know this.

In managerial accounting, we think about creating products through three stages of inventory. We start with direct materials or raw materials. As we start working on them, they become work in process (or work in progress), which means they are started but not finished. When we finish everything, we call them finished goods. I abbreviate these as DM for direct materials, WIP for work in process, and FG for finished goods. Finished goods are items that are ready to sell. For example, if we're making wooden furniture, we start with lumber (direct materials), then we start working on it (work in process), and when we finish, it's ready to sell (finished goods).

To build every product, there are three product costs, also known as manufacturing costs. These are direct materials, direct labor, and overhead. Materials are everything that goes into building the product. Labor is the cost of all employees who work on the product. Overhead is everything else. It includes factory costs like the depreciation on equipment, utilities, taxes, and any supervisors (which would be indirect labor). Smaller items like glue would be considered indirect materials. This overhead can be called MOH for manufacturing overhead or FOH for factory overhead; you should follow the format your book uses.

We will also build the cost of goods sold and introduce a new term: cost of goods manufactured. Let me show you a chart that illustrates the flow of manufacturing costs. We have three product costs (materials, labor, and overhead) and three inventory accounts (materials, work in process, and finished goods).

This diagram may look like spaghetti at first, but the basic idea is to follow the arrows, which represent journal entries. Everything starts with materials. If it's an indirect material, it goes to overhead. For example, in making wooden furniture, the wood is a direct material. The glue is an indirect material because you don't track the cost of each squirt; you just track how much glue you use over a period. So, indirect materials go to overhead.

Next, we have labor. The people who actually work on the product represent direct labor. Managerial, supervisory, maintenance, or janitorial staff in the factory represent indirect labor, and their wages go into overhead. The overhead account accumulates all the indirect costs: indirect materials, indirect labor, depreciation, utilities, taxes, and so on. This is the actual overhead. We then apply an estimated amount of overhead to our products.

To build our product, we use the work in process account. We start with a beginning work in process balance and add direct materials, direct labor, and applied overhead. What we finish is called the cost of goods manufactured, and this amount is transferred into the finished goods account. So, the three levels of inventory are raw materials, work in process (started but not finished), and finished goods.

In the finished goods account, we have a beginning balance, we add the cost of goods manufactured, and we have an ending balance. The cost of the items we sell becomes cost of goods sold. This is the amount that shows up on the income statement as an expense. Cost of goods manufactured is the total cost of products completed during the period, which includes materials, labor, and overhead. A product cost only becomes an expense when the product is sold.

To summarize the flow: product costs (materials, labor, overhead) increase the work in process inventory. When the product is finished, its cost moves to finished goods.
