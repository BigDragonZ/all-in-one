Let's get started with this problem. Kylo Company is considering investing in a long-term project that will cost $110,000. They require a 10% return and have a projected income statement for the additional revenue. Remember, for capital budgeting, we need the project's cash flows, not just the net income.

The first step is to calculate the cash flows. Net income is not the same as cash flow. In year zero, the cash flow is the initial investment, a negative $110,000. Next, we need to calculate the annual operating cash flow. Since the income statement is the same each year, the cash flow will also be the same. The formula is: Net Income + Depreciation (a non-cash expense). This gives us an operating cash flow of $31,500. Each of the five years will have a cash inflow of $31,500. Now that we have the cash flows, we can calculate the NPV, IRR, and PI. It's crucial to work with cash flows, not the income statement directly.

I've set this up in Excel to calculate the net present value. Using Excel's NPV function is very easy. The function requires the rate, which is 10%, and the values, which are the cash flows from year 1 through 5. The NPV function in Excel calculates the present value of the cash flows from year 1 onward. To get the *net* present value, we must then subtract the initial investment from year zero. So, we take the result from the NPV function and add the negative $110,000 investment. Our net present value is **$9,409**. Since the NPV is positive, we would accept this project.

Next is the internal rate of return (IRR). Because the NPV is positive, we know the IRR will be greater than our required rate of 10%. The IRR formula in Excel is even simpler. You just select all the cash flows, including the initial negative investment from year zero. The calculated IRR is **13.29%**. Since 13.29% is greater than the required 10%, this also indicates we should accept the project.

Finally, let's look at the profitability index (PI). The profitability index is the present value of the future cash inflows divided by the initial investment. To calculate this, we first need the present value of the future cash inflows (years 1-5). We can use the same Excel NPV function as before, using the 10% rate and the five annual cash flows. This gives a present value of future cash flows of $119,410. Our initial investment is $110,000. To find the PI, we divide the present value of the inflows ($119,410) by the initial investment ($110,000). The result is a profitability index of **1.09** (rounded to two decimals).

We interpret this as: for every $1 invested, the project returns $1.09 in present value terms. If the NPV is positive, the PI will be greater than 1. If the NPV is negative, the PI will be less than 1.

Let's see what happens if our required rate of return was 15% instead of 10%. With a 15% required return, the NPV becomes negative. The IRR of 13.29% is now less than our required rate. The profitability index drops to 0.96, meaning we only get back 96 cents for every dollar invested. In this scenario, we would reject the project.

The profitability index gives the same accept/reject signal as net present value, but it frames the result in terms of the return per dollar invested, which can be useful for comparing projects of different sizes.
