Let's get started. I need to do a little teaching before we work on a problem. The indirect method is only for operating activities. Here's what we basically have: **Net Income** plus or minus some adjustments. So, what are those adjustments? We start with net income, and we're always going to add back **depreciation**. Depreciation is a subtraction on the income statement. Since we start with net income, which already includes a negative number for depreciation expense, we're going to add it back to get rid of it because depreciation is a non-cash expense. It's already a subtraction, so we have to add it to reverse its effect.

The other thing we need to add is **losses**. Losses are not a cash increase or decrease. Since we start with net income, losses are already built in as a reduction. We take them out by adding the loss back. Next, we look at current assets. Anytime we have a **decrease in current assets**, that increases cash. We're going to make a simple rule here: assets and cash are opposites. If assets go up, cash goes down. If assets go down, cash goes up. Finally, an **increase in current liabilities** means an increase in cash. If you borrow money, your liability goes up and your cash goes up. So, liabilities and cash move in the same direction.

What do we subtract? We subtract out any **gains** because gains are not cash; the actual cash receipt is handled elsewhere (usually in investing activities). We also subtract an **increase in current assets** because an increase in an asset means cash goes down. Lastly, we subtract a **decrease in current liabilities**. What does it take to decrease a liability? It takes a cash payment, so cash goes down. To summarize the rules: assets and cash move in the opposite direction, while liabilities and cash move in the same direction.

All right, let's work this first problem. Any Company has the following information. Prepare the operating activities section of the statement of cash flows using the indirect method. We're given an income statement with sales, cost of goods sold, wages expense, and depreciation expense, resulting in a net income of $32,000. We also have additional information: accounts receivable increased, inventory decreased, and wages payable increased. Our job is to calculate the cash flow from operating activities.

We're going to start with the **net income** of $32,000. The first thing we always do is adjust for depreciation. The $20,000 of depreciation made net income go down, so we're going to add the $20,000 back. That will increase our cash flow from operating activities.

Now, what about the other items? **Accounts receivable** went up. Accounts receivable is an asset. If an asset goes up, cash goes down. This is because an increase in receivables means we made sales but did not collect the cash. Therefore, the $9,000 increase will be a negative adjustment. Next is the **decrease in inventory**. If inventory, an asset, goes down, that means our cash goes up, so we'll add the $17,000. Finally, we have an **increase in wages payable**. An increase in a liability is like borrowing money; we incurred the wage expense but haven't paid it in cash yet. So, this is a positive adjustment of $3,000.

So, we have $32,000 + $20,000 - $9,000 + $17,000 + $3,000. This adds up to equal $63,000. This is our **cash flows from operating activities**.
