Let's get started with this problem. We have a furniture manufacturer that makes desks, tables, and chairs. They have the following costs. We're going to classify each as either a **product cost** or a **period cost**. If it's a product cost, it will be either **direct materials (DM)**, **direct labor (DL)**, or **factory overhead (FO)**. I'll use FO for factory overhead, though you might also see it as MO for manufacturing overhead. Period costs would be something like general administrative expenses or operating expenses.

Let's look at each item.
- **Factory utilities**: If it says "factory", that's a clue it's a **product cost**. Factory utilities are part of **factory overhead (FO)**.
- **Advertising**: This is a cost of selling our product, which is a selling expense. Therefore, it's a **period cost**.
- **Depreciation on factory equipment**: Since it's for the factory, it's a product cost. It's not materials or direct labor, so it must be a **factory overhead (FO)** cost. Depreciation on factory assets is a classic overhead cost.
- **Factory supplies**: These are also considered **overhead (FO)**. They are part of the cost of running the factory but are not significant enough to be traced as direct materials.

Now for a few more examples.
- **Office supplies**: The word "office" clearly implies it's a general and administrative expense, making it a **period cost**.
- **Depreciation on office equipment**: Unlike the factory equipment which was overhead, this is for the office. Therefore, it is a **period cost**.
- **Sales department salaries or commissions**: These are also a **period cost**.

Let's analyze some costs directly related to production.
- **Wood for furniture**: Since we're making wood furniture, this is a primary component and is classified as **direct materials (DM)**.
- **Glue for furniture**: Glue is considered an indirect material. By definition, indirect materials are part of **factory overhead (FO)**.
- **Wages for factory workers**: Wages for workers who are actually building the product are classified as **direct labor (DL)**.

Finally, let's look at salaries for management.
- **Salaries for factory managers**: This is not direct labor; it's considered indirect labor. Therefore, it is part of **factory overhead (FO)**.
- **Salary for the CFO**: This is not direct labor or overhead. The salary for an executive is a general and administrative expense, which is a **period cost** because it doesn't relate to building the product.

This is how we classify costs as either product or period. Remember, if it's a product cost, it falls into one of the three big categories: materials, labor, or overhead. I hope this was helpful. Thanks for watching, and we'll see you in the next video.
