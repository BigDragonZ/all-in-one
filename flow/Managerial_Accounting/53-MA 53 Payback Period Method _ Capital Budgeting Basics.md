The DC company is considering investing in a long-term project and can pick either the Alpha or the Suma project. Capital budgeting involves investing in a project, such as buying machinery, equipment, or funding a marketing campaign, with the expectation of receiving benefits over several years. The Alpha project costs $160,000 in year zero, and its benefits decrease over time. The Suma project costs $240,000, and its benefits slowly rise over six years. Our task is to calculate the payback period for both projects.

Let's start with the Alpha project. The cash outflow in year zero is a negative $160,000. The payback period calculation determines how long it takes to recover that $160,000. To do this, we'll create a column for cumulative cash flows. Year zero starts at negative $160,000. After year one's inflow of $60,000, the cumulative balance is negative $100,000. After year two's $50,000, it becomes negative $50,000. After year three's $40,000, the cumulative deficit is down to $10,000.

To reach a zero cumulative cash flow, we need to recover this final $10,000 from the year four cash inflow of $30,000. We assume the cash flow occurs evenly throughout the year. Therefore, we need $10,000 divided by $30,000, which is 0.33 of the year. The total payback period is three full years plus a third of the fourth year. So, the payback period for the Alpha project is 3.33 years.

Now, let's look at the Suma project. The initial investment is $240,000, with cash inflows over six years. We will perform the same cumulative calculation. We start with negative $240,000. After adding the inflows year by year:
- After year one ($40,000), we're at -$200,000.
- After year two ($50,000), we're at -$150,000.
- After year three ($60,000), we're at -$90,000.
- After year four ($70,000), we have a remaining deficit of $20,000.

To get to zero, we need to recover this $20,000 from the year five cash inflow of $80,000. This requires $20,000 divided by $80,000, which is 0.25, or a quarter of the year. So, we have four full years plus a quarter of the fifth year. The payback period for the Suma project is 4.25 years.

A shorter payback period is generally preferred. If all other factors were equal, we would prefer the Alpha project's 3.33 years over the Suma project's 4.25 years. We will use this same cash flow data to calculate net present value and internal rate of return to determine which is truly the better investment.

The payback period is easy to calculate and understand. For example, you get your money back in 3.3 years. However, the method has significant drawbacks. First, it does not consider the time value of money. Second, it ignores all cash flows that occur after the payback period is reached. For the Alpha project, it ignores the remaining cash flow in year four, as well as all cash flows in years five and six. For the Suma project, it ignores most of the cash flow in year five and all of the cash flow in year six.

In summary, while the payback period is simple to calculate and understand, it is not a preferred method for making investment decisions due to its limitations. It is not as robust as other methods like Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR).
