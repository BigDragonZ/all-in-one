We'll also determine which items are most responsible for the increase in assets and liabilities. Our assets include current assets like cash, accounts receivable, inventory, and prepaid expenses. We also have long-term assets, specifically plant assets (net), which means accumulated depreciation has already been subtracted.

The key to common size analysis is that it can be hard to compare two balance sheets with different total asset values. For example, one year has total assets of $396,000, while the next has $452,500. To normalize this, common size analysis sets **total assets as the base, equal to 100%**. Every other item on the balance sheet is then expressed as a percentage of total assets.

Let me show you how to do this in Excel, which makes it much easier than doing it by hand. The core calculation is to take each line item and divide it by the total assets for that year. For the 2030 column, every item will be divided by $452,500. For the 2029 column, every item will be divided by $396,000. For example, for cash in 2030, the formula is `$24,000 / $452,500`. In Excel, to copy this formula down the column, we need to make the reference to total assets an absolute reference. You can do this by pressing F4, which adds dollar signs (e.g., `$D$14`). This locks the denominator, so when you drag the formula, it always divides by the total assets.

After applying the formula to all items, we can see the results. With total assets at 100%, we find that in 2030, cash is 5.3% of total assets, accounts receivable is 20.3%, and total current assets are 44.8%. Because a balance sheet must balance, total liabilities and owner's equity also sum to 100%. Each liability and equity item is also shown as a percentage of that total. For instance, in 2030, accounts payable makes up 25.8% of total liabilities and equity.

Now we can answer our original question: what is most responsible for the increase in total assets? By comparing the percentages from 2029 to 2030, we can see that cash as a percentage of assets actually decreased (from 9.4% to 5.3%). However, **accounts receivable** grew significantly, from 13.3% to 20.3% of total assets—an increase of 7 percentage points. This makes it the primary driver of the asset growth. On the liabilities side, what caused the increase? Long-term notes payable stayed relatively stable as a percentage, even decreasing slightly. The main driver was **accounts payable**, which increased as a percentage of total liabilities and equity.

To summarize: for a common size income statement, revenue or sales are set to 100%. For a common size balance sheet, **total assets** (and therefore total liabilities and equity) are set to 100%. This is how you perform a common size analysis on a balance sheet.
