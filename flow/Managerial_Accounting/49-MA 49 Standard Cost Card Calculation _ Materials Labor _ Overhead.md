Let's look at this problem for the Drew Company. We have the estimated standard costs to make one unit. Direct materials require 3 pounds of material at a cost of $10 per pound. Direct labor requires 2.5 hours at a rate of $22 per hour. Our variable overhead is based on direct labor hours, so it also uses 2.5 hours, and the rate is $32 per hour. I've set this up so we can do two things: first, create a standard cost card for one item, and second, calculate the total standard cost for a budgeted production of 8,000 units.

First, let's create the standard cost card for one unit. For materials, the quantity is 3 pounds and the standard price is $10 per pound. For labor, the quantity is 2.5 direct labor hours, and the standard price is $22 per hour. For variable overhead, since it's based on direct labor hours, the quantity is also 2.5 hours, and the standard price is $32 per hour.

To find the standard cost for each component, we multiply the quantity by the price.
- For materials: 3 pounds * $10/pound = **$30**. This means to make one finished item, it takes $30 of materials.
- For labor: 2.5 hours * $22/hour = **$55**. This is the labor cost that goes into our product.
- For variable overhead: 2.5 hours * $32/hour = **$80**. This is the overhead cost allocated to the product.

Adding these three costs together, the total standard cost to make one item is $30 + $55 + $80 = **$165**. A standard cost card is simply the standard quantity multiplied by the standard price to get the standard cost per unit.

Now, what if we plan to manufacture 8,000 units? We can use our standard cost card to determine the total standard cost for this production level. This allows us to compare our standard (or budgeted) cost with our actual cost to identify variances.
- **Materials**: 8,000 units * 3 pounds/unit = **24,000 pounds** of materials needed.
- **Labor**: 8,000 units * 2.5 hours/unit = **20,000 direct labor hours**.
- **Variable Overhead**: Since it's based on direct labor hours, it's also **20,000 hours**.

Next, we multiply these total quantities by their standard prices to get the total standard cost for the 8,000-unit production run.
- **Total Materials Cost**: 24,000 pounds * $10/pound = **$240,000**.
- **Total Labor Cost**: 20,000 hours * $22/hour = **$440,000**.
- **Total Variable Overhead Cost**: 20,000 hours * $32/hour = **$640,000**.

The total standard cost to make 8,000 units is the sum of these three costs: $240,000 + $440,000 + $640,000 = **$1,320,000**. Alternatively, you can multiply the cost per unit by the number of units: $165/unit * 8,000 units = $1,320,000.

The purpose of this chapter is to figure out our standard cost, compare it to our actual cost, and analyze the differences, or variances. For example, if our actual costs were $1,300,000, we would have a variance. Some differences are good (favorable), and some are bad (unfavorable), meaning we spent more money than we should have.
