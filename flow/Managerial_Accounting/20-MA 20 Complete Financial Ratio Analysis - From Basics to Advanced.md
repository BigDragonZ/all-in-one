### 盈利能力比率 (Profitability Ratios)

**Gross Profit Margin** is calculated by dividing gross profit by sales. Our gross profit is 148,000 and our net sales are 425,000. This gives us a gross profit margin of **34.81%**. This means for every dollar in sales, we generate about 34.8 cents in gross profit. It measures how effectively we are managing our cost of goods sold.

**Operating Margin** is EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) divided by sales. EBIT is the same as operating income. We take our operating income of 58,700 and divide it by sales of 425,000. Our operating margin is **13.82%**. This means for every dollar in sales, after paying for cost of goods sold and operating expenses, we have about 13.8 cents left.

**Net Profit Margin**, also known as Return on Sales (ROS), is net income divided by sales. Our net income is 36,200, and sales are 425,000. The result is **8.52%**. For every dollar in sales, we get to keep about 8.5 cents. This shows our overall ability to control costs and generate profit.

**Return on Assets (ROA)** measures how profitable we are based on the assets we have. It's net income divided by total assets. Our net income is 36,200 and our total assets are 466,500. This gives an ROA of **7.76%**. For the assets we've invested, we generate a 7.76% return. This is sometimes called Return on Investment (ROI).

**Return on Equity (ROE)** measures profitability based on the owners' equity. Because we have debt, equity is smaller than assets, so ROE is typically a larger number than ROA. We divide net income (36,200) by stockholders' equity (182,400). Our ROE is **19.85%**. For every dollar of equity the owners have put in, we generate an almost 20% return, which is very good.

There's another way to think about ROE called the **DuPont equation**, which breaks it down into Return on Sales, Total Asset Turnover, and the Equity Multiplier. We'll come back to this after we've calculated the other ratios.

### 经营活动比率 (Activity Ratios)

Activity ratios measure how efficiently we are using our assets.

**Inventory Turnover** shows how often we sell our inventory during a period. It's calculated as cost of goods sold divided by inventory. Our cost of goods sold is 277,000 and our inventory is 72,000. This means we turned over our inventory **3.85 times** during the year.

**Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)** is related to inventory turnover. We take 365 days and divide by the inventory turnover (3.85). This tells us it takes about **94.8 days** to sell our inventory. We would prefer a higher turnover and a lower number of days.

**Receivables Turnover** measures how often we collect our receivables. It's sales divided by accounts receivable. Our sales are 425,600 and our accounts receivable are 79,600. Our receivables turnover is **5.35 times** per year.

**Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** tells us the average number of days it takes to collect our receivables. We take 365 days and divide by the receivables turnover (5.35). It takes us about **68.2 days** to collect. This is likely too high; a company wants to collect its cash faster, so a lower DSO and higher receivables turnover are desirable.

**Total Asset Turnover** measures how effectively we use our assets to generate sales. It's sales divided by total assets. Our sales are 425,000 and total assets are 466,500. The result is **0.91**. This means for every dollar in assets, we're generating 91 cents in sales. To know if this is good, we need to compare it to previous years or industry averages.

### 流动性比率 (Liquidity Ratios)

Liquidity measures our short-term debt-paying ability.

**Net Working Capital** is not a ratio, but a commonly calculated number: current assets minus current liabilities. Our current assets are 185,400 and current liabilities are 81,700. Our net working capital is **103,700**. We track this number over time.

**Current Ratio** is current assets divided by current liabilities. Using the same numbers, 185,400 divided by 81,700 gives a current ratio of **2.27**. This means for every dollar in current liabilities, we have $2.27 of current assets to pay them off. This indicates good short-term debt-paying ability.

**Quick Ratio** (or Acid-Test Ratio) is a stricter test. It excludes inventory from current assets because it might be slow to sell. The formula is (Cash + Marketable Securities + Accounts Receivable) / Current Liabilities. Our quick assets are 21,600 (Cash) + 12,200 (Marketable Securities) + 79,600 (Accounts Receivable) = 113,400. Dividing this by current liabilities (81,700) gives a quick ratio of **1.39**. We have $1.39 in highly liquid assets for every dollar of current liabilities, so we feel good about our ability to pay our short-term debts.

### 市场比率 (Market Ratios)

Market ratios require information about the company's stock price and shares.

**Earnings Per Share (EPS)** is a crucial metric. The formula is (Net Income - Preferred Stock Dividends) / Weighted Average Common Shares Outstanding. Our net income is 36,200, we have no preferred stock, and the weighted average shares are 9,540. Our EPS is **$3.79**. For every share outstanding, the company has earned $3.79.

**Dividend Payout Ratio** is the percentage of earnings paid out as dividends. It's dividends per share divided by earnings per share. Our dividends per share are $0.70. So, $0.70 divided by $3.79 (EPS) is **18.47%**. We are paying out about 18.5% of our earnings as dividends and retaining the other 81.5% to grow the company.

**Market Capitalization (Market Cap)** is the total market value of the company's equity. It's the total number of shares times the market price per share. With 9,540 shares and a stock price of $107.30, the market cap is **$1,023,642**.

**Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio** helps determine how expensive a stock is. It's the market price per share divided by the earnings per share. Our market price is $107.30 and our EPS is $3.79. The P/E ratio is **28.31**. This means we are paying 28.31 times its earnings for the stock. A historical average might be 15-20, so this stock could be considered relatively expensive.

### 偿付能力比率 (Solvency Ratios)

Solvency measures long-term debt-paying ability.

**Debt-to-Assets Ratio** shows how much of our assets are financed by debt. It's total liabilities divided by total assets. Our total liabilities are 284,100 and total assets are 466,500. The ratio is **60.90%**. About 61% of our assets are financed by debt.

**Debt-to-Equity Ratio** compares how much the company owes to how much the owners have invested. It's total liabilities divided by stockholders' equity. Our liabilities are 284,100 and our equity is 182,400. The ratio is **1.56**. For every $1 of equity, we have borrowed $1.56.

**Equity Multiplier** is another way to look at leverage. It's total assets divided by total equity. Our assets are 466,500 and equity is 182,400. The multiplier is **2.56**. For every dollar in equity, we control $2.56 of assets. Note that the Equity Multiplier is always 1.0 plus the Debt-to-Equity ratio (1.56 + 1.0 = 2.56).

**Times Interest Earned (TIE)** measures the ability to cover interest payments. It's EBIT divided by interest expense. Our EBIT is 58,700 and our interest expense is 9,200. The TIE ratio is **6.38 times**. We could have paid our interest expense 6.38 times over with our pre-tax, pre-interest earnings. A higher number is better, indicating less risk.

### DuPont 分析 (DuPont Analysis)

Now we can return to the DuPont analysis, which breaks down ROE into three components: profitability, asset efficiency, and financial leverage.

**ROE = (Net Profit Margin) x (Total Asset Turnover) x (Equity Multiplier)**

Using our previously calculated numbers:
ROE = (8.52%) x (0.91) x (2.56) = **19.85%**

This matches the 19.85% we calculated for ROE directly. This framework shows how profitability (Net Profit Margin), asset use efficiency (Total Asset Turnover), and leverage (Equity Multiplier) combine to drive the return for shareholders. The more debt we have, the higher the equity multiplier, which increases our return on equity (assuming the company is profitable).
