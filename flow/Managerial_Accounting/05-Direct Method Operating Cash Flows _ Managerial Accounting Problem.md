### 1. 客户处收到的现金 (Cash Received from Customers)

To find the cash received from customers, we'll take sales revenue and adjust for the change in accounts receivable.

Our revenue is $1,922,000. Why isn't this our cash received? Because of accounts receivable. We have an increase in accounts receivable of $30,500. Think about it: would you rather have cash go up or accounts receivable go up? You'd rather have cash go up. An increase in accounts receivable means we have a decrease in cash relative to sales. We didn't collect all the sales revenue in cash. So, we'll subtract this increase.

- **Revenue**: $1,922,000

### 2. 支付给供应商的现金 (Cash Paid to Suppliers)

This one is a bit tricky. We start with Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and adjust for changes in both inventory and accounts payable. We are converting the accrual number (COGS) into a cash number. It's like a little CSI investigation for cash flow.

Our Cost of Goods Sold is $1,044,000.

First, let's look at the change in inventory: a decrease of $19,400. Did we buy more inventory, or did we use up existing inventory? We used up inventory. If we had bought more, our cash payments would have gone up. Since we bought less than we sold (our inventory level dropped), our cash payment was reduced. So, we subtract this decrease from COGS to lower the cash paid amount.

Next, the change in accounts payable: an increase of $18,100. If accounts payable goes up, it means we bought things but haven't paid for them yet. This reduces our cash payment for the period. So, we also subtract this increase.

- **Cost of Goods Sold**: $1,044,000
- **Less: Decrease in Inventory**: ($19,400)
- **Less: Increase in Accounts Payable**: ($18,100)
- **Cash Paid to Suppliers**: **$1,006,500**

This entire amount represents a cash payment, so it will be a negative number in our final statement.

### 结论

This is how you calculate operating cash flow using the **direct method**. You figure out cash received from customers, cash paid to suppliers, and then account for all other cash-based operating expenses like salaries, rent, utilities, and taxes.

Make sure you study this and verify how to get the numbers—the pluses and minuses can be confusing. Just lay it out and be organized.

The **direct method** is easier for users to read, but it's harder to prepare. You see how much work it was. For this reason, about 95% of US companies use the **indirect method**, where they start with net income and make adjustments. Only about 5% use the direct method, and now you know why.
