Let's get started with this full problem for the Martin Company. The instruction is to prepare the statement of cash flows using the indirect method. We have a comparative balance sheet with two years of data, which allows us to see the difference between the periods. The balance sheet includes current assets like cash, accounts receivable, and inventory; non-current assets like equipment and accumulated depreciation; current liabilities; and equity accounts like common stock and retained earnings.

A cash flow statement has three sections: operating, investing, and financing. Operating activities are everything involved with net income, including adjustments for depreciation, gains or losses, and changes in current assets and current liabilities. I've set up a skeleton of the cash flow statement to guide us. If we do it correctly, the total net cash flow will explain the difference between our beginning and ending cash balances. Cash went from $30,550 to $29,400, which is a decrease of $1,150. Our goal is to explain this $1,150 decrease.

We'll start the operating activities section with **Net Income**, which is $10,500. We always start with net income and then make adjustments. The first adjustment is to add back non-cash expenses. On the income statement, we have **Depreciation Expense** of $7,200. Since depreciation is a non-cash expense that reduced net income, we add it back.

Next, we adjust for the changes in current assets and current liabilities.
- **Decrease in Accounts Receivable**: Accounts receivable went from $13,450 down to $10,000, a decrease of $3,450. A decrease in an asset like accounts receivable means we collected more cash, so we add this amount.
- **Increase in Inventory**: Inventory went from $67,500 up to $90,050, an increase of $22,550. When an asset goes up, it's a use of cash. We purchased more inventory, so this is a cash outflow, and we subtract this amount.
- **Increase in Accounts Payable**: Accounts payable went from $8,800 up to $10,000, an increase of $1,200. When a liability goes up, it's a source of cash, as if we borrowed from our suppliers. So, we add this amount.
- **Decrease in Salaries Payable**: Salaries payable went from $1,950 down to $400, a decrease of $1,550. When a liability goes down, it means we paid it off, which is a use of cash. So, we subtract this amount.
Summing these items, our **Net Cash Flow from Operating Activities** is -$1,750.

Now, let's look at investing activities, which involve non-current assets. Looking at our balance sheet, equipment increased from $41,000 to $51,400, a difference of $10,400. This indicates a **Purchase of Equipment**. Additional information confirms we purchased equipment for $10,400 cash. Buying equipment is a use of cash, so this is a cash outflow. Our **Net Cash Flow from Investing Activities** is -$10,400. The change in accumulated depreciation is already accounted for by the depreciation expense in the operating section.

Finally, we have financing activities, which relate to long-term debt and stockholders' equity.
- **Sale of Stock**: Common stock increased from $25,000 to $37,000, an increase of $12,000. This means we issued new stock for cash, which is a cash inflow.
- **Cash Dividends Paid**: The additional information states we declared and paid dividends of $1,000. Paying dividends is a use of cash, so it's a cash outflow.
The change in retained earnings is explained by net income ($10,500) minus dividends paid ($1,000). Our **Net Cash Flow from Financing Activities** is the $12,000 inflow from stock minus the $1,000 outflow for dividends, totaling $11,000.

Now for the magic moment. Let's calculate the **Net Change in Cash** by summing the three sections: Operating (-$1,750) + Investing (-$10,400) + Financing ($11,000) = -$1,150. This is exactly the change we calculated at the beginning. To complete the statement, we take the **Cash at the Beginning of the Year** ($30,550), add the net change of -$1,150, and we get the **Cash at the End of the Year**, which is $29,400. This matches the ending cash balance on our balance sheet, so we know our statement is correct.

This is how you prepare a complete statement of cash flows using the indirect method. We start with net income, make adjustments for non-cash items and changes in working capital, and then account for investing and financing activities to explain the overall change in the cash balance.
