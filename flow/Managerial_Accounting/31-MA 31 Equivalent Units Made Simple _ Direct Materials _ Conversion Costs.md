**Process costing** is used when all the products are the same. We're making things like soap, bricks, or refining petroleum—all the things where we have hundreds and thousands of exactly the same product. They're not unique. **Job costing**, the previous chapter, or job-order costing, is always for unique or customized products, like custom furniture, special order products, and things that have different costs. So that's the previous chapter, job costing. We're working with process costing, and everything is the same.

The key concept to understanding this whole chapter is the idea of **equivalent units**, or **equivalent units of production**. We're trying to measure how much work has been completed or finished. Here's the basic idea: if we have 10 units that are 60% complete, that means we have six equivalent units. The idea is that the work done is equivalent to fully completing six units.

In process costing, we have materials, labor, and overhead, just like always. However, a lot of times, because we allocate overhead based on direct labor cost, we put those two together and call that **conversion cost**. So, conversion cost is labor and overhead. In our example today, we'll have direct materials and conversion costs. By the way, you could also put materials and labor together, which is called **prime costs**. If you ever see that term, prime cost is materials and labor. But for our purposes, we'll allocate costs based on materials and conversion costs.

Let's do a simple example, and then we'll work a homework problem. Let's say we're making 50 gift baskets for a fundraiser at school. On day one, everyone gets together and we fill the baskets with all the non-perishable items: gummies, chocolate, popcorn, pretzels, and all the things you would add to a gift basket. Then on day two, you add perishable items like apples, oranges, and bananas, and you finish everything. Let's say at the end of day one, everything is 70% complete. The actual number of completed baskets on day one is zero, and then all 50 are completed on day two. This shows the actual output, but it doesn't measure how much work we have done each day.

The concept of **equivalent units** helps here. On day one, we had 50 baskets that were 70% complete. So, 50 units times 70% is 35 equivalent units. This means we did enough work to have started and finished 35 baskets. On the second day, we did the remaining 30% of the work on those 50 baskets, which is 50 times 30%, or 15 equivalent units. The total work done is equivalent to 50 units (35 + 15), but we've now measured the work done each day. When we get into process costing, we take the cost and divide it by the units. We will use the equivalent units (35 and 15) to measure the work done, not the actual completed units (0 and 50).

Let's work a problem. We'll calculate equivalent units using the **weighted-average method**. There are two methods, but I'm going to focus on the weighted-average method. If you'd find a FIFO example helpful, put "FIFO" in the comments, and I can easily do a problem with FIFO. The difference in the final numbers is usually not very large.

Here's the problem: The Romeo Company uses process costing. For its first two months of operations, calculate the equivalent units for materials and conversion costs using the weighted-average method.
- **Month 1**: Begins with 0 units, starts 1,000 units, and completes 700 units. The ending work-in-process units are 40% complete for materials and 55% complete for conversion costs.
- **Month 2**: Starts with the ending inventory from Month 1, starts an additional 2,200 units, and completes 2,000 units. The ending work-in-process units are 65% complete for materials and 75% complete for conversion costs.

### Month 1 Calculation

First, let's set up the actual physical units. It's very important to stay grounded in the actual units before calculating equivalent units.
- Beginning units: 0
- Units started: 1,000
- Total units available: 0 + 1,000 = 1,000

From these 1,000 available units:
- Units completed and transferred out: 700
- Units in ending work-in-process: 1,000 - 700 = 300

Now, let's calculate the equivalent units. We have two categories: units completed, and units in ending work-in-process.
- **Units Completed (700 units)**: These are 100% complete for both materials and conversion.
  - Materials: 700 units
  - Conversion: 700 units
- **Ending WIP (300 units)**:
  - Materials: 300 units * 40% complete = 120 equivalent units
  - Conversion: 300 units * 55% complete = 165 equivalent units

**Total Equivalent Units for Month 1:**
- **Materials**: 700 (completed) + 120 (ending WIP) = **820**
- **Conversion Costs**: 700 (completed) + 165 (ending WIP) = **865**
Later, we would take our total costs for the period and divide by these equivalent units to find the cost per equivalent unit.

### Month 2 Calculation

Now let's do the same thing for month two using the same format.
- Beginning units (from Month 1's ending WIP): 300
- Units started: 2,200
- Total units available: 300 + 2,200 = 2,500

A key point for the **weighted-average method**: we don't care that the beginning 300 units were already partially complete (40% for materials, 55% for conversion). We only need to know the total units completed during the period. This is a major difference from the FIFO method, where that information would be critical.

From the 2,500 available units:
- Units completed and transferred out: 2,000
- Units in ending work-in-process: 2,500 - 2,000 = 500

Now, let's calculate the equivalent units for month two.
- **Units Completed (2,000 units)**: These are 100% complete.
  - Materials: 2,000 units
  - Conversion: 2,000 units
- **Ending WIP (500 units)**:
  - Materials: 500 units * 65% complete = 325 equivalent units
  - Conversion: 500 units * 75% complete = 375 equivalent units

**Total Equivalent Units for Month 2:**
- **Materials**: 2,000 (completed) + 325 (ending WIP) = **2,325**
- **Conversion Costs**: 2,000 (completed) + 375 (ending WIP) = **2,375**

So, in month two, even though we completed 2,000 actual units, we did enough work to be equivalent to finishing 2,325 units in terms of materials and 2,375 units in terms of conversion. We not only completed the 2,000 units but also did additional work on the ending inventory, which means we have less work to do next month.

The whole concept of process costing requires you to know the actual units and then measure how much work was done, which is what **equivalent units** represent. I hope this was helpful.
