Here's our problem: we have balance sheets for two different years and we need to calculate the liquidity ratios. Liquidity is the concept of nearness to cash. It measures how quickly we can pay off our debts by looking at how much cash and short-term assets we have compared to our current liabilities. The three main liquidity ratios you need to know are **Net Working Capital**, the **Current Ratio**, and the **Quick Ratio** (sometimes called the **Acid-Test Ratio**).

Let's start with **Net Working Capital**. This isn't a true ratio, but a dollar amount calculated as Current Assets minus Current Liabilities. We'll focus on the current assets section of the balance sheet. In our example, the only current liability is Accounts Payable. For the current year (2030), we take total current assets of 203,000 and subtract current liabilities of 116,000, which results in a net working capital of 86,000. For the previous year (2029), the net working capital was 80,300. This figure represents the resources we have available to manage day-to-day operations and pay our debts.

Next is the **Current Ratio**, a very popular metric. It uses the same numbers but in a ratio format: Current Assets divided by Current Liabilities. For the current year, that's 203,000 divided by 116,000, which equals 1.74. This means for every $1 of current liabilities, we have $1.74 of current assets. Generally, a ratio above 1.5, or closer to 2, is considered healthy. Last year, our ratio was 2.19. While it has decreased, it's not in a danger zone, which would typically be a ratio below 1.5 or less than 1.

Finally, we have the **Quick Ratio**, which is a stricter test of liquidity. Its formula is (Cash + Accounts Receivable + Marketable Securities) / Current Liabilities. The key difference is that it excludes inventory and prepaid expenses, as they may not be converted to cash quickly. In our problem, we don't have marketable securities, so we'll just use cash and accounts receivable. For the current year, this gives us a quick ratio of 0.99. This means we have only 99 cents in highly liquid assets for every $1 of current liabilities. In the previous year, the quick ratio was 1.34.

In summary, our company's liquidity has decreased over the past year. The current ratio dropped from 2.19 to 1.74, and the quick ratio fell from 1.34 to 0.99. These ratios are crucial for assessing a company's ability to pay off its short-term debt. That's how you calculate **Net Working Capital**, the **Current Ratio**, and the **Quick Ratio**.
