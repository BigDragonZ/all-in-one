# MA 30 Calculate DM, WIP, and FG Ending Balances

Today we're working in managerial accounting, specifically the chapter on job order costing. We're working on figuring out the ending balances of **Materials**, **Work-in-Process**, and **Finished Goods**.

In this problem for Oscar Company, which uses a job costing system, we are given the beginning balances for **Materials**, **Work-in-Process**, and **Finished Goods**. We need to calculate the ending balances. We also have other data, such as the **Cost of Goods Manufactured**, sales, **Cost of Goods Sold**, and the predetermined manufacturing overhead (MOH) rate, which is 70% of the direct labor cost. We also have data for materials purchased, materials used, direct labor used, and actual MOH costs.

To solve this, we will set up **T-accounts** to compute the ending balances. This is the best way to solve problems like this, as you can spatially see what you're missing and what you have. You can't just look at the numbers and know the answer; you have to use a template and a format like **T-accounts**.

First, let's look at the **Materials** T-account. The beginning direct materials balance is $8,200. We add material purchases of $22,800. The problem states that direct materials used were $27,400. To find the ending balance, we take the beginning balance ($8,200), add purchases ($22,800), and subtract the materials used ($27,400). The amount that wasn't used, $3,600, is our ending direct materials balance.

Next, for the **Work-in-Process (WIP)** T-account, we start with the beginning balance of $7,100. We add the costs incurred: direct materials used ($27,400), direct labor used ($34,500), and applied manufacturing overhead (MOH). The MOH is not given directly; it's calculated based on the predetermined rate. The rate is 70% of the direct labor cost. So, we take 70% of $34,500 to find the applied MOH, which is $24,150. The **Cost of Goods Manufactured (CGM)**, which is the value of goods completed and transferred out of WIP, is given as $47,400. The ending WIP balance is the sum of the beginning balance and all costs added ($7,100 + $27,400 + $34,500 + $24,150) minus the CGM ($47,400), which results in an ending balance of $45,750.

For the **Finished Goods** T-account, the **Cost of Goods Manufactured** ($47,400) flows in from WIP. We start with the beginning finished goods balance of $6,400. The **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**, representing the cost of goods sold and moved out of this account, is given as $38,900. The ending finished goods balance is the beginning balance ($6,400) plus the CGM ($47,400), minus the COGS ($38,900), which equals $14,900.

Finally, we need to compute the overapplied or underapplied overhead. We compare the actual MOH costs to the applied MOH. The actual MOH cost is given as $24,800. This includes costs like depreciation, taxes, indirect materials, indirect labor, and utilities, which are often not known daily, hence the need for an estimate. The applied MOH, which we calculated earlier, was $24,150. Since the actual cost ($24,800) is greater than the applied cost ($24,150), we have a difference of $650. When actual overhead is greater than applied overhead, the result is **underapplied** overhead. This results in a debit balance in the MOH account. If the estimate had been too high (e.g., we applied $25,000), it would be **overapplied**.

Estimates are rarely perfect, so having a small variance is expected. This $650 of **underapplied** overhead would typically be closed out to **Cost of Goods Sold** at the end of the period. This shows our 70% estimate was very close to the actual rate. This is how you calculate the ending balances of **Materials**, **Work-in-Process**, and **Finished Goods**, and determine if overhead is **overapplied** or **underapplied**.
