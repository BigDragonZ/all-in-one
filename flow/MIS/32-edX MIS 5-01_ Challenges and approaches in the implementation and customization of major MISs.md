In the last lecture, we introduced the concepts of business processes and their relationship with Management Information Systems. More specifically, we discussed ERPs and how they support integrative, multifunctional business processes.

We'll now move on from the "what" to the "how". We'll look at the issues involved in selecting and adapting a Management Information System, with a specific focus on ERPs, for the specific needs of a company. In essence, we shall discuss who is boss: will companies operate in the manner dictated by the ERP they adopt, or will the ERP (or other Management Information Systems) adapt to the company's specificities? As you could easily guess, the answer to this blunt question will not be straightforward.

We will therefore start by posing the "ERP versus current practice" dilemma: who adapts to whom? To answer this, we will need to differentiate the various levels at which a Management Information System, such as an ERP, can be adapted—basically, parameterization and code adaptation. On the other hand, we'll also discuss the alternative approach: modifying the way things are done through what is sometimes called Business Process Re-engineering.

ERP implementation projects must tackle these and other challenges, and they can follow various strategies, such as the daring "Big Bang" approaches or the more conservative modular rollout approaches.

Last but not least, implementing an ERP is not the end, but rather only the beginning of the headaches. We shall also discuss post-implementation evolution challenges.
