## 字幕内容

In this video, we will analyze in more detail the integrated management information systems known as **ERPs**, or Enterprise Resource Planning systems. We will relate them to the concept of integrated business processes and to the life cycle of individual business transactions.

A **business process** is a collection of related activities or tasks that produce a specific service or product, and thus create value for a customer. This customer can be external to the company or a so-called internal customer, typically another group or department within the company. Quite often, the output of one task is the input for the following task. Thus, tasks are grouped together in a process because they are related by the effect they produce and because they typically belong to a sequence, not because they are similar. A typical example of a business process is **order fulfillment**, which encompasses all the activities a company must carry out to ensure that a customer order is fulfilled—that is, received, delivered, and, depending on how it is defined, even invoiced and collected.

On the other hand, a **business function**, such as marketing or accounting, encompasses all the tasks that are similar and therefore require the same skills or knowledge. Companies are often organized by functions. Thus, the accounting department would carry out the accounting tasks for all the company's business processes that involve some accounting.

The term "business process" can be used to refer to a large, long process encompassing many activities, such as order fulfillment. However, if we decompose this large process into several **sub-processes**, these are also considered processes. Thus, there is a continuum from high-level, very long business processes that can be gradually decomposed into ever-smaller sub-processes. At some arbitrary level, we decide a component is too simple or small to be called a process and instead name it an **activity** or **task**.

Some people call this elementary level a **business transaction**, and thus say that business processes can be decomposed into elementary business transactions. This is not exactly the same concept as a *database transaction* discussed in the last section, but it is related. Both the IT-oriented database transaction and its business-facing counterpart, the business transaction, share this "all-or-nothing" characteristic.

There is, however, an alternative meaning for "business transaction" that is quite relevant for our analysis. For lack of a better term, we will call each individual entity or object that goes through a business process an **individual business transaction**. Thus, each customer order going through the order fulfillment process, each purchasing order undergoing a procurement process, and each investment proposal being subject to an approval process would be considered individual business transactions. The **life cycle** of the business transaction, therefore, would correspond to the series of activities that make up the business process it must undergo.

We will differentiate **closed transactions**, in which all required tasks have been completed and the transaction's life cycle is finished, from **open transactions**. An example of an open transaction is a customer order in an order fulfillment process that has been received and approved, but not yet shipped.

These apparently simple concepts of business processes, sub-processes, activities, business functions, and individual business transactions are key for understanding management information systems and ERPs.
