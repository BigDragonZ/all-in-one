Embarking on an ERP implementation requires a lot of thought. A decision to be made while designing the implementation project is how suddenly or how gradually we want to implement it. The alternative approaches will be discussed in this video. We will look at several aspects, such as the modules to be implemented and the premises, locations, or organizational units where it will be implemented. In most cases, the ERP being implemented will replace pre-existing older systems, sometimes called legacy systems. Thus, we'll also discuss when we shall stop using the legacy information systems replaced by the ERP.

From our description of what an ERP is, it should be clear that it involves several different functional modules that share a common database. If our final objective is to install, say, five functional modules, we could decide to initially install one or two of them, use them for a while, and once they are stabilized, go on installing additional modules. That would be a **modular approach**. The alternative would be to install all the desired modules at the same time.

A **modular approach** means initially implementing just a subset of the modules we're planning to use. Thus, it implies that we have the intention to continue implementing additional modules, and we actually take that into account in our plan. The simple fact of not implementing all existing modules does not make an implementation plan modular. In fact, few companies implement all available modules.

Let us think for a moment about the pros and cons of a modular approach, as opposed to implementing all desired modules in one go. The main advantage of a modular implementation is the resulting risk reduction. Additionally, it allows the company implementing the ERP to spread the associated effort, cost, and business disruption over a longer period of time.

Spotting the stumbling blocks is a bit less obvious. They stem from the fact that the user company must continue to operate, and nowadays, companies are fully dependent on their information systems. Thus, the modular approach requires implementing intermediate setups that still allow the company to process its transactions and operate more or less normally. The effort involved in setting up these intermediate situations and the normally less-than-optimal support they provide to the company, and particularly to the users of the systems, are the major drawbacks. For example, the modular approach might require building temporary interfaces between the modules implemented first and the legacy systems that would be replaced by the remaining modules. This sometimes leads to a truly spaghetti-like maze of interfaces and manual information transfers. Besides, areas of the database pertaining to the modules not yet implemented might have to be partially loaded to provide essential context for the modules that are being implemented first.

In many companies, the ERP must support not just one central location, but rather a number of distinct premises, factories, divisions, or even different countries or subsidiaries. Thus, a somewhat similar decision must be taken on whether the system goes live everywhere at the same time. Complex installations will normally adopt a **roll-out approach**, whereby the system is implemented first in some locations, then it is rolled out gradually to the rest.

A key argument supporting the roll-out approach is the limited availability of support staff. At or around the go-live date, the implementation support staff is heavily involved, for example, providing initial user support, sorting out any issues, and so on. A roll-out approach enables this load to be distributed more evenly over a longer period. The combination of the chosen levels of modularity and roll-out will shape the aggressiveness, and therefore the risk and stress level, of the implementation project.

A **Big Bang approach**—implementing the ERP simultaneously in all the company's units, coupled with simultaneously installing all the desired modules—will be the fastest and potentially most efficient approach if it succeeds. However, the risk level and resource requirements involved make a more gradual approach a preferable option in most cases.

One more aspect to be taken into account is the timing of the discontinuation of the legacy systems replaced by the ERP. In some cases, the old and the new systems can co-exist for a period of parallel operation until the new system proves its worth. However, in most cases, the effort involved in processing all transactions through both the old and the new system makes this approach impractical, thus requiring a cut-off point at which the switch-over takes place.

Thus, while designing the ERP implementation project, a key decision will be the gradualness of the implementation. Module-by-module implementation and gradual roll-out by location or organizational unit will generally diminish risk and reduce the volume of support resources required at a given time. On the other hand, these gradual approaches often require extra effort and lead to a protracted period of sub-optimal operation. Striking the right balance requires a careful analysis of the implementation challenges and costs, which we shall discuss in subsequent videos. Thank you for listening.
