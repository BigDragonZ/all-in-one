Hi, welcome. In this session, I'd like to talk about how the accounting inconsistencies that we've talked about play out in the numbers. I'd like to focus on the four key accounting inconsistencies I highlighted in the last session. The first is tax rates. The second are contractual commitments that might not be called debt but are effectively debt. The third are capital expenditures that don't take the form of land, building, equipment, or physical investments. Finally, I want to talk about how stock-based compensation plays out in financial statements.

### 税率 (Tax Rates)

Let's start with tax rates. I've taken six companies here. The first thing I did was the easy thing: I went to the income statement for each company, pulled up the taxable income as stated in that income statement and the taxes, and I divided the taxes by the taxable income. What I get is an effective tax rate. Not surprisingly, Peloton, which is not making money, has a zero percent tax rate. But if you look across the other companies, you'll notice a wide variation in tax rates. Netflix pays only 9.5%, while Coca-Cola, on the other hand, pays 16.8%. Toyota pays 29%, and HSBC pays almost 35%.

I'm going to state a couple of generalizations that might not always hold. First, as companies age, you're going to see effective tax rates move towards marginal tax rates. Netflix is a younger company than Coca-Cola, and you see that reflected. Sometimes, even with mature companies, you can get strange-looking tax rates. Take a look at Dr. Reddy's Labs; I have a negative tax rate. You might ask, "How can that be?" Because in 2019, Dr. Reddy's actually got a tax credit that allowed them to collect 1.6 billion from the government, which effectively left them with a negative tax rate. Would I use a negative tax rate going forward? Of course not. But effective tax rates measure accrual income and taxes.

For each of these companies, I also went into the cash flow statement. You might ask what that will tell me. It's going to tell me what these companies actually paid in taxes in 2019. If you look at Netflix, you'll see a negative cash flow associated with deferred taxes in the income statement of 94 million. What does that mean? The income statement gave me taxes of 195 million, but the statement of cash flow says, "Look, I paid 94 million more than that." So, in effect, I paid 289 million as taxes in 2019. It's the same with Coca-Cola: 1.8 billion in taxes in 2019, but in the cash flow statement, I'm paying 421 million more in deferred taxes, which gives me a total tax paid of 2.2 billion. If you want to see what your company paid in cash taxes, you've got to look at what's in the income statement, but you also have to look at what's in the cash flow statement.

Incidentally, HSBC and Dr. Reddy's, because of accounting conventions, report statements of cash flows differently. They start with pre-tax income and then show the actual cash taxes paid. Take a look at HSBC: their income statement says they paid 4.6 billion in taxes, but the cash flow statement shows they paid only 2.3 billion. In the case of Dr. Reddy's, their income statement shows they saved 1.6 billion in taxes, but the cash flow statement says that they actually paid 4.8 billion in taxes.

Finally, the balance sheet is the residue of past tax actions. If you look at my six companies, three have positive deferred tax assets, and three (Coca-Cola, Toyota, and HSBC) have deferred tax liabilities. Now, earlier when I talked about deferred tax assets and liabilities, I said don't look at them the same way you would conventional assets and liabilities. What that means is if I liquidated Netflix today, nobody's paying me 658 million. And if I liquidated Coca-Cola today, I don't owe 2.3 billion. So why are they reported? They are signals of what these companies will have to pay as taxes in the future. For companies with deferred tax liabilities, you should expect to pay more in taxes in the future because you saved on taxes in the past. For companies with deferred tax assets, there's a signal that you're going to get away with paying lower taxes in the future.

One final point about Peloton: you might be asking where the deferred tax asset comes from. Remember, Peloton is a young company that's never made money. They've had loss after loss after loss. That shows up as a net operating loss. This deferred tax asset is basically a reflection of the tax benefits they're going to get because of losses accumulated from the past.

### 非债务承诺 (Non-debt Commitments)

Now let's move on to non-debt commitments. The key with interest-bearing debt, the reason you consider it, is the contractual obligation. You have to make that debt payment in good times and in bad times. That's why we worry about debt. If you think in those terms, any contractual commitment you've entered into is effectively debt, even if it's not shown as such on the balance sheet. The most common of these contractual commitments tend to be lease commitments, where you sign a lease for five, ten, or fifteen years. You've contractually committed to make that payment.

Until 2019, accounting for the most part allowed companies a free pass if they signed lease agreements. Those lease payments were treated as operating expenses, not financing expenses—something that's never made sense. But in 2019, they fixed that problem. So let's see how those lease commitments play out in income statements and balance sheets. I'm going to use Nordstrom from their 2019 financial statements to take you through the process of how exactly leases get converted to debt and the consequences.

We go to Nordstrom's financial statements, in the footnotes, and they still report this. They give you the commitments they've entered into. So in 2020, they've committed to pay 333 million; in 2021, 353 million. These are contractual commitments. Beyond year five (which in this case is 2025), there'd be a lump sum of all commitments beyond 2025, which is 1,136 million. This is what companies have always reported. In 2019, Nordstrom went through the process of converting this into debt. Let me take you through the process.

First, they took the 1,136 million in commitments beyond year five and spread it out over five years. You might ask, "Why over five years?" There's a bit of a judgment call here, but it seems likely the 1,136 million is for many years. So basically, they have 227.2 million every year from 2026 for the next five years. Now you have 10 years of lease commitments. They discount those lease commitments back at 4.7%. Where did the 4.7% come from? That was the accountant's estimate of Nordstrom's pre-tax cost of borrowing based on interest rates in 2019. If you discount those commitments back to today, you get a present value of 2.1 billion. That is the present value of lease commitments in the future.

Now, accountants have to bring it onto the balance sheet. You will see this 2.119 billion play out on Nordstrom's balance sheet in two places. One is the current portion of that debt—the lease payments coming due in the next year—which is 244 million and shows up under current liabilities. The remaining 1,875 million now shows up as the equivalent of debt on the balance sheet. Now, incidentally, when you decide to treat these commitments as debt, there's going to be an interest expense.

Let's look at the implications of what this does to all the numbers. The most direct one is the debt on your balance sheet is now going to be much higher by the present value of lease commitments. But balance sheets have to balance, so you have to create a counter-asset of an exactly equivalent amount. Once you create an asset, you're going to depreciate it in future years. Your operating income is also going to change. Pre-2019, the entire operating lease expense would have been subtracted as an operating expense. Now, you're going to add back that lease expense and subtract out the depreciation you'd have on the leased asset to get to operating income. Generally, operating income will go up. However, the net income will not change, because in addition to the depreciation, you'll also have to subtract out the interest expense. Your taxes shouldn't change either, because you're replacing one tax-deductible expense (an operating lease expense) with two (interest and depreciation) of an equivalent amount.

Bottom line: debt will go up, assets will go up, operating income will generally go up, net income should not change, and your taxes should not change.

Of course, accountants have dealt with leases, but it's only the tip of the iceberg. This applies to any contractual commitment. To give you a sense of how this would play out with a different kind of commitment, take Netflix. In 2019, they had content commitments—commitments they've entered into with movie producers to show TV shows or movies in the future. They break down those commitments in their footnotes: less than a year, one to three years, three to five years. You have contractual commitments of 19.5 billion. Unlike leases where accounting standards have caught up, here the rules seem to be different for different companies. Netflix, given how large these commitments are, does show content assets of 24.5 billion. If you ask me to link up their commitments to how they came up with the assets, I'll have to throw up my hands because there's relatively little transparency here as to how these commitments get converted to assets. They're doing the right thing, but I wish they'd be more transparent.

### 资本性支出 (Capital Expenses)

Now let's talk about capital expenses. If you go back to the notion that capital expenses are expenses designed to create benefits over many years, there's nothing in that definition that requires those investments to be in physical, tangible assets. But unfortunately, accounting seems to have two different sets of rules. When you have capital expenditures in physical assets, it's shown as capex on the statement of cash flows, shows up in your assets, and then gets depreciated. But if your expenditures are intangible, accounting seems to be all over the place.

Take the most common of these expenses: R&D. Nobody can argue against the notion that R&D is an expense for future use; it's really to get benefits in future years. It should be a capex, but it's treated as an operating expense. By doing so, you're not just misstating your income, you're keeping your biggest asset off the books. And it's not just R&D; it's training expenses for a company reliant on human capital or acquisition costs for a subscription-based company.

I'm going to take you through the process of how you could convert an operating expense back into a capital expense. Let's take Amgen, a pharmaceutical company, and its R&D. Step one: I have to define the amortizable life for R&D. For a pharmaceutical company, it takes a really long time. I'm going to make a judgment call and assume it'll take ten years. I think 10 is more defensible than zero (which is the assumption when you treat it as an operating expense).

Next step: I go collect R&D expenses for the last 10 years. The R&D expense from 10 years ago (663 million) would have been fully written off by now. The R&D from nine years ago (823 million) would have one-tenth left over. The R&D from eight years ago would have two-tenths left over, and so on. If I add up what's left over, I come up with a total value of 13.3 billion. That tells me how much capital I've invested in R&D over the last 10 years that's still on my balance sheet. The total amortization for this year would be 1.694 billion.

So here's what's going to happen. My R&D expense of 3.03 billion, which is treated as an operating expense, I'm now going to abandon that practice. I'm going to treat R&D as a capital expense. The amortization is going to be 1.694 billion. The unamortized R&D (13.3 billion) is now going to go to my balance sheet as an asset. To adjust my income, I'm going to take the stated income, add back the R&D expense, and then subtract out the amortization. This will push up my net income and operating income by almost 1.4 billion. My company is more profitable than I thought it was.

But that's not all. I'm bringing 13.3 billion onto my balance sheet as an asset. My book equity and invested capital are going to go up by 13.3 billion. When you capitalize R&D, you change your view of the company: how much it's investing, how much it's making, and what kind of returns it's making. You're getting a much better sense of what the company does.

### 股权激励 (Stock-based Compensation)

Finally, let's talk about stock-based compensation. Until 2004, accountants were all over the place when companies granted options to their employees, often treating them as worthless. In the last few years, accounting has caught up with reality. When you grant options, companies must show an expense based on the value of the option or restricted stock at the time of issuance. You're going to see an expense line item now for stock-based compensation, which can be a pretty large one, especially for young companies.

But in the footnotes, you also see the residue of past practices. If you keep giving options every year, you're accumulating a potential increase in the share count as these options can be converted to shares. Let's take Netflix. It's no longer a young company and has granted options and restricted stock for years. The options they grant this year will show up as an expense. But if you go to the footnotes, they also show you the options carried over from previous years: 20.9 million shares' worth, with an average exercise price of $124 because many were granted when the stock price was much lower. These options have a long life, a low exercise price, and a high volatility. When I value Netflix, this is going to hang over my valuation.

Companies like to have their cake and eat it too. If you look at Peloton's prospectus, they have a pro forma statement with an "adjusted EBITDA." The biggest adjustment is they've added back stock-based compensation. You can see the logic: they're saying it's not a cash expense. But they're leaving this illusion that it's like depreciation. It's not like depreciation. They've given away a share of your company. Personally, I don't think we should be adding back stock-based compensation to get to cash flows, because grants in kind are very different from non-cash expenses like depreciation.

### 结论

Here's the bottom line: accounting statements are the raw material that you and I use in finance. If we start with that perspective, we have the right, in fact, we have the obligation, to mold that raw material to fit our own needs. That's why I have absolutely no qualms about going in and redoing the accounting to reflect my priorities. We will get different numbers for earnings, invested capital, and even share count, but I'm going to argue that the different numbers we get are a more realistic judgment of what the company looks like than what the accountant tells you.

Finally, don't fall for the delusion that until accountants come to their senses, markets are blind. In 2019, accountants made a big deal about treating leases as debt, and they acted like markets were going to be surprised. You know what markets did? They blew them off. They said, "We knew this already. We've been doing this for 30 years." Markets come to their senses a lot sooner than accountants. So while I make a big deal about R&D, guess what? Markets have moved on. They're already treating R&D as a capital expense, which partly explains the higher multiples of earnings you often see technology companies trade at. So think about that as you look at these companies. Accountants make mistakes and judgments that reflect their priorities, but their priorities do not have to be yours.
