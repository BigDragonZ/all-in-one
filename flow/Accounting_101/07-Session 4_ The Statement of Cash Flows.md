Now that we've talked about income statements and balance sheets, it's time to turn to the third financial statement: the statement of cash flows.

At first sight, the objectives of the statement of cash flows seem pretty limited. If you look at the bottom line, it appears designed to explain how much a company's cash balance changed during a period and why. But if we dig a little deeper, a statement of cash flows provides more information. First, it tells you how much cash the company generated or paid out from its operations (cash flows from operations). It also shows how much cash the company invested back into its business or elsewhere, and how much cash it raised from or returned to its debt and equity investors.

These cash flows are useful in much of financial analysis. One thing that separates a statement of cash flows from other financial statements is that the sign tells you the direction of the cash flow. In other words, a positive cash flow is shown as a positive number, and a negative cash flow is shown as a negative number. This might seem obvious, but other financial statements don't always follow this practice. Incidentally, the statement of cash flows also looks at the business through the eyes of equity investors, as we will see in a minute.

If you look at the statement of cash flows for any company, it's broken down into three parts. The first section is cash flows from operations to equity investors. I'm emphasizing "equity investors" because you'll see this detail play out. The second set is cash flows from investing, which includes investing in operating or non-operating assets. The third set is cash flows from financing. Remember, you can fund a business with debt or equity. This section shows cash flows from new debt taken on, debt repaid, new equity issuances, and cash returned to equity through dividends and buybacks. The net effect of these three sections is the change in your cash balance.

Let's break this down, starting with cash flow from operations. This section usually starts with net income. Now you can see why I've been harping on the fact that statements of cash flows are measured through the eyes of equity investors: net income is equity income. The bottom line of the income statement becomes the starting point for the cash flow from operations. To get to net income, you subtracted non-cash expenses like depreciation and amortization. Since these are accounting expenses but not cash expenses, the first step is to add them back. You add back depreciation, amortization, and any other non-cash items you've expensed.

Then, you adjust for a series of items, including the change in accounts receivable and the change in inventory. Consolidated, these line items represent the change in non-cash working capital. When you invest money in inventory, that's cash tied up; an increase in inventory is a decrease in your cash flow. Similarly, an increase in accounts receivable is a decrease in your cash flow. In contrast, when you use more supplier credit (accounts payable), that's an increase in your cash flow. By adjusting for the change in non-cash working capital, we are, in effect, converting accrual income into cash income.

I describe working capital as the residue of accrual accounting. In the statement of cash flows, by bringing in the change in non-cash working capital, you're converting accrual income into cash income. So, you take net income, add back non-cash charges, and subtract the change in non-cash working capital to arrive at cash flow from operations. This working capital effect varies across businesses. In working-capital-intensive businesses, you can see a substantial cash outflow as you're growing, because non-cash working capital usually grows with the company.

Next is cash flows from investing. I'll break this down into two groups. The first is cash flow from investing in operating assets. For a manufacturing company, this would be what you spend on building new factories or buying new equipment. As a company matures, you might also see divestitures of old assets show up as a cash inflow. What you're trying to capture is the net investment in operating assets. If you grow by acquiring other companies, the cash paid for that acquisition also shows up here. So, you consolidate capital expenditures, net out divestitures, and add cash acquisitions to get the investment in operating assets.

You can also invest in non-operating assets, like marketable securities or minority stakes in other companies. I classify these as investment in non-operating assets. When you look at cash flows from investing, you might want to separate the line items related to the company's core operations from those related to non-operating investments. The investing section tells you how much the company is putting back into its business. The investments in operating assets tell you how aggressively the company is trying to grow. If a company plans to grow its revenues and operating income, it has to invest in operating assets. One useful comparison is between depreciation (from CFO) and capital expenditures (from CFI). Depreciation represents the depletion of assets, while capital expenditures represent adding to assets. For growing companies, capital expenditures should be much greater than depreciation.

One of the limitations of the statement of cash flows is that some companies grow by acquiring other companies but skip the cash step. They pay for acquisitions with their own shares. If you acquire other companies using stock, it will not show up in a statement of cash flows. This is something to think about when you consider what you might be missing.

This brings me to the third and final section: cash flows from financing. There are two ways of financing a company: debt or equity. So, I'll break down financing cash flows into cash flows to/from debt and cash flows to/from equity. Let's start with debt. When you repay debt, it's a cash outflow. Interest expenses are already accounted for because we started with net income. So, the only debt-related cash flows here are debt repayments (outflows) and new debt issuances (inflows). The net cash flow from debt is the difference between these two. Sometimes this is broken down into short-term and long-term debt, but ultimately it's all cash in or cash out.

What about equity? New equity issuances, either in an IPO or a secondary offering, are cash inflows. Paying dividends is obviously a cash outflow, and so is buying back stock. This is where you would see how much a company spent on stock buybacks. The net cash flow from equity is the net effect of equity issuances set against dividends and stock buybacks.

Until the 1980s, dividends were the primary way companies returned cash to stockholders. Since then, especially in the U.S., companies have increasingly shifted to buybacks. From the company's perspective, it's cash leaving the firm, whether as dividends or buybacks. You, as a stockholder, might have different views, but the cash flow effect is the same. With dividends, every stockholder receives a piece of the cash. With buybacks, only those shareholders who sell their shares back get the cash. Those who don't sell receive a bonus in the form of a higher stock price. So, both are cash returns, they just have different consequences for investors.

One final piece of data you can get from the statement of cash flows is the potential dividend, or what's called Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE). This is how much the company could have paid in dividends or used to buy back stock. To calculate it, start with the cash flow from operations. Then, go to the investing section and subtract only the net capital expenditures on operating assets (including cash acquisitions). Then, factor in the net cash flows from debt (new debt raised minus debt repaid). What you're left with is the free cash flow to equity after debt payments. This is what you can potentially afford to pay out. I understand why companies might not pay all of it out, or why some might return more than this amount, but this measures what you can afford.
