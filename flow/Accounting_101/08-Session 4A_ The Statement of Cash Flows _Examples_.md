## 字幕内容

Now that we've described statements of cash flows in the abstract, it's time to get real by looking at the statements of cash flows of actual companies. As with income statements and balance sheets, I'm going to start with a life cycle perspective. Remember, there are three sections to the cash flow statement: operating cash flows, investing cash flows, and financing cash flows. Here's what you should expect to see as companies are born and age.

With very young companies, you should expect to see negative cash flows from operating because they have very little net income or are losing money, and they might have working capital needs. You should have negative cash flows from investing because you're putting money in to grow in the future. The only positive cash flow you get is in financing, and that's because you're raising new equity or new debt. So, operating cash flows would be negative, investing cash flows will be negative, and financing cash flow will be positive because you're raising money.

With a young growth company, you're going to see a continuation of that trend. Operating cash flows will continue to be negative. Investing cash flows will reflect the fact that there's continued investment to capture future growth. Financing cash flows will still be positive because you're raising new equity, either because you're going public or because a venture capitalist is supplying your equity.

As you get to be a high-growth firm, your operating cash flows now have a chance of turning positive. They might not, but they have a chance because you're starting to make money. Your investing cash flows stay negative because you're still a growth company. Your financing cash flows will start to become neutral. In other words, you're no longer raising equity like you used to, but you're definitely not paying out dividends, and you're not buying back stock.

As you become a mature growth company, your operating cash flows will become increasingly positive. You're making more money, and your working capital needs become a smaller percentage of that money you make. Your investing will stabilize; you're not investing as much as you used to, and relative to your scale as a company, it's dropped off. Your financing cash flows start to see the first inflows because you've started to borrow money and perhaps your first outflows to equity investors—a small buyback, a small dividend.

As you become a stable company, your operating cash flow should now be positive and stable. Your investing cash flows will level off; it will be mostly maintenance investing. You're not pumping up your assets; at best, you're putting your depreciation back into capex and a little bit more. You're now going to see regular debt issuances become part of your cash flows, and you're going to find that when you repay old debt, it's often with new debt. Not surprisingly, you're buying back more stock and paying more dividends.

When you're in decline, your operating cash flows start to drop off because you're making less money. Your investing cash flows could now become a positive cash flow because you're selling assets rather than investing in them; you're shrinking as a business. In your financing cash flows, if you have good sense, you'll be bringing your debt down. You should see debt repayments and increasing cash flow returns to equity because you're shrinking as a company.

Let's see if this plays out. Let's start again with **Peloton**, a young fitness equipment company that makes its money from equipment and subscriptions. Let's look at what the statement of cash flows tells you. First, let's look at the big line items. If you look at the cash from investing, operating, and financing, the operating and investing cash flows are, not surprisingly, negative because it's a young company. The financing cash flow is positive, but only because you raised $539 million in new equity. It's equity issuances that are making them. The cash provided by operating is negative because you're a young, money-losing company, but you're covering those cash flows by issuing new stock. Your investing is also obviously negative.

One interesting feature here that I want to talk about is the fact that stock-based compensation is being added back. You can see the logic for this. This is a cash flow statement, and if you're paying your employees with shares, you're really not paying cash. So when you report your income and it's after employee compensation, and that compensation was primarily in the form of stock, you're saying it's a non-cash expense and you're adding it back. I think there's a big difference between depreciation and stock-based compensation, and I'll come back and talk about that in the next session. But that's not surprising with a young company.

Let's move one step up the ladder and look at **Netflix**. If you look at its operating cash flows, they reflect the fact that it's making money now. But the biggest expense that Netflix has that makes its operating cash flows negative is how much it's spending on content. I know people talk about how much Netflix spends on content; the best place to see it is in the statement of cash flows, not in the income statement where you should see an expense, because that's an accounting expense. This is how much Netflix is actually spending on content. In 2019, Netflix spent $13.9 billion in addition to content. If you spend $13.9 billion, even if you make some money—$1.9 billion in net income—your cash flows from operations are going to be negative. So Netflix is making money, but it has negative cash flows from operations.

If you look at the investing activities, there's not much going on. Are you surprised? Their biggest investment is in content, and it shows up as part of their operating cash flow. While accountants make a big deal about separating operating from investing from financing cash flows, with companies like Netflix, they mess up. The biggest investing cash flow is, in fact, the investment in content, and it's now showing up as part of an operating cash flow. If you look at the financing cash flows, Netflix is starting to use debt, and you can see that in how much more they're raising from debt issuances than they pay off in debt. Much of their financing of those negative cash flows from operations is coming from borrowing more money. We can debate whether this is a sensible thing to do or not, but it's a reality you face with Netflix. This is a company that has negative cash flows from operations, doesn't spend much on investing in the traditional sense, but makes up for those negative cash flows from financing, primarily from debt.

Incidentally, those line items that you see highlighted under "2" are basically a consolidated working capital. It's not a big deal for Netflix, but I want to focus in again. As I've mentioned earlier, when you look at cash flows, the signs on the cash flows tell you which direction the cash is flowing. So when it says "other current assets" is minus $252 million, that means that other current assets created a negative cash flow of $252 million. For that to be true, other current assets must have increased by $252 million. When current assets go up, it's a negative cash flow. When current liabilities go up, it's a positive cash flow. "Accounts payable" being a positive cash flow tells you accounts payable went up. If you consolidate all of these items, you get the change in non-cash working capital. If that number is positive, meaning your non-cash working capital increased, it's a decrease in your cash flow, a drain on your cash flows. If that number decreases, then it's an increase in your cash flow. It's kind of tough to get that sign change in your head, but get it in your head when you look at statements of cash flows.

Let's turn to **Toyota**. One of the items you'll notice for Toyota, which shows up in other statements of cash flows, is deferred income taxes. The way to think about deferred income taxes is these are taxes you're paying from previous years. Here it's ¥86.6 billion. If you go to Toyota's income statement, you'll see an income tax item. Deferred taxes reflect what you paid over and above that. So if you see ¥100 million as a tax expense in your income statement and you see another ¥50 million as a negative cash flow in your statement of cash flows, in sum, that year the company paid ¥150 million. So deferred income taxes, in this case, is a negative cash flow; you're paying off taxes from previous years.

If you look at the dividends, there are three line items here: dividends to shareholders. But dividends, no matter who they go to, are cash outflows. So think of all of those as cash leaving the company.

As you look across sectors, as with income statements and balance sheets, there will be line items that show up in statements of cash flows that are divergent, but not as many, because statements of cash flows are ultimately about cash in and cash out. What does it matter whether a company is an oil company, a pharmaceutical company, or a bank? Let's take a look at an oil company. If you look at **Total**, the French oil company, you'll notice depreciation and amortization. You're saying that's shown up in all of the statements of cash flows, what's the big deal? Notice how big it is. Because depreciation and amortization is such a big line item, you can have oil companies that are money-losing but cash flow positive because the depreciation is not a cash expense.

Later on, when we get to financial analysis, we'll talk about how people use EBITDA multiples. One reason people like to use EBITDA is that while companies can be money-losing, if they have a big depreciation charge like they do for Total, you can end up with a positive cash flow. So depreciation, depletion, amortization, and impairment—the reason we add it back is it's a non-cash expense. Because it's so large, it can alter our perception of this company as a cash flow producing company.

If you look at the investing cash flows, you'll notice that there are a lot more disposals here. This is part of the regular course of business, so it's not something you should be ignoring. It's something they do regularly because it shows up every year. In contrast, if you see proceeds from disposal and they show up in only one out of every five or ten years, maybe it's an unusual disposal and you should not be factoring it in when forecasting. Remember, ultimately you look at financials to get a sense of what they will look like in the future. Here, it looks like these line items will continue to be there going forward.

---

So here's the bottom line. The statement of cash flows looks at a business through the eyes of equity investors; that's why we start with net income. There is information in each of the three pieces. The cash flows from operations tell you how mature this company is. As a company matures, that cash flow will go from negative to positive. The cash flows from investing will tell you something about what this company thinks about its own future. Companies that are optimistic about growth in the future should be investing more than companies that are not. And the financing activities tell you something about the mix of debt and equity that this company uses and how it's changing over time.

If your objective is to estimate cash flows prior to debt payments, not after debt payments, you could start with the statement of cash flows, but you have a little work to do. That statement of cash flows is after debt payments. You've got to add back your debt cash flows, which are in the statement, but you also have to add back interest expenses because that is subtracted out to get to net income. But a statement of cash flows tells you cash in, cash out, and from that perspective, I think it gives you information you can use to analyze the company. I hope you found this session useful.
