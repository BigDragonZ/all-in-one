In this second session of my course in accounting, I'd like to talk about income statements. Before we delve into the details, there's a broader process I want to discuss. There are two ways you can record income: one is through what's called **accrual accounting**, and the other is through **cash accounting**.

Let's take the easier half first. In cash accounting, you record revenues as you get paid, and you record expenses as you pay them. I call it "checkbook accounting." If you have a really small business, you might be allowed to get away with this—cash in and cash out. But in most businesses, you have to use accrual accounting.

What does accrual accounting require? It requires you to record transactions as they happen. For example, let's suppose your business sells something on December 30th. You haven't been paid yet, but you've sold the item. You have to record the revenue from that sale in that year's financials, even though you haven't been paid. Similarly, you have to record expenses associated with those transactions, even if you haven't paid for them yet or you paid for them last year. In effect, accrual accounting requires you to record transactions as they happen, not as cash inflows and outflows. We'll come back to the issues this creates for us in finance, because we use accrual income statements.

Broadly speaking, accountants classify expenses into three categories: operating expenses, financing expenses, and capital expenses.
- **Operating expenses** are expenses associated with generating revenues this year, like labor and material.
- **Financing expenses** are associated with the use of any capital that's not equity, most often interest on a bank loan or debt.
- **Capital expenses** are for items that create benefits over many years. An easy example is a factory built by a manufacturing company. The factory is expected to generate revenues not just this year, but over many years. Equipment that lasts many years is also a capital expense.

In an accounting sphere, each of these expenses has its place.
- **Operating expenses** show up just below the revenue line item in an income statement. You subtract them to get to operating income.
- **Capital expenses** don't show up as an expense in the year you make them. They show up on the balance sheet as an asset, but they get written off over the lifetime of the asset in the form of depreciation or amortization, which then shows up as an operating expense.
- **Financing expenses** show up below the operating income line. After you've subtracted your operating expenses and depreciation to get to operating income, you subtract financing expenses to get to taxable income. You then pay taxes to get to net income. As you can see, expenses play out in different parts of the financial statements, and if you follow first principles, it does make sense.

Let's break down the income statement. Every income statement starts with **revenues**. Then you subtract the **cost of goods sold** (COGS), which are the expenses directly associated with producing the products or service you sell, to get the **gross profit**. Then you subtract other operating expenses—selling expenses, marketing expenses, G&A expenses—to get to **operating profit**. From operating profit, you subtract financing expenses (primarily interest expense) to get to **taxable income**. Then you pay taxes to get to **net income**. Simple, right?

Let's start at the top with revenues. In accrual accounting, you record revenues as you transact. So, you show revenues when you sell something, even if you haven't been paid. For most companies, when you sell a product or service, you record the revenues, and you're done. But what if you're a software company and you sell a product on a three-year contract, where the customer pays upfront for three years of service? If you show the entire amount as revenue this year, it's not quite fair because these are revenues for the next three years. Over the last few years, accounting has become more sophisticated about this. The new rules require you to show the portion of revenues attributable to this year and show the rest as **deferred revenues** in future years. For many companies, revenue recognition is simple, but for some, it can be tricky.

Increasingly, in the footnotes of financial statements, companies are required to tell you more about their revenues. As companies enter into multiple businesses and multiple geographies, they have to tell you where they get their revenues. Most companies will provide a geographical breakdown. This breakdown might not be as detailed or comprehensive as you'd like it to be, but you have to take what you're given. For instance, many U.S. companies might report that they get 75% of their revenues in the U.S. and 25% from the "rest of the world." The rest of the world is a really big place, but it is what it is. Companies have become better at breaking down revenues geographically. If they're in multiple businesses, companies will also break down revenues by business segment. For a company like Disney, it breaks its revenues down into theme parks, broadcasting, movies, and other businesses, reflecting its diverse operations.

Let's move on to the cost of goods sold and other operating expenses. As I said, COGS are expenses associated with producing the product or service you're selling. Other operating expenses are associated with operations, but there's no direct connection to the product or service itself. For example, for Coca-Cola, the cost of the syrup and manufacturing the bottles becomes the cost of goods sold. But selling expenses, advertising expenses—these are not cost of goods sold. They show up below the COGS line. COGS is netted out from revenues to get to gross profit. Other operating expenses are netted out from gross profit to get to operating income. You'll often see a consolidated item called **SG&A (Selling, General & Administrative)** costs. This becomes a lump-all category that companies use for operational expenses that can't be traced to individual products or services.

One of the items that will show up as part of operating expenses is the portion of your capital expenditures being written off over time in the form of **depreciation**. It's worth remembering that there are three ways to think about depreciation.
1.  **Economic depreciation**: This is the true loss in usefulness of an asset as it ages and is used. You can forget about this one, because it's not what's in financial statements.
2.  **Accounting depreciation**: This is what you get in financial statements. It's calculated based on a set of rules. For example, with **straight-line depreciation**, if you buy an asset with a life of 10 years, you write off one-tenth of that asset's value every year for 10 years. Accounting depreciation is mechanical and rule-driven.
3.  **Tax depreciation**: The objective of tax depreciation is not to capture the true loss in value or even to follow accounting rules, but to minimize taxes paid. The depreciation you see reported in the income statement might not be the same as the depreciation you see in the tax statements.

Financing expenses, of course, are most commonly the interest expense on debt, which can be interest on a bank loan or coupons on a corporate bond. Increasingly, accountants have learned that there are items that should be classified as debt that don't show up as bank loans or corporate bonds. For example, when a retail store signs a 10-year lease, that contractual commitment to make lease payments is very much like debt. Accountants have come around to this point of view. This means that once you convert those lease commitments into debt, you've got to compute the interest expense you would have had on that debt. So, interest expenses can be explicit (on bank loans) or implicit (on items converted to debt). In some companies, interest expense is netted out against the interest income the company earns on its cash and marketable securities, and it's reported as a net item. If a company has a lot of cash and very little debt, that net interest expense can become a negative number.

After accounting for interest expense, there's one more cleanup operation. If your company has non-operating assets, the income from those assets will show up below the operating income line. An easy example is a company's cash holdings. This cash isn't held as currency; it's invested in marketable securities like riskless T-bills or commercial paper, and it's earning income. That income will show up below the operating income line. If you made investments in other companies, how the income is shown depends on the magnitude of your holding.
- If you own a small portion, say 3-5%, it's a **minority holding**. You would record that percentage of the other company's net income or loss as part of your income, below the operating income line.
- If you own more than 50%, it's a **majority holding**, and accounting requires you to **consolidate**. This is a messy process. You have to act like you own the entire company, counting 100% of the subsidiary's revenues and operating income as your own. Then, you record the portion of the company that doesn't belong to you on the balance sheet.

This brings us to one final cleanup item. In many income statements, you'll see a line item for **extraordinary income or expenses**. This term implies these are one-time items that you don't expect to continue. Examples would be one-time gains or losses from selling assets, a one-time lawsuit charge, or write-off charges associated with past mistakes. These are things you believe are truly one-time. However, if you see a company claiming an item is extraordinary year after year, you've got to be a little skeptical.

Increasingly in the last few years, companies have started reporting what's called a **pro forma income statement** in addition to their standard income statement. In these, they break the accounting rules, sometimes for good reason. They might believe that accounting isn't treating an expense consistently. For example, a company like Netflix could argue that what it spends on acquiring subscribers shouldn't be treated as an operating expense because those subscribers stay for multiple years. So, some adjustments are for good reasons. Others are driven by a more trivial reason: you want to make your losses look like profits, so you keep adding things back until you get there. As investors, you shouldn't completely dismiss pro forma statements, but you should verify them. When you see expenses moved or removed, you have to pass it through the test: does that make sense?

The end game in an income statement is to report how much your company made, viewed through the eyes of accountants. Those earnings can be reported as gross income, operating income, or net income. Each has a place and sends a different message about the company. I hope you found this session useful. We'll try this out on real companies in the follow-up session. Thank you for listening.
