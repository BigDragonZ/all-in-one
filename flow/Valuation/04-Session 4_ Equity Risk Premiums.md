Let's say you have all your money invested in something guaranteed, making 2%. I come to you with a question: how much more than 2% would I need to offer you for you to take your money out of that guaranteed investment and put it in stocks? The answer to that question is the **equity risk premium**. Now, think of asking that question to every investor in the stock market. The consensus answer you would get across all investors is the equity risk premium you use in valuation.

That's going to be tough to estimate, I know, and there are two basic ways you can estimate it. One is to look backwards; it's called a **historical risk premium**. Essentially, you're looking at how much you'd have made in the past. The other is to look forward, which is a dynamic, forward-looking premium. That's not as difficult as it sounds, and hopefully, by the end of this session, you will have the tools to be able to estimate a reasonable equity risk premium.

Almost everybody who does valuation uses an equity risk premium. Every analyst, every company, every consulting firm uses it. It surprises me how little thought often goes into estimating this number. I'd like to spend this session talking about what this number is and how best to estimate it. Let's start by laying the basics. The equity risk premium is the premium you, the investor, would demand over and above the risk-free rate for investing in risky assets—equities as a class.

Think about it: if you're making 2% or 3% risk-free, the question I'm asking is how much more than that would I need to offer you to buy stocks collectively? Think about a broad index. As you start thinking about that, two things are going to drive the number you come up with. One is how risk-averse you are as an individual. What's going to drive that? Some of it you're born with, some of it is a function of your age, but if you're risk-averse, you're probably going to demand a larger premium. Second, it's also going to depend on what you think about the riskiness of equities as a class. What's going to drive that? That's going to be driven by a perception of macroeconomic risk. If you've come off a crisis or you see a lot of uncertainty or volatility in the overall economy, you're going to demand a larger premium. If you're worried about catastrophic risk—the risk that something terrible could happen—you're going to demand a larger premium. Bluntly, the equity risk premium is a number that can and should change over time. As we look at the different approaches, keep that in mind. Any approach that yields the same number year after year is probably not a dependable number.

The way most people get equity risk premiums is to look backwards. I don't blame them, because that's where the data is. They look at the past and ask a very simple question: what would I have made investing in stocks over the last 10, 20, 50, or 80 years, as opposed to investing in something riskless like T-bills or T-bonds? The difference is a historical risk premium. For example, if over the last 50 years you'd have made 12% investing in stocks each year, but you'd have made only 4% investing in Treasury bonds over that same period, 12% minus 4% would have yielded an 8% historical risk premium. That should be pretty simple to do, right? Especially in a market like the U.S., we have a long history. Take a look at the table at the bottom of this page. You'll see three slices of history I've taken: one goes back to 1928 (85 years of history), one goes back 50 years, and one goes back 10 years. I get very different estimates of the risk premium depending on how far back in time I go. I get a very different premium with 85 years as opposed to 10. It also depends on what I use as my risk-free investment—if I use T-bills (a short-term government security) as opposed to T-bonds (which are long-term), and whether I compute arithmetic averages or geometric averages. You're saying, 'What the heck is that?' It sounds like inside statistics, but here's the difference: for arithmetic averages, I add up 85 numbers and divide by 85; it's a simple average. Geometric averages take into account the compounding that happens over time. Depending on the decisions you make on estimation, your equity risk premium could be as high as 7.7% or as low as 1.5%. All these numbers can't be right at the same time.
