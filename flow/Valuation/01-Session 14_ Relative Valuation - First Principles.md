## 精修内容

Let's say you decide to buy a house. You hire a realtor, the realtor shows you the house, and then he or she gives you a number: $500,000. How do you think she came up with that number? She didn't do an **intrinsic valuation** of that house. She came up with that number by looking at other houses in the neighborhood that had sold in the recent past. That is the essence of **relative valuation**. In relative valuation, you value an asset based on how similar assets are priced. In contrast, in **intrinsic valuation**, you value an asset based on its cash flows, growth, and risk. The two are obviously interconnected, but they're not always going to give you the same answers.

Most analysts who do valuation do relative valuation, and many of them do it badly. In this session, I hope to take you through a four-step process to make sure if you use relative valuation, you're dotting your i's and crossing your t's. We've talked a lot about intrinsic valuation, and I don't blame you for thinking that intrinsic valuation is the only way to value a business. I like intrinsic valuation, but in this session, I want to introduce you to the technique that most people use to value businesses, which is relative valuation.

What is relative valuation? Here are the basics. In relative valuation, you value an asset based not on its cash flows, growth, and risk, but based on what similar assets are priced at. If you've used StubHub to buy a ticket or eBay to buy something, you're doing relative valuation. You don't try to value the asset; you look to see what other people are paying for something to decide what it's worth. In the context of valuing assets, here are the three pieces you will see in a relative valuation. First, you'll see a **standardized price**. For example, when buying a house, a five-bedroom house will cost you a lot more than a two-bedroom house. So rather than compare the prices of houses, we look at price per square foot. Second, you have a set of assets that you call **comparable**. You're saying these are similar assets that have traded, and these are the prices they traded at. I would put quotes around the word "similar" because there are probably differences, even if you're very careful about your choices. The third step is, if you're careful, you control for those differences. So there's a standardized price, there are comparable assets, and there are some ways of controlling for differences across assets.

If you've ever read an equity research report, you know what I've just described. The standardized price usually takes the form of a **multiple**. The similar assets are usually called **comparable firms**, and the controls are usually storytelling. But that's relative valuation. Before I delve into relative valuation, I should also tell you that it is pervasive; it's all around us. To show you how pervasive it is, I'll give you a couple of statistics. About a decade ago, I collected about 550 equity research reports from around the globe—different investment banks, different equity research houses. I must confess I didn't read most of them. I collected them because I wanted to categorize how equity research analysts approach valuation. Out of the 550 equity research reports, about 45 were **discounted cash flow (DCF) valuations**—less than 10%. About 450 were relative valuations using multiples and comparables. The remaining 55 were kind of fuzzy; I couldn't quite categorize them. But among the ones I could categorize, relative valuations outnumbered discounted cash flow valuations 10-to-1.

I took a look at about 100 acquisition valuations, hoping or expecting things to be different in corporate finance. Out of the 100 acquisition valuations, I got a much more even split: about half were discounted cash flow valuations, and half were relative valuations. But then I took a closer look at the half that were DCF valuations and noticed that in most of them, the biggest number—the **terminal value**—was estimated using a multiple. So even those were really relative valuations. Almost every **rule of thumb** you see out there is based on a multiple. What am I talking about? "Price-to-Book less than one is cheap." "A Price-to-Earnings less than 10 is cheap." They're all based on multiples. What I'm trying to say is the language of valuation around us is a language of relative valuation.

When I first ran the statistic, I was a little puzzled by my finding. Here's why: when I teach my valuation class at NYU, I always end every class with a question. This is after everybody's valued their companies using multiple approaches: discounted cash flow valuation, relative valuation, whatever other approaches they might use. I ask them at the end of the class, after they've tried these approaches, if they had to pick one, which one they would pick as their preferred valuation approach. In the 25 years I've taught the class, I get pretty much the same answer: about 70% pick discounted cash flow valuation or intrinsic valuation (reflecting, I guess, my biases), about 20% pick relative valuation, and about 10% I'm not quite sure. So, 7-to-2, when people leave my room, they say they're going to use discounted cash flow valuation. I haven't done a follow-up question, but if I did a follow-up question five years later and asked these people on their jobs, "What approach do you use to value companies?" I would wager the numbers get switched, that 70% use relative valuation and maybe 20% still use intrinsic valuation.

So I started thinking about what it is about relative valuation that makes it so attractive to analysts, and I've come up with three possible answers. The first one came to me while I was watching a *Seinfeld* episode, one of those episodes where one of Jerry's many girlfriends accuses him of being crazy. She says, "Jerry, you're crazy!" He says, "If you think I'm crazy, see the guy who lives across the hall from me." And if you've ever seen a *Seinfeld* episode, you know who lives across the hall from Jerry: it's Kramer. Relative to Kramer, who's crazy? We forget how much valuation is selling. If you're trying to sell me something, it's far easier to sell it with a relative valuation than an intrinsic valuation. All you need to do is find something even more overvalued than what you're selling me and say, "Look, it's cheap! It's cheap relative to these five other things I found." Relative valuations are far easier to sell than intrinsic valuations because as long as you let me pick the multiple and you let me pick the comparables, I can tell you whatever story I want.

Second, it's far easier to defend a relative valuation than an intrinsic valuation. In intrinsic valuation, your assumptions are all explicit. Everybody can see what you're assuming about cash flows, growth, and risk, and that's dangerous because they can pick it apart. In relative valuation, all your assumptions are implicit. So when you use 12 times **EBITDA**, you are making assumptions, but nobody can see what those assumptions are. And if they cannot see them, how can they pick them apart?

Third, and this is something that we don't factor in enough when we think about valuation, I personally think in the long term, you're far more likely to be right with intrinsic valuation than relative valuation. But here's the catch: when you're wrong with intrinsic valuation, you're also far more likely to be wrong alone. So what? If you're wrong alone, you tend to be fired. If you use relative valuation and you're wrong, you're always going to have lots of company, and that's a safe place to be. The survival instinct kicks in, and that's why I think more people use multiples than intrinsic valuation.

Even if you're a true believer in intrinsic valuation, here's my advice to you: understand relative valuation. Keep it in your arsenal. It doesn't have to be an either/or. I am a true believer in intrinsic valuation, but I still do relative valuation. I'll often frame my intrinsic valuation in terms of relative value. So don't give up on relative valuation.

Let's talk a little bit about how we can bring relative valuation into our toolkits. Before I talk about the four basic propositions or ways you can prevent yourself from making mistakes, let me first go back and talk a little bit about multiples. As I said, to make comparisons across companies, you have to use a multiple. Every multiple has a numerator and a denominator. In your numerator, you almost always need a measure of market value. That market value can be just of your equity (that's **market cap**, as is the case with **price-earnings ratios**). It can be the market value of equity plus the value of debt (which is the market value of the firm). It can be market value of equity plus debt minus cash (that's called **Enterprise Value**). So in almost every multiple, you're going to see one of those three numbers: market cap, value of the firm, or Enterprise Value. The difference between Enterprise Value and firm value is that Enterprise Value is net of cash, so it's firm value minus cash.

In the denominator, you can see one of four measures. You can see **revenues**, because you're wary about accountants and what they can do to the bottom-line numbers. You might see **earnings**—earnings per share, operating income. You might see even measures of **cash flow**—cash earnings, **EBITDA**. You might see **book value**—book value of equity or book value of the entire firm. Or you might see some **sector-specific measure**. Remember the late 1990s when companies were compared based on value per website visitor? You can compare steel companies by dividing the market value by the number of tons of steel they produce. Basically, it could be some measure of operations: market value per employee, market value per unit of output. But in all of these measures, what you're trying to do is scale market value to some common variable.

That's the first step in relative valuation: to get a sense of what those multiples are. I'm going to take you through a four-step process that, if you're religious about following, you're going to be okay with relative valuation. My problem with relative valuation is not that people use it, but that they're shoddy about the way they use it. Here are the four steps I'm going to go through:
1.  **Define** the multiple.
2.  **Describe** the multiple.
3.  **Analyze** the multiple.
4.  **Apply** the multiple.

In too many cases, you see analysts trying to apply a multiple without going through the first three steps, and that's what gets you into trouble.

Let's start with **defining the multiple**. When I look at a multiple, there are two basic questions I ask. The first is: is this multiple consistently defined? Let me explain. Every multiple has a numerator and a denominator. If your numerator is an equity value, your denominator has to be an equity value as well. If your numerator is a firm or an enterprise value, your denominator has to be a firm or an enterprise value as well. Let me back up. Take **P/E ratios**. In the numerator, it's the market value of equity, and in the denominator, it's earnings per share, which is equity earnings divided by the number of shares. Okay, thank God for small blessings, the most widely used multiple in the world is consistent. Or take **EV/EBITDA**. In the numerator, you have market value of equity plus debt minus cash, a rough measure of the market value of the operating assets in the company. In the denominator, you have earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (**EBITDA**), a rough measure of operating cash flows. EV/EBITDA is okay.

Here's what's not consistent: a **Price/EBITDA** ratio. Here's why: your price is a market value of equity, but EBITDA is an operating cash flow. You might say, "What's wrong if I use it and I'm consistent across all 15-20 companies in my sector?" It's still not going to work, and here's why. If you have companies that borrow a lot of money—let's say they borrow money to buy back stock—the market value of equity will drop, but the EBITDA will not be affected by the borrowing. Those companies are going to look cheap to you. If you mismatch your numerator and your denominator—use equity value in the numerator and you use a pre-debt value in the denominator—you're going to find that companies that borrow a lot of money look cheap to you for all the wrong reasons. So check your multiple to make sure it's internally consistent. Second, make sure it's estimated exactly the same way for all companies in your sector. That's actually a tougher test to meet than it sounds. Using price-earnings ratios, is earnings per share estimated exactly the same way for all 15 companies? That doesn't just require that you have the same accounting standards; you need all companies to have the same amount of fidelity to those accounting standards. If they don't, aggressive companies are going to look cheap to you.

Second step in the process: **describe the multiple**. This is basic statistics. When you have a lot of data, put it up in a distribution or a histogram. What you're going to get a sense of is what's high, what's low, and what's typical. One of the things you're going to see is that none of these multiples are symmetric. Why? Because a multiple can never be lower than zero. A price-earnings ratio cannot be less than zero, but it can be 15,000, 20,000, 25,000. So what? When you have a distribution where the lowest value is zero and the highest number could be anything, your averages are going to get pulled out by those outliers. I remember what my stats professor said in my first statistics class: if you have an asymmetric distribution, a distribution where the outliers are all on one side, trust the median, not the average. We have the data to look at, and there is really no basis for rules of thumb if you don't have the data to back them up.

Third, **analyze the distribution**. Embedded in every multiple are all of the assumptions you make in discounted cash flow valuation: assumptions about cash flows, growth, and risk. There are two questions you're trying to answer here. First, when I use a multiple, what are the variables that should determine that multiple? Second, how do changes in those variables affect my multiple? For instance, if the variables that matter are growth and risk, how does a change in growth affect this multiple? You might think that's going to be really difficult to do, but not really. Here's a very simple rubric you can use. If you have an equity multiple (like P/E ratio, Price-to-Book), go back to a very simple equity valuation model, like a stable growth dividend discount model. The value of a stock is the expected dividend next year divided by the cost of equity minus the growth rate. Divide both sides of that equation by a constant, for instance, by earnings per share. You end up with an equation for the price-earnings ratio that will tell you what variables drive it. If you have an enterprise value multiple, go back to a very simple enterprise valuation model: Enterprise Value is the free cash flow to the firm next year divided by the cost of capital minus the growth rate. Do the algebra. This will allow us to come up with the variables that drive each multiple.

If you go through these three steps, then you're ready to **apply**. When you get ready to apply, there are two basic questions you have to answer. First, what am I going to call "comparable" here? What are those comparable firms? The answer might seem simple. You might say, "I'm a software company, so it's other software companies." But that's not necessarily the truth. A comparable firm from a valuation perspective is a company with similar cash flows, similar growth, and similar risk. And guess what? If you're Microsoft, none of those companies might be software companies. So think in fundamental terms. Second, think of ways in which you can control for differences on those fundamentals. Storytelling is not going to be enough. We're going to talk about how to control for differences in ways that go beyond storytelling.

Let's review. Most people use relative valuation, and most people use it shoddily. If you're going to use multiples and comparables—and I think it's a good idea to know how to do it, even if you're a believer in intrinsic valuation—remember the four-step process:
1.  **Define** the multiple. Make sure it's consistently defined and uniformly estimated.
2.  **Describe** the multiple. Think of its statistical properties, look at what's high, what's low, what's the median.
3.  **Analyze** the multiple. See what variables drive this multiple.
4.  And only then should you **apply** that multiple.
